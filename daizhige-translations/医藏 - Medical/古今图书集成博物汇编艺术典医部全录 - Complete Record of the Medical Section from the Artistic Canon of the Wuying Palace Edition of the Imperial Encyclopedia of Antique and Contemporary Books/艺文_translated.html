    Preface to the Artistic Literature Section of the Book of Han [Han, Ban Gu]

    The medical canon originates from the understanding of human blood, vessels, marrow, yin and yang, to identify the root of illnesses and the distinction between life and death. It involves the proper application of acupuncture, stones, herbs, and heat, harmonizing various medications according to their appropriateness. The acquisition of benefits is like that of using a kind stone to extract iron, utilizing materials accordingly. Those who are unskilled may misunderstand the principles, mistaking recovery for severity, and life for death. The classical formulas are based on the temperature of herbs and stones, assessing the depth of illnesses, using the taste of medicine, tailored to the response of the body, distinguishing between the five bitter and six pungent flavors, balancing water and fire to unblock and resolve issues, restoring harmony. If one loses their appropriateness, they might exacerbate heat with heat or cold with cold, injuring their essence internally, which is a unique loss. Hence, it is said: “There are illnesses that go untreated but often result in the use of traditional Chinese medicine.”[1p]
    Those skilled in the art of healing are essential tools for life, serving as a safeguard of royal authority. In ancient times, there were Qi Bo and Yu Fu; in the middle ages, there were Bian Que and Qin He. They addressed illness and governance, using diagnosis to understand administration. During the rise of the Han dynasty, there was Cang Gong, yet today, his techniques are obscure. Therefore, I discuss his texts to introduce the art of healing.

    Encouragement for Physicians: In the realm between heaven and earth, humanity is the most intelligent. What people value most is life. Although lifespans vary, whether short or long, the whims of temperature and appetite lead to different ailments, causing untimely death; thus, neglecting one’s nature can destroy health. The key to rescuing from these afflictions truly lies in effective prescriptions. Therefore, it is crucial for the esteemed physician to be well-versed in the classics, as the great masters are honored as the kings of medicine. Just as the enlightened one can alleviate suffering, they can only treat the disruptions of the four elements. There are also poets who praise the beauty of Pengcheng, the gentleness of the Qin state, the encounter of Ji Liang with the Lu family, and the meeting of Guo Zi with the people of Yue.

    Finally, with the long-lasting pursuit of immortality and the wondrous path, one can still transform the six into one using golden elixirs and refine the three into seven using silver pills. They gather secrets in jade boxes and explore the profound wisdom of ancient texts. What is peach gum? The Northern Dipper cannot hide its form.[2p]
    The golden paste is not far; the bright pearl is still ashamed of its price. In the Zhou Li, the physician is in charge of the ailments of the people; for every citizen who is ill, they are treated in accordance with their afflictions.     On Medicine by Li Hua (Tang Dynasty)

Before he set off, he cautioned, "With neighboring countries in conflict, how can a physician proceed with his task?" He replied, "I do not dispense medicine or herbs; I will use words to cure the Duke of Jin so that the nation suffers no harm." The Duke of Qin was pleased and honored him with a grand send-off.

    Upon arriving in Jin, the physician found the Duke in a luxurious setting, surrounded by more than a hundred attendants adorned in jewels, while chefs prepared feasts in the courtyard before inviting guests. The physician declined, saying, "I was commissioned by my lord to remedy your illness. Now, the great country has turned to appearances and meals to treat me; this is beyond my capacity." The military officials replied, "This is how our lord honors you, not an oversight, please respect our customs." The physician said, "I have journeyed a thousand miles at great physical strain and lack energy. All I desire is one cup of wine, a dish of fruit, and some light side dishes to accompany my meal; I don't wish to burden your great country with more."

    Having respectfully accepted their offerings, he examined the Duke and said, "Your voice is hoarse and waning, your energy is turbulent and superficial, your complexion is cold yet your build is robust. You are akin to the King of Chu; this can either be remedied or not."[6p]

The Duke of Jin said: "I have never ventured into Chu and am unfamiliar with its king. I wish to hear about that country." Like floating water, half is within; the waves of the vast water sway, rising and falling with the wind. Flowing clouds shift in weight, at times vanishing, at times brightening; the roads stretch and wind, surrounding mountains and rivers. What appeared high is now beneath the cart; the dark valley is shrouded, stones thrown make no sound. Describing its inner pastures, the mountains darkly frame the doors, with a hazy and jagged view, as if strong blades and curtains were to come apart and come together again. The decayed mountains are sparse and vast, while the thick source roars in the nose. Branching streams ripple, converging in deep currents; day and night there is sound, cool against the heat. Through cliffs, rushing torrents rush forth and leap away; initially thought reachable, suddenly seeming boundless. Whirling and sliding, swirling in eddies; stones rolling against fortifications, fire igniting on the riverbanks. The young nobles of the city petitioned to open the gates for battle, while the elder ones advised that the ruler must prioritize the well-being of the people. Chu relies on its wealth and strength, and indulges in desires that are not sufficiently frightening. The guest indeed healed Jin with his words, hence it is said he spoke as a physician."[11p]
    The "Zhou Guan" states that physicians are responsible for treating the illnesses of the people. Generally, any affliction one suffers can be cured, but if left untreated for too long, it may lead to death. One has yet to observe an illness that cannot be cured. In the regions of Ba and Chu, there is a custom of consulting spirits, which has existed since ancient times. When the five elements are in disarray, causing the suffering of severe plagues, it is often said that these are illnesses brought by the seasons and not something medicine can address. Therefore, they pray to the spirits without rest, offering chicken, pig, duck, and sheep in hopes of receiving bountiful blessings. If they cannot recover, they blame themselves for the spirits not coming to aid; if they are fortunate enough to heal, they attribute it to their diligent prayers and numerous offerings. Otherwise, how could they contend with the elements?

    Moreover, if treatment is delayed, the poison of the illness gradually affects the entire household. Thus, even close friends and relatives dare not approach each other's homes, fearing the spread of infection; children fear their parents, and wives avoid their husbands. Wealthy individuals may still manage to have a shaman protect them, while the poor are left to suffer alone in silence. In such circumstances, not only is the charity of medicine cut off, but even food supplies are also scarce. As a result, those who die are often around eighteen or nineteen, yet the people remain oblivious to the causes.

    I once inquired about this with others, and it turned out the real issue was the dominance of shamans over physicians. Alas! Can shamans truly surpass all physicians? What they conquer are merely the easily misled common folk. For illnesses are rooted within the body, while spirits lurk outside. Good medicine is meant to treat internal afflictions.[12p]
    Now, if one does not address the ailments within but solely seeks fortune from without, when the situation becomes dire, it is inappropriate to neglect the need for care and inquiry; would this not be a mistake? If crops in flourishing fields are damaged by pests, one must completely eliminate the pests for the crops to thrive. If a household has valuable possessions but thieves invade, the owner must wield weapons to either kill or capture the intruders to protect their wealth. The same is true for the human body: when confronted with the influences of yin and yang and facing illness, one should have a physician examine the sounds, look at the color, check the pulse, and prescribe medicine to address the internal organs, allowing the body to return to equilibrium. If one avoids seeking medical treatment and expects to reap the rewards without eliminating the pests, or seeks to preserve their wealth without driving away thieves, it is utterly unattainable. How much more so for the state, which values medical texts as the utmost priority.[13p]
    During the Yuan Dynasty, the renowned editor Ming Fang issued guidelines for the imperial regions, detailing the conditions of epidemics in meticulous fashion. Later, during the Qingli period, the virtuous Minister Fan Wenzheng suggested that physicians from the capital and other regions gather for lectures and training to improve their skills, thereby eliminating errors and saving lives, which would truly assist in effective governance. However, the common people were unaware of the court's benevolent intentions and instead fell prey to false beliefs, endangering their lives, which was genuinely lamentable. Now, the doctors and scholars are instructed to study the methods of ancient figures like Shen Nong and Bian Que, and come together in the teachings of Qi Bo and Yu Shi to correct wrong practices and eliminate falsehoods, ensuring that treatable diseases do not harm people, thus contributing to the welfare of the populace. Yet, there is concern for the customs of the Ba Cong people, who remain attached to old ways and do not appreciate the miraculous effects of medicine, excessively praying to misguided deities; hence, inscriptions are made to convey this.[14p]

I heard of Master Zhu from Danxi, whose name was renowned everywhere, and urgently summoned him for treatment. When the master arrived, after taking my pulse, he said: "Internally, your essence is shaken; externally, your form is strained, depleting your yin and consuming your vitality. You should withdraw your gaze and listen within the vast court of the Void; relying solely on medicine is not sufficient." He then instructed his senior disciple, Jia Junsicheng, to stay and take care of me. Jia treated me as if I were his own sibling, attending to my needs in every way. If he suspected my anger was excessive, he would treat me with sadness; if he thought I was too sad, he would treat me with joy; if my joy was excessive, he would address it with fear; if my fear was excessive, he would evoke contemplation; if my contemplation was excessive, he would counter it with anger.[25p]
    To the left and to the right, supporting and assisting, and then gently adjusting them. Furthermore, when there is a reversal, one applies medicine to the Yongquan point to awaken them; when they are in a state of stupor, one presses the Xin Yu point to stabilize them. Such treatment continues for several years; it cannot be abandoned overnight. Better to eat simple food and wear plain clothes than to go a day without Ji Jun. Better for scholars to be not reckless and for guests to be not noble than to go a day without Ji Jun. I then recovered from my illness, and Ji Jun has been greatly beneficial to me. I hope you can share a word to highlight Ji Jun's goodness and make it clear that I do not forget his kindness, though I do not know if it is appropriate. I have read Zhang Jun's letter and feel greatly moved by it. Those who govern the people in this world are often well-fed and indulge in pleasure.[26p]
    Their view of our people is indifferent, as if the richness of Qin and Yue are not connected to each other; and not only are they not connected, but they also extract the marrow and cut the fat without knowing when to stop. Who is as diligent in caring for the people's ailments as Zhang Jun? The doctors of the world, with their many engagements, are too rushed to be gracious, often leaving without belief even after a single night; some have no time to check pulses or prescribe remedies, and cannot be retained. Who among them is like Jia Jun, who cares and nurtures for years without growing weary? All these can be recorded. I am now taking up my pen to follow in the footsteps of writers, and if I do not write about this, where can I find material to write about! Nevertheless, the officials today are cruel and oppressive, relentlessly beating and striking; my people have suffered this for far too long! As I look around, where are the wise leaders? Between states and towns, is there anyone with the skill to apply the right measures to revive the people? If there are such, though I am slow-witted, I could still prepare ink and brush, writing extensively and yet not pen a single word about it. This serves as a preface.[27p]
    In ancient times, physicians had to be well-versed in the texts of the three generations. The so-called three generations are: first, the "Acupuncture and Moxibustion," second, the "Shennong's Materia Medica," and third, the "Great Fundamental Pulse Treatise." The pulse treatise is for observing evidence, the materia medica is for distinguishing drugs, and acupuncture and moxibustion are for eliminating diseases. Without these three, one cannot be called a physician. Therefore, it is said in the records of rituals: "A physician who does not understand the three generations will not be trusted with their medicine." The transmitters of the classics clearly state their doctrines and refute the fallacies, using the concept of father and son passing down through three generations as a reference. How perplexing that is! The art of medicine requires deep contemplation, keen insight, and clarity of thought before one can engage with it. Even a father may not necessarily pass down his knowledge to his son.

    In my hometown, there is a physician named Yan, who has been in the medical profession for three generations. His medical practice focuses solely on major treatments, ignoring all else entirely. There is also Mr. Zhu Ping, whose family has historically practiced Confucianism, but he first gained recognition as a physician, diligently studying medical texts. A young man was afflicted with lung issues, coughing and producing blood-streaked phlegm. Yan said, "This is a consumption; he will die in three months." Mr. Zhu disagreed, stating, "No, the qi is rising and becoming abundant, but the central axis is lost; the fire organ is in turmoil, burning like gold in a furnace. This is called lung atrophy; treatment must be administered." Afterward, the young man indeed recovered.

    An old man of sixty suffered from fevers and chills, initially experiencing a slight shiver, his teeth chattering, followed by severe heat, as intense as being steamed in a pot. Yan said, "This is phlegm; if untreated, it will worsen." Mr. Zhu contradicted him, claiming, "No, the pulse is excess and knotted, the rhythm is chaotic, with an exterior that seems strong but is internally weak; calamity emerges, yet fortune is found at the extreme. This is called the resolution."[28p]
    Medicine can cure, while without it, the condition worsens. Eventually, when it becomes severe, treatment can lead to recovery. A woman suffered from a cough and would expel several liters of phlegm, which smelled foul and affected anyone nearby. Yan stated that this was a cold-induced cough and recommended warmth. Pingjun disagreed. He noted that the balance of yin and yang was not achieved, resulting in stagnation of qi and blood, creating a situation with no resolution, which he termed a severe obstruction. If treated improperly, after a long time, the condition may stabilize but could still lead to the birth of a son. His statement proved true later on. Yan had been a physician for three generations, while Pingjun was just beginning, raising questions about the merit of their practices.

    Alas! There were many renowned physicians in the past, too numerous to mention. Among them were Hua Yuanhua, Zhang Sibao, and Xu Zhizang, whose treatments and results were remarkable, and one never heard of their familial lineage passing down. Yet, for three thousand years, the interpretations of medical texts have clouded the principles of the "Book of Rites," obscuring clarity. Those seeking medical help do not inquire about the profundity of the texts but follow practitioners they see persevering in their work, regardless of their expertise. When questioned, they say this is what the keepers of rituals suggest. Is this acceptable?

    Ge Sheng, from a prominent family in Huai, was skilled in medicine and had studied the writings of three generations, practicing and mastering them. He treated ailments effectively, determining life or death with results as accurate as if he were consulting an unerring oracle. Scholars who might be unable to adequately provide medicine would nonetheless dispense it without seeking compensation, and the virtuous gentlemen would universally commend him. He was awarded the seventh rank robe and appointed to oversee the medical officers, with people relying on him for their lives. Oh! Is Ge Sheng not one who bears no shame before the ancient physicians?[29p]
    Preface to the Gift for Physician Jia

    The way of medicine has been difficult to articulate for a long time. However, one must carefully diagnose to establish measurement, set standards, weigh and balance, match colors and pulses, and observe the excesses and deficiencies, as well as the harmonious and contrary states. This must be coupled with the patient's movements and their breathing, after which one can treat them; thus, the matter is indeed of great seriousness. If one does not have a deep understanding of the gains and losses in the multitude of written works, can one still engage in this?

    The "Huangdi Neijing," although suspected to have been emulated and attributed to figures of pre-Qin times, contains profound words and deep meanings, and its examinations are credible and well-evidenced, making it a foundational text for physicians. Below this, we have texts from the people of Qin and Yue, which are gentle, yet there are no books to pass down. The work "Bajiyi Nanjing" by the Yue people primarily expounds upon the essentials of the "Neijing." Further down, there are the teachings of Chunyu Yi and Hua Tuo, which are also part of the art of guiding. The practice of opening the abdomen to treat illnesses involves mystical elements. Sima Qian thoroughly documented the state of medical conditions, yet the so-called "strange wind" and "mysterious wind" are completely unknown to contemporary people, let alone searching for the profound meanings of treatment. Below this, we find Zhang Ji, whose "Jinkui Yuhan Jing" and discussions of typhoid diseases are indeed timeless classics. However, it primarily details injuries from the six qi and the resulting emotions, diet, and exertion, without thoroughly discussing these points, compounded by the difficulties in sequentially following the disordered and simplified text. Below him is Wang Shuhe.[30p]
    Uncle He compiled the "Pulse Classic" with Qi Bo, Hua Tuo, and others, discussing the principles of yin and yang, distinguishing the three divisions and nine conditions, categorizing the human greeting and vital energy, and detailing the twelve meridians along with the ailments of the three jiaos, five organs, and six bowels, making it exceedingly clear. Unfortunately, it was trivialized by some superficial individuals into a simplistic pulse song, preventing the original work from being widely disseminated. Following him was Chao Yuanfang, whose "On the Source of Illnesses" appears not to have been unwitnessed, as it discusses wind, cold, and two types of dampness, but neglects the topic of damp-heat, which is a flaw. Next was Wang Bing, who advanced the understanding of the changes in the five movements and six atmospheres, compiling the "Heavenly Origin Jade Strategy," which is comprehensive and meticulous, yet difficult for people; should one cling too rigidly to it, they would become stagnant and disconnected. Following them were Wang Tao and Sun Simiao.[31p]
    Si Miao possessed exceptional knowledge and a compassionate heart. His works, such as the "Thousand Gold Prescriptions" and "Wings," include details on causes of harm to humans, which are expressed with deep fervor. Although later generations have ventured into his teachings, they may still leave gaps in their understanding of pathogenic fevers, leading to regrets.

    Though Tao was lacking in skill, the "Secret Essentials of the Outer Archives" contained detailed discussions about the regulations of cautery and acupuncture, reflecting some ancestral teachings. However, the view that acupuncture can save the living but cannot revive the dead is somewhat biased.

    Following him were Qian Yi, Pang Anshi, and Xu Shuwei. Xu Shuwei operated within set limits without making significant discoveries, while Anshi, although capable of remarkable adaptations, ultimately did not stray from established parameters; both men grasped the basics. Only Yi deeply explored the profound principles of acupuncture, extracting its essence and creating methods tailored for the five organs according to their specific needs.[32p]
    If the liver has excessive fire, it leads to leakage without nourishment; the kidneys, being true water, provide nourishment without leakage. This reveals the secrets of the "Inner Canon," an understanding particularly important for those who know how to apply it. The world generally perceives it through the lens of infantile medicine, showing how shallow their understanding is. The writings that have been lost and collected by Yan Xiaozhong are largely influenced by his perspective and are not the original truth of the teachings. Below him are Zhang Yuanshu of Shanggu, Liu Wansheng of Hejian, and Zhang Congzheng of Suishui. Although Yuanshu and Wansheng set up unusual dreams and extraordinary figures to enhance their teachings, they were indeed inspired by the principles of the original teachings. Zhang Congzheng, on the other hand, is primarily a follower of Wansheng.

    Yuanshu’s methods, which combined ancient formulas with new ailments, could not align with the past; he did not treat diseases according to formulas, which is why his writings did not circulate. What remains today has largely been shaped by later interpretations. The understanding of these principles has been deeply grasped by Dong Yuan and Li Gao. Gao clarified the internal and external injuries, placing significant emphasis on the importance of nourishing the spleen. He viewed the spleen as the central organ of the body, asserting that when it is balanced, all organs are balanced as well. Zhang Congzheng advocated for three methods involving vomiting, sweating, and purging for treatment, considering six critical factors of wind, cold, heat, damp, fire, and dryness—key aspects of medical practice. His treatments were often aggressive, and the remedies harsh; those unskilled in these methods risked causing death. Wansheng discussed diseases caused by wind and fire using the nineteen conditions from the "Inner Canon," which served as the basis for "Original Disease Patterns." This was concise and profound, difficult for the majority of practitioners to emulate. Upon examining how these methods were established, they mostly revolved around the dual principles of replenishment and aggression.[33p]
    Alas! Since the existence of the "Inner Canon," there have been a total of one hundred seventy-nine physicians' texts archived by the authorities, amounting to two hundred ninety works and two thousand two hundred fifty-nine volumes; this is indeed not a small number. He who has not yet had time for an in-depth discussion, I will address what is currently being discussed: Do the physicians of this world truly apply themselves fully to this? If they have not done so, how can they measure, establish standards, weigh, and assess pulse and color? It is nearly unimaginable that they would treat human life so lightly. Nevertheless, there is something essential here regarding the concepts of opposition and compliance. That which is called rising and falling, floating and sinking, I will follow; that which is called warming and cooling, cold and heat, I will oppose. If one can truly adhere to this principle, then even if one does not treat in the manner of the ancients, they will not stray far from the truth.[34p]
    Yet it is not easy to achieve in one fell swoop. If one does not seek to understand the vastness and interconnections, how can one suddenly return to the realm of simplicity? The difficulty in speaking of medicine lies precisely here. Wu Shang? Jia Sicong, the disciple of Lian, is of inherently gentle and upright nature, possessing the conduct of a gentleman. He once studied with Master Lian under the guidance of Mr. Wen from the southern city, learning to interpret the classics. After some time, Sicong left to pursue medical teachings at the door of Mr. Yanxiu Zhu. He explored all that was written by the Confucian scholars, and upon venturing out to treat ailments, often achieved remarkable results. Among the gentry, he frequently composed poetry and asked Lian for prefaces. If Lian was not knowledgeable about medicine, how could he be informed of Sicong’s achievements? Yet Sicong, tirelessly seeking assistance, straightforwardly discussed the successes and failures of ancient texts, encouraging Sicong to study in this manner. Initially, there was no time, like on other days when writers skillfully wield their pens and tongues to create texts.[35p]
    I have observed that in recent times, no place has praised physicians more than Wu region. Among the physicians of Wu, none is more renowned than the Ge family. The medical practice of the Ge family excels in discerning the rise and fall of the five movements and six qi, using this to analyze illness and determine treatment methods. This is the art of Zhongzheng, which sets him apart from others.

    Indeed, one of Zhongzheng's uncles, known as Hengzhai, once authored a book, stating that medicine should consider the fluctuations of time to determine what is to be added or diminished. Liu Shouzheng, Zhang Zihe, and others practiced their art during a time of strength for the Jurchens, when the people were robust and fierce, and thus often employed methods of release. However, as times became weak, with the aftermath of war and famine causing hardship, Zhang Jiegu, Li Ming and their peers tended to focus more on restoration and nourishment.

    By the time of the Song dynasty, physicians generally aimed to preserve vital qi but did not comprehend the necessary offense. They could nurture diseases but not cure them, failing to understand the transformations involved. Such discussions illustrate an understanding of the changes in qi movements and the implications of yin and yang transformations.

    As for modern physicians, they are filled with benevolence and righteousness. They have extensively studied various texts and are proficient in the Taoist arts. They are well-versed in the principles of yin and yang, and have a clear understanding of qi dynamics. They differentiate medicines by their warming or cooling effects and assess the pulse for superficial or interior conditions. Their treatment employs supplements or purges based on a careful examination of the illness's nature—whether it is deficiency or excess. They tailor their prescriptions according to the illness and administer remedies accordingly. Their clever techniques are instinctive, adapting fluidly without rigidity. They do not seek to enhance their reputations but aim merely to benefit others. They do not calculate personal gain or profit, and regardless of wealth or poverty, they apply the same remedies. Their ability to bring the dead back to life is as profound as the heavens and earth. Such enlightened physicians will have their names celebrated for generations to come.[36p]
    Admonitions for Inferior Physicians

    Today's inferior physicians flaunt their oddities, lacking knowledge of classical texts and understanding of characters. They self-promote to deceive the world, rushing to people's doors without invitation. They present banal remedies and ask for ailments superficially. They boast of their abilities, indulging in flattery. Patients do not examine their issues, treating their ailments vaguely. They do not investigate the origins of their illnesses or distinguish between true and false conditions. They disregard life and death, acting recklessly in the moment. Suddenly, illnesses worsen and they hastily disperse. They endanger lives for minimal profit. Such inferior physicians are shameful and to be feared.

    Admonitions for Patients

    Today's patients are often stingy with their expenditures. They refuse urgent treatment, waiting for self-recovery. They do not seek the wise but hope for the easy solution. They overlook their conditions, carelessly trying various remedies. Some even invoke spirits, which leads to nothing worthwhile. Stepping on frost without caution invites the hard ice. By the time they seek skilled physicians, their illnesses have become severe. Even if there are miraculous elixirs, they are hard to cure. Their ignorance and delay result from their own actions. They only resign to fate, thinking luck will improve. Such confusion is truly lamentable. These patients must rectify this folly.

    Warnings for Physicians

    The weight of human life is immense, and the role of a physician is the most challenging. The source of illness must be thoroughly investigated, and medicines must be prescribed with care. One should achieve complete treatment and not linger over years. Life and death are crucial matters known only to heaven. Compassionate colleagues should think deeply about their duty to help the world.[37p]
    The Original Path of Medicine

    Great is medicine! Its origins are far-reaching! Since the chaos was separated and the wilderness began to take shape, the light and clear aspects of yang rose to form heaven, while the heavy and turbid aspects of yin descended to form earth. Heaven flourished and took its place above, while earth sank and took its place below. Thus, the essence of yang became the sun, rising in the east and setting in the west; the essence of yin became the moon, visible at night and hidden during the day. The dual principles stood firm, and the two luminaries moved in their courses. At this point, the primal energy condensed in the void, and water began to emerge; the red energy radiated through the void, and fire began to emerge; the azure energy floated in the void, and wood began to emerge; the white energy traversed the void, and metal began to emerge; the black energy met in the void, and earth began to emerge. With the five elements complete, myriad things were born, establishing the way of the three origins. Therefore, in human life, one must obtain the righteous energy of heaven and earth. The head is round, resembling heaven; the feet are square, resembling earth. Heaven has yin and yang, and humans have qi and blood. Heaven has five elements, and humans have five organs. The people of the divine Tian clan lived in nests in caves, subsisting on raw flesh and drinking blood. They moved to avoid the cold and took refuge in shadows to escape the heat. When the great simplicity had not yet opened, what ailments could there be? Later, Fuxi observed the heavens and the energy, and created hexagrams. In later generations, there emerged the "Heavenly Origin Jade Book," regarded as Fuxi's text, which was recited and passed down orally for ten generations. Shennong tasted all the herbs, experiencing seventy poisons in a single day; thereafter, the study of herbal medicine flourished.[38p]
    The Yellow Emperor donned his robes and ruled the world, engaging in dialogue with Qi Bo, the Heavenly Master, to discuss profound questions. They explored celestial phenomena above and the geography below, aiming to alleviate the suffering of the people. Thus, the "Inner Canon" was created. Once this text was established, when people fell ill, they would necessarily rely on acupuncture to treat external ailments and decoctions for internal healing. Subsequently, as simplicity vanished and desires grew rampant among the populace, disasters multiplied, and only with potent, minor, chronic, and non-toxic medicines could relief be achieved. The essence of medicine lies within the book "Suwen." It comprises twenty-four volumes and eighty-one chapters, detailing the principles of vital energy, clarifying the fundamentals of meridians, discussing diseases to address their root causes, and prescribing appropriate treatments—all systematically arranged and presented with clarity and coherence.

    Works such as the "Great Treatise on the Celestial Origin," "Great Treatise on the Six Origins," "Great Treatise on the Five Constants," and the "Great Treatise on the Changes of Qi," along with several chapters on intricate and subtle principles, truly represent a great legacy for ages, a complete path for liberation from bondage, guiding vital energy to save the common people, and aiding the weak to find peace. Below Xu and Qi, generations have found notable figures. Bian Que grasped a few principles and expounded on them in the "Nanjing"; Huangfu Shiyu followed and composed "Jia Yi"; Yang Shangshan compiled the "Tai Su." Just as the interpretations of Jin and Yuan revealed insights, and Master Qi's annotations clarified understanding, it is said that a pure source will yield a clear flow, and a well-structured display will cast a correct shadow—such has been the wisdom of renowned physicians through the ages.[39p]
    Only Zhang Zhongjing, the magistrate of Changsha during the Han dynasty, uniquely sought the root of medical knowledge, exploring subtle meanings, and establishing principles for regulation of rulers, ministers, and assistants. He created medical formulas, articulating the interplay of emptiness and fullness—a true secret not passed down for a millennium—demonstrating the qualities of a great sage and a second saint, with contributions that bridge past and future. Scholars since the Han and Tang dynasties have sought to delve into these profound insights. Moreover, the principles of the "Inner Canon" are deep and obscure, with no clear path for entry. Works like Chao Yuanfang’s "The Sources of Illness" and Sun Simiao’s "Thousand Golden Prescriptions" contain elaborate language, yet their principles become increasingly unclear; their prescriptions grow wider, straying from authenticity. Few have sincerely applied the teachings of the "Inner Canon."

    Upon Zhu Fengyi's discussion as the magistrate of Changsha, compiling the "Living Human Book" of Nanyang, Zhang Zhongjing taught that yin and yang should be interpreted as inner and outer, while Fengyi explained yin and yang in terms of cold and heat. A slight deviation can lead to significant errors; while they may have saved many lives, equally, many have died. Fortunately, Liu Zhi’s "Essential Discussions" and "Original Patterns of Illness" have been created, making the principles of the "Inner Canon" as clear as the sun and moon. With the creation of "Direct Rectification" and "Illuminating Discussions," the laws of Changsha have been condensed to essential principles. Transforming the Gui Zhi Ma Huang Decoction into the Double Resolving Powder and altering the Ten Jujube Decoction into the Three Flower Divine Support Pill demonstrates significant contributions to the sage’s teachings.[40p]
    At the same time, there were Zhang and others who expounded on the Great Way of the "Inner Canon," continuing the rightful source of Hejian and discussing learning with Ma Zhigui, also writing the book "Confucianism and Filial Piety." They claimed: "In vomiting, there is sweating; in diarrhea, there is replenishment. The sage has only three methods, with no fourth method." This is an unchangeable, definitive discourse and a profound maxim. Thus, there was the sorrow of Liu and Zhang.

    As for Dongyuan Lao-ren, he elucidated the principles of the "Suwen," adhering to the methods of Zhang Zhongjing, and authored "Ten Books on Rescuing Life and Extracting Essence" for the world, clarifying the pulse and the weights and measures of medications, as well as the rising, descending, floating, and sinking of drugs; hence, there are analogies of righteous and hegemonic methods.

    Regarding the Danxi Zhu family, they meticulously researched febrile diseases and internal injuries, with a particular focus on the profound implications of phlegm and fire, achieving remarkable insights. Song Taishi Lian remarked that their collection represents the pinnacle of medical achievement, which is indeed true.

    As for my dynasty, it revised the "Da Guan Materia Medica," created the "Bronze Man Acupuncture Classics," and the "Imperially Granted Medical Formulas," establishing the Taiyi Department to assist the sacred self, and appointing excellent physicians to assist the royal court, with the benevolent pharmacy to alleviate the sufferings of the people, demonstrating a heart of compassion akin to that of Xuanqi, surpassing the Han and Tang dynasties. Thus, esteemed physicians continued to emerge.

    For instance, Tao Jie'an's insights on febrile diseases disclosed matters not mentioned by Zhang Zhongjing; Xue Ji excelled in surgery, supplementing the gaps left by Dongyuan; Ge Kejiu's treatment of internal injuries, and Qian Ying's pediatric studies, were also not inferior to the Danxi legacy. The accomplishments of scholars in this age are indeed remarkable!

    If future students are aware of the essentials and follow them accordingly, the path will be accurate, and the medical doctrine will also be approachable. Therefore, it is said: "To know the essentials is to conclude with a single word."[41p]

    　　　　扁鹊仓公传赞 【汉　司马迁】
    　　太史公曰：女无美恶，居宫见妒；士无贤不肖，入朝见疑。故扁鹊以其伎见殃，仓公乃匿迹自隐而当刑，缇萦通尺牍，父得以复宁。故老子曰：美好者不祥之器，岂谓扁鹊等邪？若仓公者，可谓近之矣。
    　　　　汉书艺文志序 【汉　斑固】

    　　医经者，原人血脉经络骨髓，阴阳表里，以起百病之本，死生之分，而用度针石汤火所施，调百药齐和之所宜。至齐之得，犹慈石取铁，以物相使。拙者失理，以愈为剧，以生为死。经方者，本草石之寒温，量疾病之浅深，假药味之滋，因气感之宜，辨五苦六辛，致水火之齐，以通闭解结，反之于平。及失其宜者，以热益热，以寒增寒，精气内伤，不见于外，是所独失也。故谚曰：有病不治，常得中医。　　又 【汉　斑固】[1p]
    　　方技者，皆生生之具，王官之一守也。太古有岐伯、俞跗，中世有扁鹊、秦和。盖论病以及国，原诊以知政。汉兴有仓公，今其技术暗昧，故论其书以序方技。
    　　　　劝医论 【梁　简文帝】

    　　劝医曰：天地之中，惟人最灵。人之所重，莫过于命。虽修短有分，夭寿悬天，然而寒暑反常，嗜欲乖节，故疟寒痟暑，致毙不同；伐性烂肠，摧年匪一。拯斯之要，实在良方。故只域医王明于释典，如大师乃以医王为号，以如来能烦恼病，只能治四大乖为故。亦有骚人之咏彭城，秦国之称和缓，季梁之遇卢氏，虢子之值越人。爰至久视飞仙，长生妙道，犹变六一于金液，改三七于银丸。蓄玉匣之秘，研紫书之奥。桃胶何是，北斗靡遁其形。[2p]
    金浆非远，明珠还耻其价。能使业门之下，鼓响独传；雍祀之傍，萧声犹在。周礼疾医掌万民之疾，凡民之有病者，分而治之。岁终则各书其所治，而入于医师，知其愈与不愈，以为后之法戒也。至如研精元理，考核儒宗，尽日清谈，终夜讲习。始学则负墟尚谀，积功则为师乃着。日就月将，方称硕学；专经之后，犹须剧谈。网罗愈广，钩深理见；厌饫不寤，惟日不足。又若为诗，则多须见意，或古或今，或雅或俗，皆须寓目，详其去取，然后丽辞方吐，逸韵乃生。[3p]
    岂有秉笔不讯而能善诗，塞兑不谈而能善易？扬子云言：读赋千首，则能为赋。况医之为道，九部之诊甚精，百药之品难究。察色辨声，其功甚秘；秋辛夏苦，几微难识。而比之术者，未尝稽合，曾无讨论，多以少壮之时，涉猎方疏，略知甘草为甜，桂心为辣，便是宴驭自足；经方眠弃，同庚(岂戈)之读《庄子》，异孔丘之好《周易》。然而疾者求我，又不能尽意攻治，假使不能为地，自可即为己益。所以然者，若无隔贵贱，精加消息，以前验后，自可解之。日知所亡，坐成妙术，而又告以不能也。[4p]
    治疾者众，必以孟浪酬塞，悞人者多，爱人者鲜。是则日处百方，月为千轴，未尝不轻其药性，任其死生，浮华之功，于何而得？及其爱深亲属，情切支肌，患起膏肓，疴兴府俞，虽欲尽其治功，思无所出。何以故然？本不素习，卒难改变故也。胡麻鹿藿，才救头痛之疴；麦曲芎藭，反止河鱼之疾。思不出位，事局辕下，欲求反死者于元都，扬己名于绿帙，其可得乎？术道困穷，于斯实至。诚当善思此意，更兴其美，非直传名于后，亦是功德甚深，比夫脱一鸽于权衡，活万鱼于池水，不可同日而论焉。[5p]
    　　　　言医 【唐　李华】

    　　晋侯方图秦，既而有疾，秦伯使医和视之，将行，戒之曰：邻国相病，大夫何以为行？对曰：臣不发药石，请以词痊晋侯而国无害。秦伯悦，以卿礼遣之。和至于晋，晋君幄铜鞮之宫，凭丰肥，倚柔容，更衣被珠玉者百许人，膳夫列鼎于庭而后延客。客辞曰：始受命于寡君，以除君疾为役。今大国反以色与食病臣，非臣所及也。中军师对曰：此寡君待先生之礼也，不意为过，敬惟所择。客曰：臣辔而驰千里，形甚劳而气不足，所欲者，酒一盛，果一器，腒鱐佐饭而已，其余不敢烦大国。再拜受赐而诊之，曰：君声流而阳，气浊而浮，色寒而容壮，与楚王相若，亦可为也，亦不可为也。[6p]
    晋侯曰：楚子何如而方寡人？客曰：臣尝聘楚，楚境大而富，山川林薮之盛，逾淮而竟南海，晋与齐秦不敌也。晋侯曰：寡人未尝涉楚，且置楚王，愿闻其国之说。客曰：君不念，臣亦未究楚封疆之事，直以所见言之。楚也，近郊去郢，尚三百里。引车登冈，平视诸宫；丹素烛天，仰不见空。如水漂浮，半在其中；沧波动摇，低昂随风。蔼蔼南极，山松不尽；乍伏乍起，参差高卑。流云重轻，或灭或明；道路绵绵，萦山绕川。车盖如轩，稍觉登原；赤霄冒顶，举手摩天。向之高者，乃在车下；阴壑冥冥，投石无声。状其乳苑之内，则连山黯以当户，容杳杳而嶪嶪，若坚刃与幔涂，呀将拆而复合。露封隙之嵌空，声小往而大答。耸崖岘以日爌，穿偃仆而云罯。[7p]
    滨江皋衍，百里芳草，往往白沙，日炙皛溔；绿野芊绵，走举苍连；箘簵楩梓，橘柚之林；密孕元气，寒暑若一；翳不流风，幽不漏日；猿狙飞走，经息百态；啾啾互号，终昕竟晦；坠英纷目，如雪蔽路；四望无人，移足没履；黄鸟时鸣，白鹇飞度；临险瞯江，江隈为潭；庱庱不动，常有神怪，龟鱼涵泳，露鳞出介；纤草以飖，风飐波起；崩涛迸沫，势不得止。[8p]
    精怖魂怕，毛骨洗然；攀木瞑眸，犹惧踣泉；颓麓疏冗，繁源鼻歕；支流潆潆，合注汤汤；昼夜有声，当暑清凉；透崖扑湍，跃而后逝；初疑可及，忽似无际；旋眩回滑，淜泊兑宕；辊石敌寨，火发川上；才夷又亚，倾沙委浪；白烟微苍，通波满望；淡淡艳艳，久而生垠；淅淅飞雨，冥冥起云；沅湘春生，苍梧日晚；声与听尽，色随望远；苹荇荷华，组绣一川。愕羽族之多名，纷合散于水间；泛随流而将下，时逆浪而复还。喧呼雷骇，沉起云翻；两不相伤，貌豫礼闲；缘涯迭观，照江成霞；碧水涟漪，深浅见沙；旁经闱闼，溢浸栏槛；上有嫔嫱，绵音入云。侵杳眇而将绝，随隤风而复闻。[9p]
    齐宋郑卫之乐，张于宫中；撞金击石，草木竞发；坚城雉坼，崇山峰坠；鸟兽狂悸，淮湖皆沸。首饰戴千金，一膳倾千家。耻不相及，人以粒计；仓禄之众，半于平人；秣马之费，倍于租入。其余绮丽之富，奉养之侈，率与是侔。楚王甚泰而楚人甚病，申叔请老而不与政。言未毕，晋侯舒气而伸干，曰：向先生吉亦可为也，何哉？客曰：此未足累楚，故曰可为也。若张而无厌，则不可为也。晋侯色生力起，斥御者撤膳羞而请曰：先生终说寡人病，幸闻矣。[10p]
    客乘时而动之曰：楚使令尹司马理兵于北疆，以临敝邑。敞邑大夫，少者则请开关以战，老者则曰君务息人。楚恃其富强，因侈生欲，未足畏也。寡君乃发府，将赒而四境，寡小君以四时之用为请，寡君曰：是出于人而归于人，无人则无是矣，夫何爱焉！申命上大夫布币于人而谢之，曰：孤不德，使尔父子兄弟，不自保于楚师，故罄以相劳。秦人感君皆泣，妇人处子亦请执报楚，楚朝闻而夕卷师。君臣震伏而受职于秦，此先王不战之术也。晋侯恍以楚事而照于晋，遂辍谋秦。由是大国修好，小国来朝，天下皆服。客果以词痊晋，故曰言医。
    　　　　迷医 【宋　龚鼎臣】[11p]

    　　《周官》载医掌养万民之疾病。盖凡受疾者，举可治也。唯久之不治，遂革以死。未见其有治疾而不可治者也。巴楚之地，俗言巫鬼，实自古而然。当五气相沴，或致厉疫之苦，率以谓天时被是疾，非医药所能攻，故请祷鬼神无少暇，鸡豚鸭羊之荐，唯恐不丰。迨其不能，则莫不自咎事鬼神之未至；或幸而愈，乃曰：由祷之勤也，荐之数也。不然，乌能与天时抗乎？又有治之不早，其疾之毒日相熏灼，一家之人皆至乎病。故虽亲友之厚，百步之外，不敢望其门庐，以至得病之家，惧相迁染，子畏其父，妇避其夫。若富财之人，尚得一巫觋守之；其穷匮者，独僵卧呻吟一室而已。如是则不特绝医药之馈，其饮食之给亦绝矣。如是以死者，未尝不十八九，而民终不悟。余尝访于人，其患非他，繇觋师之胜医师耳。呜呼！觋者其能必胜诸医哉！其所胜之者，盖世俗之人易以邪惑也。夫疾病干诸内，鬼神冥诸外。良药，所以治内也。[12p]
    今不务除疾于内，而专求外福之来，及其甚也，其存恤讯问之宜，不复相通，不其谬欤？夫稼茂田畴为螟蜮所害，唯能悉除螟蜮，则稼之秀可实也。家蓄高货而盗入其门，主人操刃持梃，或杀或捕，则赀之厚可全也。人之身亦然。冒阴阳之气，辄遇疠疫，当得医者察声视色，按脉授药，使离诸腹心肝膈，然后其体可平。若不医之用，易异不除螟蜮而望稼穑之实，不驱盗贼而求家赀之全，决不可得。矧惟国家重医药之书，最为事要。[13p]
    元朝编辑名方，颁布天下郡国，其间述时疫之状，实为纤悉。及庆历中范文正公建言，俾自京师以逮四方学医之人，皆聚而讲习，以精其术，其黜庸谬，救生灵，倬然为治道之助。而世俗罔识朝廷仁爱之意如此，而徒惑邪诞而夭性命，愚实悯之。今已戒医博士日与医之徒，考神农子仪扁鹊秦和之术，一会于岐伯俞柎之道，以正黜邪，以诚消妄，使可治之疾，不终害人，亦济民之一事也。而虑巴賨之俗，尚安故态，不知医效之神，倍祷淫祀之鬼，故刻词以告。[14p]
    　　　　医铭 【宋　吕诲】

    　　晋人武泰，通医术，守臣献状，补太医正，还乡里，创起应圣侯庙。艺既成，归善于师，又将广懋来学，其志有足称者。予谪官于是，迁守蒲中，既行，丐文以显于庙，因作医铭，嘉乃意勤，遂成其志。知予言有以滋其善也。铭曰：　　六气五行，人禀而生，三部九候，纳诸和平。昔称绝技，湔肠涤胃，辅以砭石，因之决溃。察脉之原，当于未然，不攻而胜，庶几十全。愈世之病，如持国柄，常使众邪，不得干正。能尽己意，膏肓必起，苟利于艺，毫厘千里。泰也有为，心不忘师，义利之重，慎乎所治。[15p]

    　　　　盖公堂记 【宋　苏轼】

    　　始吾居乡，有病寒而咳者问诸医，医以为蛊，不治且杀人，取其百金而治之。饮以蛊药，攻伐其胃肠，烧灼其体肤，禁切其饮食之美者，期月而百疾作，内热恶寒而咳不已，累然真蛊者也。又求于医，医以为热，授之以寒药，旦朝吐之，暮夜下之，于是始不能食；惧而反之，则锺乳乌喙，杂然并进，而漂疽痈疥眩瞀之状，无所不至，三易医而疾愈甚。里老父教之曰：是医之罪，药之过也！子何疾之有？人之生也，以气为主，食为辅。今子终日药不释口，臭味乱于外，而百毒战于内，劳其主，隔其辅，是以病也。子退而休之，谢医却药而进所嗜，气完而食美矣！则夫药之良者，可以一饮而效。从之，期月而病良已。[16p]
    　　　　药诵 【前人】

    　　嵇中散作幽愤诗，知不免矣，而卒章乃曰：采薇山阿，散发岩岫，永啸长吟，颐性养寿者，悼此志之不遂也。司马景王既杀中散而悔，使悔于未杀之前，中散得免于死者，吾知其扫迹灭景于人间，如脱兔之投林也。采薇散发，岂其所难哉？孙真人着大风恶疾论曰：神仙传数十人，皆因恶疾而得仙道。何者？割弃尘累，怀颍阳之风，所以因祸而取福也。吾始得罪迁岭表，不自意全，既逾年无后命，知不死矣。然旧苦痔，至是大作，呻呼几百日。地无医药，有亦不效。道士教吾去滋味，绝熏血，以清净胜之。[17p]
    痔有虫，馆于吾后，滋味熏血，既以自养，亦以养虫。自今日以往，旦夕食淡面四两，犹复念食则以胡麻、茯苓麨足之，饮食之外，不啖一吻。主人枯槁则客自弃去。尚恐习性易流，故取中散真人之言，对病为药，使人诵之日三，曰：东坡居士！汝忘逾年之忧，百日之苦乎？使汝不幸而有中散之祸，伯牛之疾，虽欲采薇散发，岂可得哉？今食麻麦、茯苓多矣。居士则歌以答之曰：事无事之事，百事治兮；味无味之味，五味备兮。茯苓麻麦，有时而匮兮，有则食，无则已者，与我无既兮。呜呼噫嘻！馆客不终，以是为愧兮。[18p]
    　　　　送夏医序 【宋　朱熹】

    　　予尝病世之为论者，皆以为天下之事，宜于今者不必根于古，谐于俗者不必本于经。及观夏君之医，而又有以知其决不然也。盖夏君之医，处方用药，奇怪绝出，有若不近人情者，而其卒多验。及问其所以然者，则皆据经考古，而未尝无所自也。予于是窃有感焉。因书遗之，以信其术于当世，又以风吾党之不师古而自用者云。[19p]
    　　　　与杨元甫论梁宽甫病证书 【元　许衡】
    　　梁宽甫候：右胁，肺部也；嗽而唾血，举动喘促者，肺脉也；发热脉数不能食者，火来刑金，肺与脾俱虚也。肺与脾俱虚而火乘之，其病为逆如此者，例不可补泻。盖补金则虑金与火持，而喘咳益增；泻火则虑火不退位，而痃癖反盛。正宜补中益气汤先扶元气，少以活病药加之。闻已用此药而不获效，意必病势苦逆而药力未到也。当与宽甫熟论，远期秋凉，庶就平复。盖脉病恶春夏火气，至秋冬则退也。止宜于益气汤中，随四时阴阳，升降浮沉，温凉寒热，及见有证增损服之。升降浮沉则顺之，温凉寒热则反之。顺其顺，和其和，为治之大方也。或觉气壅，间服加减枳朮丸；或有饮食，间服局方枳朮汤。数月后，庶逆气少回，逆气回则治法可施。[20p]
    但恐今日以至色青色赤，及脉弦脉洪，则无及矣。近世论医，有主河间刘氏者，有主易州张氏者。张氏用药，依准四时阴阳而增损之，正《内经》四气调神之义。医而不知此，妄行也。刘氏用药，务在推陈致新，不使少有怫郁，正造化新新不伤之义。医而不知此，无术也。然而主张氏者，或未尽张氏之奥，则瞑眩之剂，终莫敢投，至失机后时而不救者多矣。主刘氏者，或未悉刘氏之蕴，则劫效目前，阴损正气，遗祸于后日者多矣。能用二家之长，而无二家之弊，则治庶几乎！宽甫病候，初感必深，所伤物当时消导不尽，停滞淹延，变生他证，以至于今。恐亦宜仿刘氏推陈致新之意，少加消导药于益气汤中，庶有渐缓之期也。鄙见如此，未敢以为必然，惟吾才卿元甫子益共商论之。[21p]
    　　　　读药书漫记 【元　刘因】

    　　人秉是气，以为五脏百骸之身者，形实相孚而气亦流通。其声色气味之接乎人之口鼻耳目者，虽若泛然，然其在我而同其类者，固已月　曶然而相合，异其类者，固已怫然而相戾，虽其人之身，亦不得而自知也。如饮药者，以枯木腐骨荡为虀粉，相错合以饮之，而亦各随其气类而之焉，盖其原一也。故先儒谓：木味酸，木之根立地中似骨，故骨以酸养之。金味辛，金之缠合异物似筋，故筋以辛养之。咸，水也，似脉；苦，火也，似气；甘，士也，似肉。其形固已与类矣，而其气安得不与之流通也！推而言之，其吉凶之于善恶亦类也。　　又 【元　刘因】[22p]
    　　天生此一世人，而一世事固能办也，盖亦足乎已而无待于外也。岭南多毒，而有金蛇白药以治毒；湖南多气，而有姜橘茱萸以治气。鱼鳖螺蚬，治湿气而生于水；麝香羚羊，冶石毒而生于山。盖不能有以胜彼之气，则不能生于其气之中。而物之与是气俱生者，夫固必使有用于是气也。犹朱子谓天将降乱，必生弭乱之人以拟其后。以此观之，世固无无用之人，人固无不可处之世也。[23p]
    　　　　丹溪翁传赞 【元　戴良】

    　　论曰：昔汉严君平博学无不通，卖卜成都，人有邪恶非正之问，则依蓍龟为陈其利害，与人子言依于孝，与人弟言依于顺，与人臣言依于忠。史称其风声气节，足以激贪而厉俗。翁在婺得道学之源委，而混迹于医，或以医来见者，未尝不以葆精毓神开其心。至于一语一默，一出一处，凡有关于伦理者，尤谆谆训诲，使人奋迅感慨激厉之不暇。左丘明有云：仁人之言，其利溥哉！信矣！若翁者，殆古所谓直谅多闻之益友，又可以医师少之哉。
    　　　　赠贾思诚序 【明　宋濂】

    　　同里张君，以书来谓濂曰：壬辰之秋，兵发中原，大江之南，所在皆绎骚。时惟伯嘉纳公持部使者节来莅浙东，慎简群材，官而任之，以保障乎一方。余虽不敏，公不以为无似，俾摄录事判官。判官职在抚治一城生聚，凡其捍御绥辑之策，不惮昼夜而勤行之，以酬公知遇之万一。然节宣之功不加，日积月深，以劳而致疾。[24p]
    疾之初作，大热发四体中，继之以昏仆；迨其苏也，双目运眩，耳中作秋蝉鸣，神思恍惚，若孑孑然离群而独立，若御惊飙而游行太空，若乘不系之舟以簸荡于三峡四溟之间，殊不能自禁。闻丹溪朱先生彦修者，名传四方，亟延治之。先生至，既脉曰：内摇其真，外劳其形，以亏其阴，以耗其生，宜收视返听于太虚之庭，不可专借药而已之也。因属其高弟子贾君思诚，留以护治之。贾君即视余如手足之亲，无所不至其意。虑余怒之过也，则治之以悲；悲之过也，则治之以喜；喜之过也，则治之以恐；恐之过也，则治之以思；思之过也，则治之以怒。[25p]
    左之右之，扶之掖之，又从而调柔之。不特此也，其逆厥也，则药其涌泉以寤之；其怔忡也，则按其心俞而定之。如是者数年，不可一朝夕离去。宁食不鲜羞，衣不裼裘，何可一日以无贾君？宁士不鲁邹，客不公侯，何可一日以无贾君？余疾于是乎告瘳，而贾君有功于余者甚大矣。子幸赐之一言，多贾君之善，而昭余之不敢忘德于贾君，不识可不可乎？余发张君之书，重有感焉。世之为民宰者，恒饱食以嬉。[26p]
    其视吾民之颠连漠然，若秦越肥瘠之不相维系；非惟不相维系，又盬其髓，刳其膏而不知止。孰有如张君勤民之疾者乎？世之医者，酬接之繁，不暇雍容，未信宿辄谢去；至有视不暇脉，脉不暇方，而不可挽留者。孰有如贾君调护数年之久而不生厌者乎？是皆可书。余方执笔以从文章家之后，此而不书，乌乎书！虽然，今之官政苟虐，敲扑椎击，惟日不足，我民病此久矣！我瞻四方，何林林乎？州邑之间，其有贤牧宰能施刀圭之剂，以振起之者乎？设有是，余虽不敏，犹能研墨濡毫，大书而不一书。是为序。
    　　　　赠医师葛某序 【前人】[27p]

    　　古之医师必通于三世之书。所谓三世者，一曰《针灸》，二曰《神农本草》，三曰《太素脉诀》。脉诀所以察证，本草所以辨药，针灸所以去疾。非是三者，不可以言医。故记礼者有云：医不三世，不服其药也。传经者，明载其说，复斥其非，而以父子相承三世为言，何其惑欤？夫医之为道，必志虑渊微，机颖明发，然后可与于斯，虽其父不能传其子也。吾乡有严生者，三世业医矣。其为医，专事乎大观之方，他皆愦愦，绝弗之省。又有朱聘君，家世习儒，至聘君始以医鸣，医家诸书，则无不精览。一少年病肺气上，喀喀鸣喉中，急则唾，唾血成缕。严曰：此瘵也，后三月死。聘君曰：非也。气升而腴，中失其枢，火官司令，烁金于炉，是之谓肺痿，治之坐。已而果生。一六十翁患寒热，初毛洒淅，齿击下上，热继之，盛如蒸甑。严曰：此痰也，不治将差。聘君曰：非也。脉淫以芤，数复乱息，外强中干，祸作福极，是之谓解(亻亦)。[28p]
    药之则瘳，不药则剧。已而果剧，治乃愈。一妇女有哕疾，每吐涎数升，腥触人，人近亦哕。严曰：此寒哕也，法宜温。聘君曰：非也。阴阳未平，气苞血聚，其势方格，靡有攸处，是之谓恶阻。在法不当治，久则自宁，且生男。言后辄验。夫严生之医三世矣，聘君则始习为之，而优劣若是者，医其可以世论否耶？嗟夫！昔之名医众矣，未暇多论。若华元化，若张嗣伯，若许智藏，其治证皆入神，初不闻其父子相传也。自传经惑于是非，使《礼经》之意，晦而不白三千年矣。世之索医者，不问其通书与否，见久于其业者，则瞀瞀焉从之。人问其故，则曰是记礼者云尔也，其可乎哉？葛生某，淮之巨族也，明于医，三世之书，皆尝习而通之，出而治疾，决死生，验差剧，若独照而龟卜无爽也者。士或不能具药，辄注之不索其偿，士君子翕然称誉之，名上丞相府，赐七品服，俾提举诸医官，有疾者，遂倚之以为命。呜呼！若葛生者，其无愧古之医师者欤！[29p]
    　　　　赠医师贾某序 【前人】

    　　医之为道，难言久矣。然必审诊以起度量，立规矩，称权衡，合色脉，表里有余不足顺逆之法，复参其人之动静，与其息之相应，然后从而治之，则其事为甚不轻矣。非洞明应世群书之得失，尚可与于斯乎？《黄帝内经》，虽疑先秦之士依仿而托之，其言深，其旨邃以宏，其考辨信而有征，是当为医家之宗。下此则秦越人和缓，无书可传。越人所著《八十一难经》，则皆举《内经》之要而推言者也。又下此则淳于意、华佗之熊经鸱顾，固亦导引家之一术，至于刳腹背湔肠胃而去疾，则涉于神怪矣。意之医状，司马迁备志之。其所谓迥风杳风者，今人绝不知为何证，况复求于治疗之深旨乎？又下则张机。机之《金匮玉函经》及伤寒诸论，诚千古不刊之典。第详于六气所伤，而情欲食饮罢劳之所致者，略而弗议，兼之文字错简，亦未易以序次求之也。又下此则王叔和。[30p]
    叔和纂岐伯、华佗等书为《脉经》，叙阴阳内外，辨三部九候，分人迎气口，条陈十二经络，洎夫三焦五脏六腑之病，最为着明。惜乎为妄男子括以肤陋之脉歌，遂使其本书不盛布于世也。又下此则巢元方。其《病源候论》似不为无所见者，但言风寒二湿，而不着湿热之文，乃其失也。又下此则王冰。冰推五运六气之变，撰为《天元玉策》，周详切密，亦人之所难，苟泥之则局滞而不通矣。又下此则王焘、孙思邈。[31p]
    思邈以绝人之识，操慈仁恻厚之心，其列《千金方》、《翼》及工害人之祸，至为愤切，后人稍闯其藩垣，亦足以其术鸣，但不知伤寒之数，或弗能无遗憾也。焘虽暗劣，《外台秘要》所言方证符禁灼灸之详，颇有所袓述，然谓针能杀生人而不能起死人者，则一偏之见也。又下此则钱乙、庞安时、许叔微。叔微在准绳尺寸之中而无所发明，安时虽能出奇应变而终未离于范围，二人皆得机之粗者也。惟乙深造机之阃奥，而撷其精华，建为五脏之方，各随所宜。[32p]
    肝有相火则有泻而无补，肾为真水则有补而无泻，皆启《内经》之秘，尤知者之所取法。世概以婴孺医目之，何其知乙之浅哉？其遗书散亡，出于阎孝忠所集者，多孝忠之意，初非乙之本真也。又下此则上谷张元素、河间刘完素、睢水张从正。元素之与完素，虽设为奇梦异人以神其授受，实闻乙之风而兴起然者。若从正则又宗夫完素者也。元素以古方新病，决不能相值，治疾一切不以方，故其书亦不传。其存于今者，皆后来之所傅会。其学则东垣、李杲深得之。杲推明内外二伤，而多注意于补脾土之说。盖以土为一身之主，土平则诸脏平矣。从正以吐汗下三法，风寒暑湿火燥六门，为医之关键，其治多攻，其剂多峻厉，不善学者杀人。完素论风火之病，以《内经》病机气宜十九条，若为《原病式》，简奥粹微，有非大观局诸医所可仿佛。究其设施，则亦不越补攻二者之间也。[33p]
    嗟乎！自有《内经》以来，医书之藏有司者，凡一百七十九家，二百九部，二千二百五十九卷，亦不为不多也。他未遑深论，即今所论者言之，世之医师，果能尽心于斯否乎？脱或未尽心于斯，则夫起度量、立规矩、称权衡、合色脉之属，焉能察而行之？不至以人命为戏也几希矣。虽然，亦有要焉，逆与顺之谓也。曰升降、曰浮沉，吾则顺之；曰温凉、曰寒热，吾则逆之。果能此道矣，则去夫先医之所治，虽不中，不远矣。[34p]
    然又未易以一蹴至也。非求之极博而观其会通，安可遽反于至约之域乎？医之道所以难言者，盖若此而已。乌伤？贾思诚，濂外弟也，性醇介，有君子之行，尝同濂师事城南闻先生，学治经。久之，思诚复去受医说于彦修朱先生之门。诸儒家所著，无所不窥，出而治疾，往往有奇验。荐绅间多为赋诗，而属濂以序。濂非知医者，将何以为思诚告哉？而思诚请之不倦，因为直疏历世群书之得失，而勖思诚以学者如此。初不暇，如他日作者簸弄笔舌，交错以成文也。
    　　　　论医 【明　王袆】[35p]

    　　予观近时言医者，莫盛于吴中。而吴中世业医者，莫盛于葛氏。葛氏之医，其术善于推五运六气之盛衰，以审病证而定治法。此仲正之术，所以异于人人也。盖仲正之诸父曰恒斋者，尝自著书，其说以谓医当视时之盛衰而为损益。刘守真、张子和辈，值金人强盛，民悍气刚，故多用宣泄之法。及其衰也，兵革之余，饥馑相仍，民劳志困，故张洁古、李明之辈，多加补益之功。至宋之纪年，医者大抵务守护气元而不知攻伐之机，能养病而不能治病，失在不知通其变也。其说如此，可谓能明夫气运之变，而通于阴阳之化者矣。
    　　　　明医箴 【明　龚信】

    　　今之明医，心存仁义。博览群书，精通道艺。洞晓阴阳，明知运气。药辨温凉，脉分表里。治用补泻，病审虚实。因病制方，对证投剂。妙法在心，活变不滞。不衒虚名，惟期博济。不计其功，不谋其利。不论贫富，药施一例。起死回生，恩同天地。如此明医，芳垂万世。[36p]
    　　　　庸医箴 【前人】

    　　今之庸医，衒奇立异。不学经书，不通字义。妄自矜夸，以欺当世。争趋人门，不速自至。时献苞苴，问病为意。自逞以能，百般贡谀。病家不审，模糊处治。不察病源，不分虚实。不畏生死，孟浪一时。忽然病变，急自散去。悞人性命，希图微利。如此庸医，可耻可忌。
    　　　　病家箴 【前人】

    　　今之病家，多惜所费。不肯急医，待至自愈。不求高明，希图容易。不察病情，轻投妄试。或祷鬼神，诸般不啻。履霜不谨，坚冰即至。及请明医，病已将剧。纵有灵丹，难以救治。懵然不悟，迟悞所致。惟说命尽，作福未至。这般糊涂，良可叹息。如此病家，当革斯弊。
    　　　　警医箴 【前人】

    同时有张子和者，出明《内经》之大道，续河间之正源，与麻知几讲学，而作《儒门事亲》之书，乃曰：吐中有汗，泻中有补，圣人止有三法，无第四法，乃不易之确论，至精之格言。于是有刘张之泒矣。若东垣老人，明《素问》之理，宗仲景之法，作《济生拔萃十书》以传于世，明脉取权衡规矩，用药体升降浮沉，是以有王道霸道譬焉。至于丹溪朱氏，伤寒内伤杂病无不精研，痰火奥义，尤其独得。宋太史濂谓其集医家之大成，诚哉是言也。迨及我朝，修《大观本草》，制《铜人俞穴针灸经》、《御赐医方》等书，设太医以辅圣躬，立良医以佐王府，惠民药局以济民间夭札，其仁天下之心，宛如轩岐一揆而远迈汉唐，是以明医迭出。如陶节庵之伤寒，发仲景之所未发；薛己之外科，补东垣之未备；葛可久之内伤，钱瑛之小儿，亦无忝于丹溪。昭代作人之功，其盛矣乎！后学知道统之自，则门径不差，而医道亦可近矣。故曰：知其要者，一言而终。[41p]

    Praise for the Biography of Bian Que and Cang Gong [Han, Sima Qian]

    The Grand Historian said: Women have no true beauty or ugliness, yet in the palace, they are prone to jealousy; scholars possess neither virtue nor vice, but when entering the court, they are seen with suspicion. Thus, Laozi said: “Beautiful things are vessels of misfortune,” can we say that Bian Que is among them? Those like Cang Gong could be considered to be closer to this idea.

    Preface to the Artistic Literature Section of the Book of Han [Han, Ban Gu]

    The medical canon originates from the understanding of human blood, vessels, marrow, yin and yang, to identify the root of illnesses and the distinction between life and death. It involves the proper application of acupuncture, stones, herbs, and heat, harmonizing various medications according to their appropriateness. The acquisition of benefits is like that of using a kind stone to extract iron, utilizing materials accordingly. Those who are unskilled may misunderstand the principles, mistaking recovery for severity, and life for death. The classical formulas are based on the temperature of herbs and stones, assessing the depth of illnesses, using the taste of medicine, tailored to the response of the body, distinguishing between the five bitter and six pungent flavors, balancing water and fire to unblock and resolve issues, restoring harmony. If one loses their appropriateness, they might exacerbate heat with heat or cold with cold, injuring their essence internally, which is a unique loss. Hence, it is said: “There are illnesses that go untreated but often result in the use of traditional Chinese medicine.”[1p]

Observing colors and discerning sounds, their effectiveness is deeply secret; in autumn, ailments can be subtle, and in summer, sufferings are hard to identify. When compared to the practitioners, there has never been thorough agreement or discussion; often, in youth, scholars skim the surface, knowing vaguely that licorice is sweet and cassia is spicy, thinking this is enough for their practice. Yet, the established methods are disregarded; they read Zhuangzi in the same year, while others favor the Book of Changes. However, when patients seek my help, I still cannot fully achieve the desired treatment, even if I cannot reach the source, it can still benefit myself. Therefore, if there are no barriers of social status, and one diligently enhances their knowledge with past experience, they can indeed unravel the complexities. Daily learning what is lacking, one can compose brilliant techniques, yet I would still say it cannot be done.[4p]

    治疾者众，必以孟浪酬塞，悞人者多，爱人者鲜。是则日处百方，月为千轴，未尝不轻其药性，任其死生，浮华之功，于何而得？及其爱深亲属，情切支肌，患起膏肓，疴兴府俞，虽欲尽其治功，思无所出。何以故然？本不素习，卒难改变故也。胡麻鹿藿，才救头痛之疴；麦曲芎藭，反止河鱼之疾。思不出位，事局辕下，欲求反死者于元都，扬己名于绿帙，其可得乎？术道困穷，于斯实至。诚当善思此意，更兴其美，非直传名于后，亦是功德甚深，比夫脱一鸽于权衡，活万鱼于池水，不可同日而论焉。[5p]

    There are many who treat illnesses, but they often respond carelessly and indiscriminately. Those who truly care for others are rare. Thus, one may spend days traversing various methods and months honing countless techniques, yet they have never truly respected the efficacy of their medicines, leaving matters of life and death to chance. Their superficial accomplishments are of no real value. When love runs deep among kin, and feelings are intense, but ailments arise in critical areas, and diseases manifest severely, even if one wishes to exert their full efforts to heal, they find that there are no results. Why is this? It is because they lack foundation in these matters and are thus difficult to change.

    Certain herbs like hu ma (flaxseed) can only alleviate headaches, while others like mai qu (barley) and qiong huan (spicebush) may counteract specific ailments. If one fails to recognize their place and is constrained by their circumstances, how can they hope to resuscitate the dead in the capital and gain fame among the elite? Such aspirations are unattainable. The path of healing is genuinely challenging—this is a stark reality.

    It is indeed necessary to contemplate these thoughts and elevate what is good. This is not only about leaving a name for future generations; it is also an act of profound merit. Comparing it to freeing a single pigeon on a scale or reviving ten thousand fish in a pond is not something that can be discussed in the same breath.[5p]

    On Medicine by Li Hua (Tang Dynasty)

Before he set off, he cautioned, "With neighboring countries in conflict, how can a physician proceed with his task?" He replied, "I do not dispense medicine or herbs; I will use words to cure the Duke of Jin so that the nation suffers no harm." The Duke of Qin was pleased and honored him with a grand send-off.

The physician declined, saying, "I was commissioned by my lord to remedy your illness. Now, the great country has turned to appearances and meals to treat me; this is beyond my capacity." The military officials replied, "This is how our lord honors you, not an oversight, please respect our customs." The physician said, "I have journeyed a thousand miles at great physical strain and lack energy. All I desire is one cup of wine, a dish of fruit, and some light side dishes to accompany my meal; I don't wish to burden your great country with more."

Its territory is vast and rich, with mountains, rivers, forests, and marshes stretching beyond the Huai River all the way to the South Sea. Jin cannot compete with Qi and Qin." The Duke of Jin said: "I have never ventured into Chu and am unfamiliar with its king. I wish to hear about that country." The guest said: "Your Grace, I do not recall delving into the details of Chu's boundaries either; I can only speak of what I have seen. Chu is near the outskirts, about three hundred miles from Ying. If you drive a cart up the hill, you can see the various palaces; the red and white hues illuminate the heavens, yet you cannot see the sky above. Like floating water, half is within; the waves of the vast water sway, rising and falling with the wind. The majestic southern peaks, pine trees are everlasting; sometimes lurking, sometimes rising, creating a varied landscape of heights and depths. Flowing clouds shift in weight, at times vanishing, at times brightening; the roads stretch and wind, surrounding mountains and rivers. The cart covers seems like a pavilion as you ascend the high ground; the red sky looms overhead, and you raise your hand to touch the heavens. What appeared high is now beneath the cart; the dark valley is shrouded, stones thrown make no sound. Describing its inner pastures, the mountains darkly frame the doors, with a hazy and jagged view, as if strong blades and curtains were to come apart and come together again. Dew seals the gaps in the rock, sounds small as they come, yet resonate loudly in response. The cliffs are steep and bathed in sunlight, piercing through the horizon as clouds hover."[7p]

The decayed mountains are sparse and vast, while the thick source roars in the nose. Branching streams ripple, converging in deep currents; day and night there is sound, cool against the heat. Through cliffs, rushing torrents rush forth and leap away; initially thought reachable, suddenly seeming boundless. Whirling and sliding, swirling in eddies; stones rolling against fortifications, fire igniting on the riverbanks. Confirmed as tamed and yet turbulent, sand spilling into waves; white smoke faintly blue, waters stretching into view. Faintly vibrant, lasting long enough to form boundaries; soft rain falls, clouds rise in the dark. Spring is born in the Yuan and Xiang rivers, evening arrives in Cangwu; sound and silence end, color follows the distant gaze. Water plants and lotus flowers create a delicate embroidery across the river. Astonished by the multitude of bird names, they disperse and unite upon the water; drifting downstream, sometimes resisting the waves to return. Roaring and terrifying like thunder, clouds rise and churn; neither harms the other, appearances are serene in leisurely grace. By the riverbank, they repeatedly observe, reflecting the river into a rosy haze; clear water ripples, with depths revealing sand; beside the corridors, overflow drenches the railings. Above are fair maidens, soft sounds entering the clouds. As dusk deepens and is about to vanish, following the drifting wind, the sounds are heard once more.[9p]

Strong city walls crumbled, towering mountains collapsed; birds and beasts were frightened, and the Huai Lake boiled over. Adornments worth thousands were worn, a meal could feed a thousand households. It is disgraceful not to be connected, measured by grains; the stores of grain were half that of commoners; the expenses for feeding horses doubled the rent collected. The remaining lavish wealth and extravagant maintenance were on par with this. The King of Chu was very secure, yet the people of Chu were very ill; Shén Shūqǐ aged but did not engage in governance. Before finishing his words, the Duke of Jin let out a breath and stretched his hand, saying: "Sir, your good fortune is indeed possible, why is that?" The guest replied: "This is not enough to burden Chu, hence I say it is possible. But if it expands endlessly, then it is not possible." The Duke of Jin’s countenance changed, strength surged within him; he dismissed the attendants, withdrew the meal shamefully, and requested, saying: "Sir, you finally mention the ills of your humble servant, I am fortunate to have heard this."[10p]

    The guest, riding at the right time, said: "The ruler of Chu has sent the Commandant Sima to manage the troops at the northern border, threatening our city. The young nobles of the city petitioned to open the gates for battle, while the elder ones advised that the ruler must prioritize the well-being of the people. Chu relies on its wealth and strength, and indulges in desires that are not sufficiently frightening. The ruler then mobilized resources to provide aid to the borders, while the smaller rulers sought to ensure supplies for the four seasons. The ruler remarked: 'This aid is given by people and returned to people; without people, there would be none of this. What is there to cherish?' He then ordered the senior nobles to distribute money to the people and express gratitude, saying: 'I am unworthy, causing your fathers and brothers not to protect themselves against the army of Chu, which is why I have exhausted all efforts for you.' The people of Qin, moved by the ruler's words, wept, and even women and daughters requested to take revenge on Chu. Upon hearing this, the court of Chu withdrew its army by evening. Both ruler and ministers were deeply shocked and accepted their roles under Qin; this was the method of the former kings who did not engage in warfare. The Duke of Jin was perplexed by the events in Chu and, consequently, ceased plotting against Qin. Thus, the great nations established friendly relations while the smaller ones came to pay homage, leading all under heaven to submit. The guest indeed healed Jin with his words, hence it is said he spoke as a physician."[11p]

    The "Zhou Guan" states that physicians are responsible for treating the illnesses of the people. Generally, any affliction one suffers can be cured, but if left untreated for too long, it may lead to death. One has yet to observe an illness that cannot be cured. In the regions of Ba and Chu, there is a custom of consulting spirits, which has existed since ancient times. When the five elements are in disarray, causing the suffering of severe plagues, it is often said that these are illnesses brought by the seasons and not something medicine can address. Therefore, they pray to the spirits without rest, offering chicken, pig, duck, and sheep in hopes of receiving bountiful blessings. If they cannot recover, they blame themselves for the spirits not coming to aid; if they are fortunate enough to heal, they attribute it to their diligent prayers and numerous offerings. Otherwise, how could they contend with the elements?

    Moreover, if treatment is delayed, the poison of the illness gradually affects the entire household. Thus, even close friends and relatives dare not approach each other's homes, fearing the spread of infection; children fear their parents, and wives avoid their husbands. Wealthy individuals may still manage to have a shaman protect them, while the poor are left to suffer alone in silence. In such circumstances, not only is the charity of medicine cut off, but even food supplies are also scarce. As a result, those who die are often around eighteen or nineteen, yet the people remain oblivious to the causes.

    I once inquired about this with others, and it turned out the real issue was the dominance of shamans over physicians. Alas! Can shamans truly surpass all physicians? What they conquer are merely the easily misled common folk. For illnesses are rooted within the body, while spirits lurk outside. Good medicine is meant to treat internal afflictions.[12p]

    今不务除疾于内，而专求外福之来，及其甚也，其存恤讯问之宜，不复相通，不其谬欤？夫稼茂田畴为螟蜮所害，唯能悉除螟蜮，则稼之秀可实也。家蓄高货而盗入其门，主人操刃持梃，或杀或捕，则赀之厚可全也。人之身亦然。冒阴阳之气，辄遇疠疫，当得医者察声视色，按脉授药，使离诸腹心肝膈，然后其体可平。若不医之用，易异不除螟蜮而望稼穑之实，不驱盗贼而求家赀之全，决不可得。矧惟国家重医药之书，最为事要。[13p]

    Now, if one does not address the ailments within but solely seeks fortune from without, when the situation becomes dire, it is inappropriate to neglect the need for care and inquiry; would this not be a mistake? If crops in flourishing fields are damaged by pests, one must completely eliminate the pests for the crops to thrive. If a household has valuable possessions but thieves invade, the owner must wield weapons to either kill or capture the intruders to protect their wealth. The same is true for the human body: when confronted with the influences of yin and yang and facing illness, one should have a physician examine the sounds, look at the color, check the pulse, and prescribe medicine to address the internal organs, allowing the body to return to equilibrium. If one avoids seeking medical treatment and expects to reap the rewards without eliminating the pests, or seeks to preserve their wealth without driving away thieves, it is utterly unattainable. How much more so for the state, which values medical texts as the utmost priority.[13p]

    元朝编辑名方，颁布天下郡国，其间述时疫之状，实为纤悉。及庆历中范文正公建言，俾自京师以逮四方学医之人，皆聚而讲习，以精其术，其黜庸谬，救生灵，倬然为治道之助。而世俗罔识朝廷仁爱之意如此，而徒惑邪诞而夭性命，愚实悯之。今已戒医博士日与医之徒，考神农子仪扁鹊秦和之术，一会于岐伯俞柎之道，以正黜邪，以诚消妄，使可治之疾，不终害人，亦济民之一事也。而虑巴賨之俗，尚安故态，不知医效之神，倍祷淫祀之鬼，故刻词以告。[14p]

    During the Yuan Dynasty, the renowned editor Ming Fang issued guidelines for the imperial regions, detailing the conditions of epidemics in meticulous fashion. Later, during the Qingli period, the virtuous Minister Fan Wenzheng suggested that physicians from the capital and other regions gather for lectures and training to improve their skills, thereby eliminating errors and saving lives, which would truly assist in effective governance. However, the common people were unaware of the court's benevolent intentions and instead fell prey to false beliefs, endangering their lives, which was genuinely lamentable. Now, the doctors and scholars are instructed to study the methods of ancient figures like Shen Nong and Bian Que, and come together in the teachings of Qi Bo and Yu Shi to correct wrong practices and eliminate falsehoods, ensuring that treatable diseases do not harm people, thus contributing to the welfare of the populace. Yet, there is concern for the customs of the Ba Cong people, who remain attached to old ways and do not appreciate the miraculous effects of medicine, excessively praying to misguided deities; hence, inscriptions are made to convey this.[14p]

    The six qi and five elements are the essence of life; the three regions and nine pulses converge in harmony.     Their view of our people is indifferent, as if the richness of Qin and Yue are not connected to each other; and not only are they not connected, but they also extract the marrow and cut the fat without knowing when to stop. Who is as diligent in caring for the people's ailments as Zhang Jun? The doctors of the world, with their many engagements, are too rushed to be gracious, often leaving without belief even after a single night; some have no time to check pulses or prescribe remedies, and cannot be retained. Who among them is like Jia Jun, who cares and nurtures for years without growing weary? All these can be recorded. I am now taking up my pen to follow in the footsteps of writers, and if I do not write about this, where can I find material to write about! Nevertheless, the officials today are cruel and oppressive, relentlessly beating and striking; my people have suffered this for far too long! As I look around, where are the wise leaders? Between states and towns, is there anyone with the skill to apply the right measures to revive the people? If there are such, though I am slow-witted, I could still prepare ink and brush, writing extensively and yet not pen a single word about it. This serves as a preface.[27p]

    　　古之医师必通于三世之书。所谓三世者，一曰《针灸》，二曰《神农本草》，三曰《太素脉诀》。脉诀所以察证，本草所以辨药，针灸所以去疾。非是三者，不可以言医。故记礼者有云：医不三世，不服其药也。传经者，明载其说，复斥其非，而以父子相承三世为言，何其惑欤？夫医之为道，必志虑渊微，机颖明发，然后可与于斯，虽其父不能传其子也。吾乡有严生者，三世业医矣。其为医，专事乎大观之方，他皆愦愦，绝弗之省。又有朱聘君，家世习儒，至聘君始以医鸣，医家诸书，则无不精览。一少年病肺气上，喀喀鸣喉中，急则唾，唾血成缕。严曰：此瘵也，后三月死。聘君曰：非也。气升而腴，中失其枢，火官司令，烁金于炉，是之谓肺痿，治之坐。已而果生。一六十翁患寒热，初毛洒淅，齿击下上，热继之，盛如蒸甑。严曰：此痰也，不治将差。聘君曰：非也。脉淫以芤，数复乱息，外强中干，祸作福极，是之谓解(亻亦)。[28p]

    In ancient times, physicians had to be well-versed in the texts of the three generations. The so-called three generations are: first, the "Acupuncture and Moxibustion," second, the "Shennong's Materia Medica," and third, the "Great Fundamental Pulse Treatise." The pulse treatise is for observing evidence, the materia medica is for distinguishing drugs, and acupuncture and moxibustion are for eliminating diseases. Without these three, one cannot be called a physician. Therefore, it is said in the records of rituals: "A physician who does not understand the three generations will not be trusted with their medicine." The transmitters of the classics clearly state their doctrines and refute the fallacies, using the concept of father and son passing down through three generations as a reference. How perplexing that is! The art of medicine requires deep contemplation, keen insight, and clarity of thought before one can engage with it. Even a father may not necessarily pass down his knowledge to his son.

    In my hometown, there is a physician named Yan, who has been in the medical profession for three generations. His medical practice focuses solely on major treatments, ignoring all else entirely. There is also Mr. Zhu Ping, whose family has historically practiced Confucianism, but he first gained recognition as a physician, diligently studying medical texts. A young man was afflicted with lung issues, coughing and producing blood-streaked phlegm. Yan said, "This is a consumption; he will die in three months." Mr. Zhu disagreed, stating, "No, the qi is rising and becoming abundant, but the central axis is lost; the fire organ is in turmoil, burning like gold in a furnace. This is called lung atrophy; treatment must be administered." Afterward, the young man indeed recovered.

    An old man of sixty suffered from fevers and chills, initially experiencing a slight shiver, his teeth chattering, followed by severe heat, as intense as being steamed in a pot. Yan said, "This is phlegm; if untreated, it will worsen." Mr. Zhu contradicted him, claiming, "No, the pulse is excess and knotted, the rhythm is chaotic, with an exterior that seems strong but is internally weak; calamity emerges, yet fortune is found at the extreme. This is called the resolution."[28p]

    药之则瘳，不药则剧。已而果剧，治乃愈。一妇女有哕疾，每吐涎数升，腥触人，人近亦哕。严曰：此寒哕也，法宜温。聘君曰：非也。阴阳未平，气苞血聚，其势方格，靡有攸处，是之谓恶阻。在法不当治，久则自宁，且生男。言后辄验。夫严生之医三世矣，聘君则始习为之，而优劣若是者，医其可以世论否耶？嗟夫！昔之名医众矣，未暇多论。若华元化，若张嗣伯，若许智藏，其治证皆入神，初不闻其父子相传也。自传经惑于是非，使《礼经》之意，晦而不白三千年矣。世之索医者，不问其通书与否，见久于其业者，则瞀瞀焉从之。人问其故，则曰是记礼者云尔也，其可乎哉？葛生某，淮之巨族也，明于医，三世之书，皆尝习而通之，出而治疾，决死生，验差剧，若独照而龟卜无爽也者。士或不能具药，辄注之不索其偿，士君子翕然称誉之，名上丞相府，赐七品服，俾提举诸医官，有疾者，遂倚之以为命。呜呼！若葛生者，其无愧古之医师者欤！[29p]

    Medicine can cure, while without it, the condition worsens. Eventually, when it becomes severe, treatment can lead to recovery. A woman suffered from a cough and would expel several liters of phlegm, which smelled foul and affected anyone nearby. Yan stated that this was a cold-induced cough and recommended warmth. Pingjun disagreed. He noted that the balance of yin and yang was not achieved, resulting in stagnation of qi and blood, creating a situation with no resolution, which he termed a severe obstruction. If treated improperly, after a long time, the condition may stabilize but could still lead to the birth of a son. His statement proved true later on. Yan had been a physician for three generations, while Pingjun was just beginning, raising questions about the merit of their practices.

    Alas! There were many renowned physicians in the past, too numerous to mention. Among them were Hua Yuanhua, Zhang Sibao, and Xu Zhizang, whose treatments and results were remarkable, and one never heard of their familial lineage passing down. Yet, for three thousand years, the interpretations of medical texts have clouded the principles of the "Book of Rites," obscuring clarity. Those seeking medical help do not inquire about the profundity of the texts but follow practitioners they see persevering in their work, regardless of their expertise. When questioned, they say this is what the keepers of rituals suggest. Is this acceptable?

    Ge Sheng, from a prominent family in Huai, was skilled in medicine and had studied the writings of three generations, practicing and mastering them. He treated ailments effectively, determining life or death with results as accurate as if he were consulting an unerring oracle. Scholars who might be unable to adequately provide medicine would nonetheless dispense it without seeking compensation, and the virtuous gentlemen would universally commend him. He was awarded the seventh rank robe and appointed to oversee the medical officers, with people relying on him for their lives. Oh! Is Ge Sheng not one who bears no shame before the ancient physicians?[29p]

    　　　　赠医师贾某序 【前人】

    　　医之为道，难言久矣。然必审诊以起度量，立规矩，称权衡，合色脉，表里有余不足顺逆之法，复参其人之动静，与其息之相应，然后从而治之，则其事为甚不轻矣。非洞明应世群书之得失，尚可与于斯乎？《黄帝内经》，虽疑先秦之士依仿而托之，其言深，其旨邃以宏，其考辨信而有征，是当为医家之宗。下此则秦越人和缓，无书可传。越人所著《八十一难经》，则皆举《内经》之要而推言者也。又下此则淳于意、华佗之熊经鸱顾，固亦导引家之一术，至于刳腹背湔肠胃而去疾，则涉于神怪矣。意之医状，司马迁备志之。其所谓迥风杳风者，今人绝不知为何证，况复求于治疗之深旨乎？又下则张机。机之《金匮玉函经》及伤寒诸论，诚千古不刊之典。第详于六气所伤，而情欲食饮罢劳之所致者，略而弗议，兼之文字错简，亦未易以序次求之也。又下此则王叔和。[30p]

    Preface to the Gift for Physician Jia

    The way of medicine has been difficult to articulate for a long time. However, one must carefully diagnose to establish measurement, set standards, weigh and balance, match colors and pulses, and observe the excesses and deficiencies, as well as the harmonious and contrary states. This must be coupled with the patient's movements and their breathing, after which one can treat them; thus, the matter is indeed of great seriousness. If one does not have a deep understanding of the gains and losses in the multitude of written works, can one still engage in this?

    The "Huangdi Neijing," although suspected to have been emulated and attributed to figures of pre-Qin times, contains profound words and deep meanings, and its examinations are credible and well-evidenced, making it a foundational text for physicians. Below this, we have texts from the people of Qin and Yue, which are gentle, yet there are no books to pass down. The work "Bajiyi Nanjing" by the Yue people primarily expounds upon the essentials of the "Neijing." Further down, there are the teachings of Chunyu Yi and Hua Tuo, which are also part of the art of guiding. The practice of opening the abdomen to treat illnesses involves mystical elements. Sima Qian thoroughly documented the state of medical conditions, yet the so-called "strange wind" and "mysterious wind" are completely unknown to contemporary people, let alone searching for the profound meanings of treatment. Below this, we find Zhang Ji, whose "Jinkui Yuhan Jing" and discussions of typhoid diseases are indeed timeless classics. However, it primarily details injuries from the six qi and the resulting emotions, diet, and exertion, without thoroughly discussing these points, compounded by the difficulties in sequentially following the disordered and simplified text. Below him is Wang Shuhe.[30p]

    叔和纂岐伯、华佗等书为《脉经》，叙阴阳内外，辨三部九候，分人迎气口，条陈十二经络，洎夫三焦五脏六腑之病，最为着明。惜乎为妄男子括以肤陋之脉歌，遂使其本书不盛布于世也。又下此则巢元方。其《病源候论》似不为无所见者，但言风寒二湿，而不着湿热之文，乃其失也。又下此则王冰。冰推五运六气之变，撰为《天元玉策》，周详切密，亦人之所难，苟泥之则局滞而不通矣。又下此则王焘、孙思邈。[31p]

    Uncle He compiled the "Pulse Classic" with Qi Bo, Hua Tuo, and others, discussing the principles of yin and yang, distinguishing the three divisions and nine conditions, categorizing the human greeting and vital energy, and detailing the twelve meridians along with the ailments of the three jiaos, five organs, and six bowels, making it exceedingly clear. Unfortunately, it was trivialized by some superficial individuals into a simplistic pulse song, preventing the original work from being widely disseminated. Following him was Chao Yuanfang, whose "On the Source of Illnesses" appears not to have been unwitnessed, as it discusses wind, cold, and two types of dampness, but neglects the topic of damp-heat, which is a flaw. Next was Wang Bing, who advanced the understanding of the changes in the five movements and six atmospheres, compiling the "Heavenly Origin Jade Strategy," which is comprehensive and meticulous, yet difficult for people; should one cling too rigidly to it, they would become stagnant and disconnected. Following them were Wang Tao and Sun Simiao.[31p]

    思邈以绝人之识，操慈仁恻厚之心，其列《千金方》、《翼》及工害人之祸，至为愤切，后人稍闯其藩垣，亦足以其术鸣，但不知伤寒之数，或弗能无遗憾也。焘虽暗劣，《外台秘要》所言方证符禁灼灸之详，颇有所袓述，然谓针能杀生人而不能起死人者，则一偏之见也。又下此则钱乙、庞安时、许叔微。叔微在准绳尺寸之中而无所发明，安时虽能出奇应变而终未离于范围，二人皆得机之粗者也。惟乙深造机之阃奥，而撷其精华，建为五脏之方，各随所宜。[32p]

    Si Miao possessed exceptional knowledge and a compassionate heart. His works, such as the "Thousand Gold Prescriptions" and "Wings," include details on causes of harm to humans, which are expressed with deep fervor. Although later generations have ventured into his teachings, they may still leave gaps in their understanding of pathogenic fevers, leading to regrets.

    Though Tao was lacking in skill, the "Secret Essentials of the Outer Archives" contained detailed discussions about the regulations of cautery and acupuncture, reflecting some ancestral teachings. However, the view that acupuncture can save the living but cannot revive the dead is somewhat biased.

    Following him were Qian Yi, Pang Anshi, and Xu Shuwei. Xu Shuwei operated within set limits without making significant discoveries, while Anshi, although capable of remarkable adaptations, ultimately did not stray from established parameters; both men grasped the basics. Only Yi deeply explored the profound principles of acupuncture, extracting its essence and creating methods tailored for the five organs according to their specific needs.[32p]

    肝有相火则有泻而无补，肾为真水则有补而无泻，皆启《内经》之秘，尤知者之所取法。世概以婴孺医目之，何其知乙之浅哉？其遗书散亡，出于阎孝忠所集者，多孝忠之意，初非乙之本真也。又下此则上谷张元素、河间刘完素、睢水张从正。元素之与完素，虽设为奇梦异人以神其授受，实闻乙之风而兴起然者。若从正则又宗夫完素者也。元素以古方新病，决不能相值，治疾一切不以方，故其书亦不传。其存于今者，皆后来之所傅会。其学则东垣、李杲深得之。杲推明内外二伤，而多注意于补脾土之说。盖以土为一身之主，土平则诸脏平矣。从正以吐汗下三法，风寒暑湿火燥六门，为医之关键，其治多攻，其剂多峻厉，不善学者杀人。完素论风火之病，以《内经》病机气宜十九条，若为《原病式》，简奥粹微，有非大观局诸医所可仿佛。究其设施，则亦不越补攻二者之间也。[33p]

    If the liver has excessive fire, it leads to leakage without nourishment; the kidneys, being true water, provide nourishment without leakage. This reveals the secrets of the "Inner Canon," an understanding particularly important for those who know how to apply it. The world generally perceives it through the lens of infantile medicine, showing how shallow their understanding is. The writings that have been lost and collected by Yan Xiaozhong are largely influenced by his perspective and are not the original truth of the teachings. Below him are Zhang Yuanshu of Shanggu, Liu Wansheng of Hejian, and Zhang Congzheng of Suishui. Although Yuanshu and Wansheng set up unusual dreams and extraordinary figures to enhance their teachings, they were indeed inspired by the principles of the original teachings. Zhang Congzheng, on the other hand, is primarily a follower of Wansheng.

    Yuanshu’s methods, which combined ancient formulas with new ailments, could not align with the past; he did not treat diseases according to formulas, which is why his writings did not circulate. What remains today has largely been shaped by later interpretations. The understanding of these principles has been deeply grasped by Dong Yuan and Li Gao. Gao clarified the internal and external injuries, placing significant emphasis on the importance of nourishing the spleen. He viewed the spleen as the central organ of the body, asserting that when it is balanced, all organs are balanced as well. Zhang Congzheng advocated for three methods involving vomiting, sweating, and purging for treatment, considering six critical factors of wind, cold, heat, damp, fire, and dryness—key aspects of medical practice. His treatments were often aggressive, and the remedies harsh; those unskilled in these methods risked causing death. Wansheng discussed diseases caused by wind and fire using the nineteen conditions from the "Inner Canon," which served as the basis for "Original Disease Patterns." This was concise and profound, difficult for the majority of practitioners to emulate. Upon examining how these methods were established, they mostly revolved around the dual principles of replenishment and aggression.[33p]

    嗟乎！自有《内经》以来，医书之藏有司者，凡一百七十九家，二百九部，二千二百五十九卷，亦不为不多也。他未遑深论，即今所论者言之，世之医师，果能尽心于斯否乎？脱或未尽心于斯，则夫起度量、立规矩、称权衡、合色脉之属，焉能察而行之？不至以人命为戏也几希矣。虽然，亦有要焉，逆与顺之谓也。曰升降、曰浮沉，吾则顺之；曰温凉、曰寒热，吾则逆之。果能此道矣，则去夫先医之所治，虽不中，不远矣。[34p]

    Alas! Since the existence of the "Inner Canon," there have been a total of one hundred seventy-nine physicians' texts archived by the authorities, amounting to two hundred ninety works and two thousand two hundred fifty-nine volumes; this is indeed not a small number. He who has not yet had time for an in-depth discussion, I will address what is currently being discussed: Do the physicians of this world truly apply themselves fully to this? If they have not done so, how can they measure, establish standards, weigh, and assess pulse and color? It is nearly unimaginable that they would treat human life so lightly. Nevertheless, there is something essential here regarding the concepts of opposition and compliance. That which is called rising and falling, floating and sinking, I will follow; that which is called warming and cooling, cold and heat, I will oppose. If one can truly adhere to this principle, then even if one does not treat in the manner of the ancients, they will not stray far from the truth.[34p]

    然又未易以一蹴至也。非求之极博而观其会通，安可遽反于至约之域乎？医之道所以难言者，盖若此而已。乌伤？贾思诚，濂外弟也，性醇介，有君子之行，尝同濂师事城南闻先生，学治经。久之，思诚复去受医说于彦修朱先生之门。诸儒家所著，无所不窥，出而治疾，往往有奇验。荐绅间多为赋诗，而属濂以序。濂非知医者，将何以为思诚告哉？而思诚请之不倦，因为直疏历世群书之得失，而勖思诚以学者如此。初不暇，如他日作者簸弄笔舌，交错以成文也。
    　　　　论医 【明　王袆】[35p]

    Yet it is not easy to achieve in one fell swoop. If one does not seek to understand the vastness and interconnections, how can one suddenly return to the realm of simplicity? The difficulty in speaking of medicine lies precisely here. Wu Shang? Jia Sicong, the disciple of Lian, is of inherently gentle and upright nature, possessing the conduct of a gentleman. He once studied with Master Lian under the guidance of Mr. Wen from the southern city, learning to interpret the classics. After some time, Sicong left to pursue medical teachings at the door of Mr. Yanxiu Zhu. He explored all that was written by the Confucian scholars, and upon venturing out to treat ailments, often achieved remarkable results. Among the gentry, he frequently composed poetry and asked Lian for prefaces. If Lian was not knowledgeable about medicine, how could he be informed of Sicong’s achievements? Yet Sicong, tirelessly seeking assistance, straightforwardly discussed the successes and failures of ancient texts, encouraging Sicong to study in this manner. Initially, there was no time, like on other days when writers skillfully wield their pens and tongues to create texts.[35p]

    　　予观近时言医者，莫盛于吴中。而吴中世业医者，莫盛于葛氏。葛氏之医，其术善于推五运六气之盛衰，以审病证而定治法。此仲正之术，所以异于人人也。盖仲正之诸父曰恒斋者，尝自著书，其说以谓医当视时之盛衰而为损益。刘守真、张子和辈，值金人强盛，民悍气刚，故多用宣泄之法。及其衰也，兵革之余，饥馑相仍，民劳志困，故张洁古、李明之辈，多加补益之功。至宋之纪年，医者大抵务守护气元而不知攻伐之机，能养病而不能治病，失在不知通其变也。其说如此，可谓能明夫气运之变，而通于阴阳之化者矣。
    　　　　明医箴 【明　龚信】

    　　今之明医，心存仁义。博览群书，精通道艺。洞晓阴阳，明知运气。药辨温凉，脉分表里。治用补泻，病审虚实。因病制方，对证投剂。妙法在心，活变不滞。不衒虚名，惟期博济。不计其功，不谋其利。不论贫富，药施一例。起死回生，恩同天地。如此明医，芳垂万世。[36p]

    I have observed that in recent times, no place has praised physicians more than Wu region. Among the physicians of Wu, none is more renowned than the Ge family. The medical practice of the Ge family excels in discerning the rise and fall of the five movements and six qi, using this to analyze illness and determine treatment methods. This is the art of Zhongzheng, which sets him apart from others.

    Indeed, one of Zhongzheng's uncles, known as Hengzhai, once authored a book, stating that medicine should consider the fluctuations of time to determine what is to be added or diminished. Liu Shouzheng, Zhang Zihe, and others practiced their art during a time of strength for the Jurchens, when the people were robust and fierce, and thus often employed methods of release. However, as times became weak, with the aftermath of war and famine causing hardship, Zhang Jiegu, Li Ming and their peers tended to focus more on restoration and nourishment.

    By the time of the Song dynasty, physicians generally aimed to preserve vital qi but did not comprehend the necessary offense. They could nurture diseases but not cure them, failing to understand the transformations involved. Such discussions illustrate an understanding of the changes in qi movements and the implications of yin and yang transformations.

    As for modern physicians, they are filled with benevolence and righteousness. They have extensively studied various texts and are proficient in the Taoist arts. They are well-versed in the principles of yin and yang, and have a clear understanding of qi dynamics. They differentiate medicines by their warming or cooling effects and assess the pulse for superficial or interior conditions. Their treatment employs supplements or purges based on a careful examination of the illness's nature—whether it is deficiency or excess. They tailor their prescriptions according to the illness and administer remedies accordingly. Their clever techniques are instinctive, adapting fluidly without rigidity. They do not seek to enhance their reputations but aim merely to benefit others. They do not calculate personal gain or profit, and regardless of wealth or poverty, they apply the same remedies. Their ability to bring the dead back to life is as profound as the heavens and earth. Such enlightened physicians will have their names celebrated for generations to come.[36p]

    Admonitions for Inferior Physicians

    Today's inferior physicians flaunt their oddities, lacking knowledge of classical texts and understanding of characters. They self-promote to deceive the world, rushing to people's doors without invitation. They present banal remedies and ask for ailments superficially. They boast of their abilities, indulging in flattery. Patients do not examine their issues, treating their ailments vaguely. They do not investigate the origins of their illnesses or distinguish between true and false conditions. They disregard life and death, acting recklessly in the moment. Suddenly, illnesses worsen and they hastily disperse. They endanger lives for minimal profit. Such inferior physicians are shameful and to be feared.

    Admonitions for Patients

    Today's patients are often stingy with their expenditures. They refuse urgent treatment, waiting for self-recovery. They do not seek the wise but hope for the easy solution. They overlook their conditions, carelessly trying various remedies. Some even invoke spirits, which leads to nothing worthwhile. Stepping on frost without caution invites the hard ice. By the time they seek skilled physicians, their illnesses have become severe. Even if there are miraculous elixirs, they are hard to cure. Their ignorance and delay result from their own actions. They only resign to fate, thinking luck will improve. Such confusion is truly lamentable. These patients must rectify this folly.

    Warnings for Physicians

    The weight of human life is immense, and the role of a physician is the most challenging. The source of illness must be thoroughly investigated, and medicines must be prescribed with care. One should achieve complete treatment and not linger over years. Life and death are crucial matters known only to heaven. Compassionate colleagues should think deeply about their duty to help the world.[37p]

    The Original Path of Medicine

    Great is medicine! Its origins are far-reaching! Since the chaos was separated and the wilderness began to take shape, the light and clear aspects of yang rose to form heaven, while the heavy and turbid aspects of yin descended to form earth. Heaven flourished and took its place above, while earth sank and took its place below. Thus, the essence of yang became the sun, rising in the east and setting in the west; the essence of yin became the moon, visible at night and hidden during the day. The dual principles stood firm, and the two luminaries moved in their courses. At this point, the primal energy condensed in the void, and water began to emerge; the red energy radiated through the void, and fire began to emerge; the azure energy floated in the void, and wood began to emerge; the white energy traversed the void, and metal began to emerge; the black energy met in the void, and earth began to emerge. With the five elements complete, myriad things were born, establishing the way of the three origins. Therefore, in human life, one must obtain the righteous energy of heaven and earth. The head is round, resembling heaven; the feet are square, resembling earth. Heaven has yin and yang, and humans have qi and blood. Heaven has five elements, and humans have five organs. The people of the divine Tian clan lived in nests in caves, subsisting on raw flesh and drinking blood. They moved to avoid the cold and took refuge in shadows to escape the heat. When the great simplicity had not yet opened, what ailments could there be? Later, Fuxi observed the heavens and the energy, and created hexagrams. In later generations, there emerged the "Heavenly Origin Jade Book," regarded as Fuxi's text, which was recited and passed down orally for ten generations. Shennong tasted all the herbs, experiencing seventy poisons in a single day; thereafter, the study of herbal medicine flourished.[38p]

    黄帝垂衣裳而天下治，与岐伯天师更相问难，上推天文，下穷地理，中拯民瘼，《内经》自此而作矣。此经既作，民之有疾，必假砭针以治其外，汤液以疗其内。厥后大朴散而风化开，民务繁而欲心纵，灾沴多端，非大毒、小毒、常毒、无毒之药，弗能蠲矣。医之大原，《素问》一书而已矣。二十四卷，八十一篇，其间推原运气之加临，阐明经络之标本，论病必归其要，处治各得其宜，井然而有条，灿然而不紊。若天元纪大论、六元正纪大论、五常政大论、气交变大论、至真要大论数篇，乃至精至微之妙道，诚万世释缚脱难，全真导气，拯黎元于仁寿，济羸劣以获安者之大典也。轩岐以下，代不乏人。扁鹊得其一二，演而述《难经》；皇甫士安次而为《甲乙》；杨上善纂而为《太素》。如金元起之解，启元子之注，所谓源洁则流清，表端则影正，历代之明医也。[39p]

    The Yellow Emperor donned his robes and ruled the world, engaging in dialogue with Qi Bo, the Heavenly Master, to discuss profound questions. They explored celestial phenomena above and the geography below, aiming to alleviate the suffering of the people. Thus, the "Inner Canon" was created. Once this text was established, when people fell ill, they would necessarily rely on acupuncture to treat external ailments and decoctions for internal healing. Subsequently, as simplicity vanished and desires grew rampant among the populace, disasters multiplied, and only with potent, minor, chronic, and non-toxic medicines could relief be achieved. The essence of medicine lies within the book "Suwen." It comprises twenty-four volumes and eighty-one chapters, detailing the principles of vital energy, clarifying the fundamentals of meridians, discussing diseases to address their root causes, and prescribing appropriate treatments—all systematically arranged and presented with clarity and coherence.

    Works such as the "Great Treatise on the Celestial Origin," "Great Treatise on the Six Origins," "Great Treatise on the Five Constants," and the "Great Treatise on the Changes of Qi," along with several chapters on intricate and subtle principles, truly represent a great legacy for ages, a complete path for liberation from bondage, guiding vital energy to save the common people, and aiding the weak to find peace. Below Xu and Qi, generations have found notable figures. Bian Que grasped a few principles and expounded on them in the "Nanjing"; Huangfu Shiyu followed and composed "Jia Yi"; Yang Shangshan compiled the "Tai Su." Just as the interpretations of Jin and Yuan revealed insights, and Master Qi's annotations clarified understanding, it is said that a pure source will yield a clear flow, and a well-structured display will cast a correct shadow—such has been the wisdom of renowned physicians through the ages.[39p]

    独有汉长沙太守张仲景者，揣本求源，采微索隐，取其大小奇偶之制，定君臣佐使之法，而作医方，表里虚实，真千载不传之秘，乃大贤亚圣之资，有继往开来之功也。汉唐以下，学者岂不欲涉其渊微之旨？矧《内经》之理深幽，无径可入。如巢元方之作《病源》书，孙思邈之作《千金方》，辞益繁而理愈昧，方弥广而法失真。《内经》之书，施用者鲜矣。及朱奉议宗长沙太守之论，编南阳活人之书，仲景训阴阳为表里，奉议解阴阳为寒热，差之毫厘，谬以千里，其活人也固多，其死人也不寡矣。幸而守真刘子《要旨论》、《原病式》二书既作，则《内经》之理，昭如日月之明；《直格书》、《宣明论》二书既作，则长沙之法，约如枢机之要。如改桂枝麻黄各半汤为双解散，变十枣汤为三花神佑丸，其有功于圣门也不浅矣。[40p]

    Only Zhang Zhongjing, the magistrate of Changsha during the Han dynasty, uniquely sought the root of medical knowledge, exploring subtle meanings, and establishing principles for regulation of rulers, ministers, and assistants. He created medical formulas, articulating the interplay of emptiness and fullness—a true secret not passed down for a millennium—demonstrating the qualities of a great sage and a second saint, with contributions that bridge past and future. Scholars since the Han and Tang dynasties have sought to delve into these profound insights. Moreover, the principles of the "Inner Canon" are deep and obscure, with no clear path for entry. Works like Chao Yuanfang’s "The Sources of Illness" and Sun Simiao’s "Thousand Golden Prescriptions" contain elaborate language, yet their principles become increasingly unclear; their prescriptions grow wider, straying from authenticity. Few have sincerely applied the teachings of the "Inner Canon."

    Upon Zhu Fengyi's discussion as the magistrate of Changsha, compiling the "Living Human Book" of Nanyang, Zhang Zhongjing taught that yin and yang should be interpreted as inner and outer, while Fengyi explained yin and yang in terms of cold and heat. A slight deviation can lead to significant errors; while they may have saved many lives, equally, many have died. Fortunately, Liu Zhi’s "Essential Discussions" and "Original Patterns of Illness" have been created, making the principles of the "Inner Canon" as clear as the sun and moon. With the creation of "Direct Rectification" and "Illuminating Discussions," the laws of Changsha have been condensed to essential principles. Transforming the Gui Zhi Ma Huang Decoction into the Double Resolving Powder and altering the Ten Jujube Decoction into the Three Flower Divine Support Pill demonstrates significant contributions to the sage’s teachings.[40p]

    同时有张子和者，出明《内经》之大道，续河间之正源，与麻知几讲学，而作《儒门事亲》之书，乃曰：吐中有汗，泻中有补，圣人止有三法，无第四法，乃不易之确论，至精之格言。于是有刘张之泒矣。若东垣老人，明《素问》之理，宗仲景之法，作《济生拔萃十书》以传于世，明脉取权衡规矩，用药体升降浮沉，是以有王道霸道譬焉。至于丹溪朱氏，伤寒内伤杂病无不精研，痰火奥义，尤其独得。宋太史濂谓其集医家之大成，诚哉是言也。迨及我朝，修《大观本草》，制《铜人俞穴针灸经》、《御赐医方》等书，设太医以辅圣躬，立良医以佐王府，惠民药局以济民间夭札，其仁天下之心，宛如轩岐一揆而远迈汉唐，是以明医迭出。如陶节庵之伤寒，发仲景之所未发；薛己之外科，补东垣之未备；葛可久之内伤，钱瑛之小儿，亦无忝于丹溪。昭代作人之功，其盛矣乎！后学知道统之自，则门径不差，而医道亦可近矣。故曰：知其要者，一言而终。[41p]

    At the same time, there were Zhang and others who expounded on the Great Way of the "Inner Canon," continuing the rightful source of Hejian and discussing learning with Ma Zhigui, also writing the book "Confucianism and Filial Piety." They claimed: "In vomiting, there is sweating; in diarrhea, there is replenishment. The sage has only three methods, with no fourth method." This is an unchangeable, definitive discourse and a profound maxim. Thus, there was the sorrow of Liu and Zhang.

    As for Dongyuan Lao-ren, he elucidated the principles of the "Suwen," adhering to the methods of Zhang Zhongjing, and authored "Ten Books on Rescuing Life and Extracting Essence" for the world, clarifying the pulse and the weights and measures of medications, as well as the rising, descending, floating, and sinking of drugs; hence, there are analogies of righteous and hegemonic methods.

    Regarding the Danxi Zhu family, they meticulously researched febrile diseases and internal injuries, with a particular focus on the profound implications of phlegm and fire, achieving remarkable insights. Song Taishi Lian remarked that their collection represents the pinnacle of medical achievement, which is indeed true.

    As for my dynasty, it revised the "Da Guan Materia Medica," created the "Bronze Man Acupuncture Classics," and the "Imperially Granted Medical Formulas," establishing the Taiyi Department to assist the sacred self, and appointing excellent physicians to assist the royal court, with the benevolent pharmacy to alleviate the sufferings of the people, demonstrating a heart of compassion akin to that of Xuanqi, surpassing the Han and Tang dynasties. Thus, esteemed physicians continued to emerge.

    For instance, Tao Jie'an's insights on febrile diseases disclosed matters not mentioned by Zhang Zhongjing; Xue Ji excelled in surgery, supplementing the gaps left by Dongyuan; Ge Kejiu's treatment of internal injuries, and Qian Ying's pediatric studies, were also not inferior to the Danxi legacy. The accomplishments of scholars in this age are indeed remarkable!

    If future students are aware of the essentials and follow them accordingly, the path will be accurate, and the medical doctrine will also be approachable. Therefore, it is said: "To know the essentials is to conclude with a single word."[41p]
