    By Cao Pi of the Three Kingdoms Period

    Emperor Huangdi was buried in Qiaoshan, but when the mountain collapsed, his body was nowhere to be found, leaving only his sword and shoes behind. Juyan 697.

    During the reign of Duke Mu of Qin, a man from Chen Cang dug up an unusual object; its shape did not resemble a dog or a sheep, and no one could name it. Some versions of Juyan suggest it looked like neither a sheep nor a pig. The man brought it to Duke Mu of Qin, and along the way, he encountered two children. The children said, "This is called an 'ao,' which Juyan refers to as 'yuan.' The note in Shuowen Jiezi explains that 'yuan' is pronounced 'ao' and lives underground, feeding on the brains of the dead. If you want to kill it, insert a pine branch into its head." This account is also found in Juyan 375 and 954. The 'ao' then said, "Those two children are named Chen Bao; whoever captures the male will become a king, and whoever captures the female will become a hegemon." The man from Chen Cang abandoned the 'ao' and chased the two children, who transformed into pheasants and flew into a flat forest. He reported this to Duke Mu of Qin, who then organized a large hunt and indeed captured the female. It transformed into a stone and was placed between the rivers Qian and Wei. By the time of Duke Wen of Qin, a shrine was built for it, named Chen Bao. The male flew south and settled in what is now the county of Zhi in Nanyang. This is also recorded in Guangji 461. The Qin state wanted to commemorate this omen, hence naming the county after it. Every time the Chen Cang shrine is celebrated, a red light over ten zhang long appears, coming from Zhi County and entering the Chen Bao shrine, accompanied by a sound like a rooster. This is also noted in Leiju 90, Juyan 917, and Shuchao 89. [1]

    During the reign of Duke Wen of Qin, a paulownia tree transformed into a cow. A rider attacked it, but the horse could not defeat it. The rider fell to the ground, and his hair came loose and fell out. The cow was frightened and entered the water. Therefore, the Qin state established horsemen with feathered headdresses and sent them as vanguard. According to the Hou Hanshu (Book of the Later Han) and other sources, the cow was so frightened that it entered the waters of the Feng River, and the Qin state subsequently built a temple called the "Temple of the Angry Ox."

    Gan Jiang and Mo Ye were swordsmiths who forged swords for the king of Chu. After three years, they completed the swords, which were renowned for their exceptional quality. The swords were divided into male and female, and the male sword was kept by Mo Ye while the female sword was given to the king. Mo Ye told his wife, "I have hidden the male sword in the north side of the South Mountain, on the south side of the North Mountain. It is embedded in a pine tree growing on a stone. If the king discovers it and kills me, if you give birth to a son, tell him." When the king eventually discovered the sword and killed Mo Ye, his wife later gave birth to a son named Chibei, who was told about the sword. Chibei chopped down a pine tree in the South Mountain but did not find the sword. He suddenly found it inside a house pillar. The king of Chu dreamed of a man with a three-inch-wide forehead who said he wanted to take revenge. The search was very urgent, and the man escaped to the Zhuxing Mountain. He met a guest who wanted to help him take revenge. The man cut off his head and offered it to the king. The guest ordered the head to be cooked in a large pot, but it did not rot for three days and three nights. The king went to see it, and the guest leaned and struck the king with the male sword, causing the king's head to fall into the pot. The guest then took his own life. The three heads were all cooked and could not be distinguished, so they were buried separately and named the "Tomb of the Three Kings." [3]
