    The Book of Strange Things
    By Cao Pi of the Three Kingdoms Period

    Emperor Huangdi was buried in Qiaoshan, but when the mountain collapsed, his body was nowhere to be found, leaving only his sword and shoes behind. Juyan 697.

    During the reign of Duke Mu of Qin, a man from Chen Cang dug up an unusual object; its shape did not resemble a dog or a sheep, and no one could name it. Some versions of Juyan suggest it looked like neither a sheep nor a pig. The man brought it to Duke Mu of Qin, and along the way, he encountered two children. The children said, "This is called an 'ao,' which Juyan refers to as 'yuan.' The note in Shuowen Jiezi explains that 'yuan' is pronounced 'ao' and lives underground, feeding on the brains of the dead. If you want to kill it, insert a pine branch into its head." This account is also found in Juyan 375 and 954. The 'ao' then said, "Those two children are named Chen Bao; whoever captures the male will become a king, and whoever captures the female will become a hegemon." The man from Chen Cang abandoned the 'ao' and chased the two children, who transformed into pheasants and flew into a flat forest. He reported this to Duke Mu of Qin, who then organized a large hunt and indeed captured the female. It transformed into a stone and was placed between the rivers Qian and Wei. By the time of Duke Wen of Qin, a shrine was built for it, named Chen Bao. The male flew south and settled in what is now the county of Zhi in Nanyang. This is also recorded in Guangji 461. The Qin state wanted to commemorate this omen, hence naming the county after it. Every time the Chen Cang shrine is celebrated, a red light over ten zhang long appears, coming from Zhi County and entering the Chen Bao shrine, accompanied by a sound like a rooster. This is also noted in Leiju 90, Juyan 917, and Shuchao 89.

On the way, they met two children who said, "This is called媦, and it feeds on the brains of the dead below." The spirit then said, "Those two children are named Chen Bao; whoever captures the male will become a king, and whoever captures the female will become a hegemon." They chased the children, who then transformed into pheasants. Duke Mu of Qin conducted a great hunt and indeed captured the female pheasant, so he built a shrine for it. During the worship, there were flashes of light, thunder, and lightning. The male pheasant settled in Nanyang, and a red light several tens of feet long came into the Chen Cang shrine.

    In the ancient county of Gudao in Wudu, there is a shrine to a fierce bull, which is said to have originally been a great mulberry tree in the south mountain. In the 27th year of Duke Wen of Qin's reign, they attacked it, and the wound on the tree healed immediately. Duke Wen of Qin then sent forty men with axes to chop it down, but it did not break. One tired soldier, who injured his foot and could not leave, lay under the tree. He heard the ghosts talking to each other: "Are you tired of attacking?" One said, "We are very tired." Another said, "Duke Wen of Qin will surely keep attacking." The other replied, "What can he do to me?" The first one said, "What about covering it with red ashes?" Then they remained silent. The soldier reported this, and Duke Wen of Qin ordered the soldiers to wear red clothes, follow where they cut, and cover the tree with ashes. It broke off and transformed into a cow, entering the water. So Qin built a shrine for it. Commentary on the Wei River chapter of the Waterways Classic (水經渭水篇注).

    During the reign of Duke Wen of Qin, a paulownia tree transformed into a cow. A rider attacked it, but the horse could not defeat it. The rider fell to the ground, and his hair came loose and fell out. The cow was frightened and entered the water. Therefore, the Qin state established horsemen with feathered headdresses and sent them as vanguard. According to the Hou Hanshu (Book of the Later Han) and other sources, the cow was so frightened that it entered the waters of the Feng River, and the Qin state subsequently built a temple called the "Temple of the Angry Ox."

    Gan Jiang and Mo Ye were swordsmiths who forged swords for the king of Chu. After three years, they completed the swords, which were renowned for their exceptional quality. The swords were divided into male and female, and the male sword was kept by Mo Ye while the female sword was given to the king. Mo Ye told his wife, "I have hidden the male sword in the north side of the South Mountain, on the south side of the North Mountain. It is embedded in a pine tree growing on a stone. If the king discovers it and kills me, if you give birth to a son, tell him." When the king eventually discovered the sword and killed Mo Ye, his wife later gave birth to a son named Chibei, who was told about the sword. Chibei chopped down a pine tree in the South Mountain but did not find the sword. He suddenly found it inside a house pillar. The king of Chu dreamed of a man with a three-inch-wide forehead who said he wanted to take revenge. The search was very urgent, and the man escaped to the Zhuxing Mountain. He met a guest who wanted to help him take revenge. The man cut off his head and offered it to the king. The guest ordered the head to be cooked in a large pot, but it did not rot for three days and three nights. The king went to see it, and the guest leaned and struck the king with the male sword, causing the king's head to fall into the pot. The guest then took his own life. The three heads were all cooked and could not be distinguished, so they were buried separately and named the "Tomb of the Three Kings."

Wei Wuji was furious at the hawk's attack and ordered the capture of hawks throughout the country, resulting in the capture of over two hundred.     Lu Shaoqian obtained a talisman from a celestial being. The youngest daughter of the King of Chu was afflicted by a demon and requested Lu Shaoqian's help. Lu Shaoqian stopped to rest a few miles away from the palace. At night, a carriage covered with a turtle shell and accompanied by thousands of horsemen arrived. The driver introduced himself as Bo Jing and awaited Lu Shaoqian. He invited Lu Shaoqian to drink several cups of wine and enjoy several tables of food. Before parting, Bo Jing said, "The illness of the King of Chu's daughter was caused by me. If you can help her, I will repay you with twenty million coins." Lu Shaoqian accepted the money and returned to the palace by another route. He treated the princess, and before her room, someone pushed the door open and said, "Shaoqian deceived your father!" A strong wind then blew toward the northwest, and when they looked, there was blood in a basin. The princess then lost consciousness but revived at midnight. The king sent someone to follow the wind and found a dead snake several meters long with thousands of smaller snakes lying dead beside it in the northwest of the city. Later, the imperial edict ordered the counties to investigate the incident, and it was found that the Grand Farmer had lost twenty million coins, and the Imperial Kitchen had lost several tables of food. Lu Shaoqian submitted a report with all the details, and the emperor was amazed. Guangji 456.

    During the reign of Emperor Wen (甘露 period), Gong Sun Da served as the magistrate of Chen County and died in office. As the preparations for his burial were underway, his children and dozens of local officials attended the funeral. Gong Sun Da had a five-year-old son who suddenly began speaking as if possessed by a spirit, his voice sounding like his father's. He scolded the crowd: "The mourning cry has already been mentioned in the first twelve characters of the Yuelu (a collection of notes and anecdotes). I want to say something." He then called his children and taught them in turn. The children were overwhelmed with grief and unable to bear it, so he comforted them by saying, "The cycle of the four seasons still has an end; human life is short and fragile, but there is no limit." He spoke thousands of words like this, all of which formed coherent statements. The child then asked, "When people die, they are all unaware. Only you, my father, are exceptionally wise and unique, so do you alone have a spirit?" He replied, "The matter of life and death is not easily explained; the affairs of ghosts and spirits are beyond human knowledge." He asked for paper to write and filled it with meaningful words. He threw the paper on the ground and said, "Seal this letter and send it to the Lord of Wei. If there is a letter from him, deliver it to him." Indeed, a letter came from the Lord of Wei. (This story is recorded in Yuelu 884 and Guangji 316.)

    In Han Zhong there was a ghostly deity known as Luan Hou, who often dwelled on the ceiling and delighted in eating preserved fish. As cited in Shu Chao, volume 146, it is said that the Han River deity resided on the ceiling, enjoyed eating preserved vegetables, and could foretell auspicious or inauspicious events. During the Ganlu era, a great locust plague arose; wherever the locusts passed, the crops were entirely destroyed. The governor sent an envoy to inform Luan Hou, offering preserved fish as a sacrifice. The deity said to the official, "Locusts are a minor matter; I shall deal with them at once." After he spoke, the locusts suddenly flew out. The official vaguely recalled that the creatures resembled crows and made a sound like that of a waterfowl. When the official returned, he gave a full report to the governor. Indeed, a countless number of birds came to feast on the locusts, and in a short time, they were all devoured. Guangji, volume 292.

    Xianyu Ji of Xi He served as the governor of Qinghe during the Jianwu era. He claimed to have spent six million coins to build a house, but before it was completed, he died. Zhao Gao took his place, and upon calculating the costs, found that only two million coins had been spent. Wu Guan Huang Bing and Gongcao Liu Shang claimed that the excess funds had been taken by Ji himself, and so they submitted a petition to confiscate Ji’s land, property, and servants, sending his wife and children to Ri Nan. Soon after, the ghost of Ji entered the government office during the day and, together with Shang and Bing, reviewed the accounts, determining that the remaining two million coins had been hidden by Shang and his associates. Ji then submitted a petition to the emperor, requesting that the matter be referred to Shang, and the emperor ordered the return of Ji’s land and property. Yulian, volume 836.
