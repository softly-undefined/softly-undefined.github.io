    Once upon a time, there was a man named Fan Liao, who was known for his utmost filial piety. His close relatives had passed away early, and he took care of his stepmother after his father remarried. The stepmother fell ill with a severe swelling that internally formed an abscess, causing unbearable pain and preventing her from sleeping day or night. Fan Liao became deeply distressed, and over the course of more than a month, his body became extremely thin and weak, to the point where people could hardly recognize him. He wanted to call a healer to perform acupuncture, but fearing the pain it might cause his stepmother, he instead suckled on the swelling on her body with his mouth, and she immediately felt slightly better. The pus continuously flowed out of the wound, and his stepmother was finally able to sleep peacefully at night.

    One night, she had a dream in which a ghostly child spoke to her: "If the wound is fed by a carp, you will recover and your life will be extended. If no carp is eaten, then death will ensue." Upon hearing this, Fan Liao was filled with worry and fear. He looked up to heaven and sighed, "My lack of filial piety has led to this. In the depths of winter, when ice is frozen, how can I get this fish to eat?" He embraced his stepmother's head and parted from her, crying as he went in and out, shedding tears of sorrow. He looked up to heaven and sighed again, "If heaven pities me, may the fish be moved to appear. I beg of the spirits to help."

    Fan Liao then removed his clothes and lay on the ice, but no fish appeared. He lay naked on the ice, determined to fulfill his plea. The heavens, moved by his filial piety, caused two carps to appear beneath his back. Joy arose in his heart, and he brought the carps back to feed his stepmother. He fed them to the wound, and she immediately recovered completely. Her life became long, and she lived a long time, eventually reaching the age of 110 before passing away. Fan Liao's filial piety was as unchanging as the pine and cypress trees, a testament to his enduring virtue.

Upon hearing this, Zhang Song hurriedly ran to the ground to look for purslane, but found none. He then burst into tears and said, "Alas, my parents, who bore me and toiled for me! Zhang Song grew up to be an adult, and his mother died of her illness. His family became wealthy. The coffins and tombs he made were all done by his own hands, without using the labor of servants.
