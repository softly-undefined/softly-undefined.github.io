    The Strange and the Marvelous
    By Zǔ Tái Zhī, Jin Dynasty

    Emperor Wu of Han was hosting a banquet with his ministers in the Weiyang Palace when he suddenly heard a voice say, "An old minister risks his life to speak." Looking up, he saw an elderly man, about eight or nine inches tall, leaning on a staff and hobbling, extremely aged. The man descended the column, put down his staff, bowed deeply, and remained silent. He then looked up at the palace hall and pointed down at the emperor's feet, and suddenly vanished. Dongfang Shuo said, "His name is 'Algae Dweller,' and he embodies the essence of both water and wood. In spring, he nests in deep forests, and in winter, he hides in deep rivers. Now that palaces and halls are being built, his dwelling is being cut down, so he has come to complain to the emperor. He said, looking up at the palace, 'The palace is named Unending, and I am complaining that Your Majesty is still invading my dwelling, which is unending.' Pointing at the emperor's feet, he said, 'Enough,' and wished that the emperor's palaces and halls be sufficient here, and not to build more." The emperor halted the construction of the palaces and sleeping quarters. After a short time, the emperor personally visited the river capital and heard the sound of music and singing underwater, as well as the presence of good herbs. [1]

    During the Jian'an era, the wife of Liu Zhao, the governor of Hejian, died in the governor's mansion. Afterward, when the new governor arrived, he dreamed of a beautiful woman who became his household; she gave him a pair of golden objects, which the governor could not identify. The woman then said, "This is ○ (pronounced as 'zhuì')." ○ refers to an object shaped like a pearl, as large as a finger, and flexible according to one's will. The governor placed it in his pillow. When the previous governor came to attend the funeral, he mentioned the ○, and upon opening the coffin, he found that the woman's arm indeed no longer had the ○. This account is recorded in the Wuying Palace Collection, volume 718.

    Before the state of Wu fell, there was often a purple-red aura seen between the constellations of Ox and Dipper. Astronomers and skilled diviners all feared that Wu was rising; only Zhang Xian, who was particularly skilled in astronomy, knew it was the aura of a divine sword, not a good omen for the south. This is recorded in the Wuying Palace Collection, volume 6.

    When Tao, the Taiview, was in humble circumstances, he was preparing to bury his father. Being poor, he did it himself; he had a spotted cow, which he had already loaded, but it suddenly disappeared, so he went to look for it. Suddenly, he met an old man on the road who asked, "What are you looking for?" Tao replied in detail. The old man then pointed and said, "I saw a cow lying in a field on a hill, it must be your cow. The place where this cow is lying is a good spot to build a tomb and bury your father, which will bring great fortune; even if it is slightly less, it will still bring you to the highest rank of an official, and your descendants will be governors of major regions." He then pointed to another mountain and said, "This mountain is also good, but not as good as the previous one, but it will still produce a prefectural governor." After speaking, the old man disappeared. Tao built the tomb there, and everything happened as the old man had said. This is recorded in the Wuying Palace Collection, volume 900. [3]

    A Clerk in Wu, named Sheng Chong (referred to as Zhong in some sources), was known for his filial piety. His mother, Lady Wang, had lost her sight. When Sheng Chong went on a brief trip, he ordered a servant to prepare food for his mother. The servant took a larva of a beetle, cooked it, and served it to Lady Wang, who found it very delicious but did not know what it was. Upon returning, Sheng Chong asked his mother about the food. She told him, "After you left, the servant brought me a dish that was extremely delicious; however, it was neither fish nor meat. You should ask her about it." Sheng Chong then questioned the servant, who admitted, "It was indeed a larva of a beetle!" Sheng Chong wept bitterly and embraced his mother, and she suddenly regained her sight. This story is recorded in Zhu Lin (49) and Yuelan (411 and 948).

    In Wu, there was a scholar-official who was on a temporary leave from the capital. On his way back, he reached a place called Qu'atai. There, he saw a beautiful woman and called her to come immediately. He then invited her to stay with him. The official removed a golden ornament from his arm and fastened it on her arm, ordering her to return. She did not come back. The next day, he sent someone to search for her, but no one could find her. Suddenly, as he passed by a pigpen, he saw a sow with a golden ornament on its arm. This story is recorded in Yuelan (930 and 717) and Shu Chao (135). [6]
