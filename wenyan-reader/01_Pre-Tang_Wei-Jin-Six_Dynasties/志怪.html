    　　汉武帝与近臣宴会于未央殿，忽闻人语云：“老臣冒死自陈。”乃见屋梁上有一翁，八九寸，拄杖偻步，笃老之极；缘柱而下，放杖稽首，默而不言；因仰首视殿屋，俯指帝脚，忽然不见。东方朔曰：“其名‘藻居’，兼水木之精，春巢幽林，冬潜深河。今造宫室，斩伐 其居，故来诉于帝。曰仰视宫殿，殿名未央，诉陛下方侵其居宅未央也；俯指陛下脚者，足也，愿陛下宫殿足于此，不愿更造也。”上为之息宫寝之役。居少时，帝亲幸河都，闻水底有弦歌之声，又有善芥。

    The Strange and the Marvelous
    By Zǔ Tái Zhī, Jin Dynasty

    Emperor Wu of Han was hosting a banquet with his ministers in the Weiyang Palace when he suddenly heard a voice say, "An old minister risks his life to speak." Looking up, he saw an elderly man, about eight or nine inches tall, leaning on a staff and hobbling, extremely aged. The man descended the column, put down his staff, bowed deeply, and remained silent. He then looked up at the palace hall and pointed down at the emperor's feet, and suddenly vanished. Dongfang Shuo said, "His name is 'Algae Dweller,' and he embodies the essence of both water and wood. In spring, he nests in deep forests, and in winter, he hides in deep rivers. Now that palaces and halls are being built, his dwelling is being cut down, so he has come to complain to the emperor. He said, looking up at the palace, 'The palace is named Unending, and I am complaining that Your Majesty is still invading my dwelling, which is unending.' Pointing at the emperor's feet, he said, 'Enough,' and wished that the emperor's palaces and halls be sufficient here, and not to build more." The emperor halted the construction of the palaces and sleeping quarters. After a short time, the emperor personally visited the river capital and heard the sound of music and singing underwater, as well as the presence of good herbs.

    Soon, an old man and a few young men appeared from the beams above, dressed in red robes and white sashes, adorned with ribbons and jade ornaments, looking very fresh and beautiful, emerging from the waves without their clothes becoming wet. The emperor asked, "Did you hear the music playing at the bottom of the water? Is it you?" The old man replied, "Your humble servant risked his life to appeal to Your Majesty, and fortunately, you granted us your great mercy, stopping the axes and allowing us to preserve our homes. We are overwhelmed with joy, so we privately celebrated." The emperor then presented a purple conch shell, shaped like butter. He said, "I am ignorant and unable to recognize you; only Eastern Sheng knows. Could you think of giving this to him?" The old man then ordered someone to retrieve something from the cave. One person accepted the command and dived into the spring's depths, returning quickly with a large pearl, a foot in diameter, radiant beyond compare. The emperor admired it greatly and asked Sima Qian, "How do you know this pearl is from the cave?" Sima Qian replied, "There are treasures in the caves at the bottom of the river." The emperor rewarded Sima Qian with fifty thousand coins and took the pearl. (From Shu Chao, Volume 158)

    During the Jian'an era, the wife of Liu Zhao, the governor of Hejian, died in the governor's mansion. Afterward, when the new governor arrived, he dreamed of a beautiful woman who became his household; she gave him a pair of golden objects, which the governor could not identify. The woman then said, "This is ○ (pronounced as 'zhuì')." ○ refers to an object shaped like a pearl, as large as a finger, and flexible according to one's will. The governor placed it in his pillow. When the previous governor came to attend the funeral, he mentioned the ○, and upon opening the coffin, he found that the woman's arm indeed no longer had the ○. This account is recorded in the Wuying Palace Collection, volume 718.

    Before the state of Wu fell, there was often a purple-red aura seen between the constellations of Ox and Dipper. Astronomers and skilled diviners all feared that Wu was rising; only Zhang Xian, who was particularly skilled in astronomy, knew it was the aura of a divine sword, not a good omen for the south. This is recorded in the Wuying Palace Collection, volume 6.

    When Tao, the Taiview, was in humble circumstances, he was preparing to bury his father. Being poor, he did it himself; he had a spotted cow, which he had already loaded, but it suddenly disappeared, so he went to look for it. Suddenly, he met an old man on the road who asked, "What are you looking for?" Tao replied in detail. The old man then pointed and said, "I saw a cow lying in a field on a hill, it must be your cow. The place where this cow is lying is a good spot to build a tomb and bury your father, which will bring great fortune; even if it is slightly less, it will still bring you to the highest rank of an official, and your descendants will be governors of major regions." He then pointed to another mountain and said, "This mountain is also good, but not as good as the previous one, but it will still produce a prefectural governor." After speaking, the old man disappeared. Tao built the tomb there, and everything happened as the old man had said. This is recorded in the Wuying Palace Collection, volume 900.

    In the Yixing Prefecture, there was a green dragon in the stream beneath the long bridge that was devouring people. Zhou Chu held a sword on the side of the bridge, waiting. After a long time, the dragon emerged. He then hung himself from the bridge, leapt onto the dragon's back, and stabbed it. The dragon was wounded several times, blood flowed throughout the stream, and it died from the prefectural stream to the Goupu of Taihu Lake. (Chuxue Ji, Volume 7)

    During the Yongjia period of Emperor Huai of Jin, Ding Zu from the commandery of Cao crossed the river to the Yinling area. At that time, it was dark and foggy. There was a shrine on the north side of the road. He saw something like a person, standing on its head, with blood dripping from its eyes, pooling on the ground in two places, each over a sheng (a unit of measure). Zu and his younger brother shouted at it together, and it disappeared. The blood pooled where it stood turned into thousands of fireflies, flying in all directions. (Yulüan, Volume 68)

    During the Long'an period, Chen Kui set up a fish net by the river. When the tide receded, he found a woman in the net. She was six chi tall, had a beautiful appearance, but no clothes. The water receded, and she could not move, lying in the sand; when spoken to, she did not respond. Someone dishonored her. Kui had a dream at night: "I am a river deity, I lost my way and fell into your net yesterday, and was subsequently mistreated. Now I will report to my superior deity to have you killed." Kui dared not move her, and when the tide came, it carried her away with the water. The man who had dishonored her soon fell ill. (Yulüan, Volume 68)

    A humble official from Jiankang named Cao Zhe encountered the Lady of Mount Lu. She provided him with a feast, and after eating the leftovers, birds pecked at the jars. The jars were intricately carved with strange and unnamed shapes. She then presented seven-tiered boxes and plates, which contained no ordinary food. The Lady of Mount Lu summoned her daughter, named Wan, to meet Cao Zhe. Wan was pleased upon seeing him and ordered her maid, named Qionglin, to fetch a zither. Wan played the zither and sang:

    "Ascending Mount Lu, its peaks are majestic and towering.
    Bathed in the warm breeze, the purple clouds caress me.
    Summoning you, I cleanse myself in the divine waters.
    Rejoicing in good fortune, I spread my wings among the clouds.
    Playing the zither, joy knows no bounds.
    Clouds and dragons unite in perfect harmony."

    After finishing her song, Wan took her leave.

    During the reign of the Lord of Mount Lu, Cao Zhe was welcomed and married to Wan. However, he felt uneasy and repeatedly asked to withdraw from the marriage. Wan secretly shed tears and composed a poem to express her farewell. She also gave him a finely woven undergarment as a gift.

    Translation

    A Clerk in Wu, named Sheng Chong (referred to as Zhong in some sources), was known for his filial piety. His mother, Lady Wang, had lost her sight. When Sheng Chong went on a brief trip, he ordered a servant to prepare food for his mother. The servant took a larva of a beetle, cooked it, and served it to Lady Wang, who found it very delicious but did not know what it was. Upon returning, Sheng Chong asked his mother about the food. She told him, "After you left, the servant brought me a dish that was extremely delicious; however, it was neither fish nor meat. You should ask her about it." Sheng Chong then questioned the servant, who admitted, "It was indeed a larva of a beetle!" Sheng Chong wept bitterly and embraced his mother, and she suddenly regained her sight. This story is recorded in Zhu Lin (49) and Yuelan (411 and 948).

    In Wu, there was a scholar-official who was on a temporary leave from the capital. On his way back, he reached a place called Qu'atai. There, he saw a beautiful woman and called her to come immediately. He then invited her to stay with him. The official removed a golden ornament from his arm and fastened it on her arm, ordering her to return. She did not come back. The next day, he sent someone to search for her, but no one could find her. Suddenly, as he passed by a pigpen, he saw a sow with a golden ornament on its arm. This story is recorded in Yuelan (930 and 717) and Shu Chao (135).

    The Chief Justice Xu Yuanli was holding a wedding for his daughter. His cousin and maternal uncle, Kong Zhengyang, went together to Xu's house. On the way, they saw a small boy, naked, holding a small knife, about five or six inches long, sharpening it on a wall. He was talking to himself. Then he jumped onto a carriage and sat in the curved railing, repeatedly looking at the knife. When they arrived at Xu's house, in front of the mulberry tree, the boy jumped down again and sat in the ashes, sharpening the knife once more. By the time the sun was high, the new bride got into the carriage and saw the boy with the knife enter the room. He immediately stabbed her, and she fell to the ground. They carried her back, undressed her, and found that her abdomen was purple, about the size of a wine tray. She died shortly after. The ghost child went out, dancing with the knife, which had blood on it. He smeared the blood on the mulberry leaves, and soon the leaves caught fire and burned. (See Yuzhuan 345)

    Gou Xi, the governor of Yan Province, was five hundred miles away from the capital. Someone brought him a rare and precious delicacy and wanted to send it to his relatives and nobles in the capital, but he was worried that it would lose its freshness over the two-day journey. He offered a reward for a horse that could travel hundreds of miles in a day. Someone offered a horse and said, "This horse can travel a thousand miles in a day." Gou Xi then ordered a carriage and a skilled driver to prepare for the journey. The horse left early in the morning and arrived in the capital at noon. After picking up a reply, it returned to Yan Province, reaching the governor's residence by the first watch of the night. Gou Xi was amazed by the horse's speed and suspected that its bones must be different. He had the horse killed to examine it, but found no supernatural qualities. The only unusual thing was that its ribs were as thick as small bamboo, running from its head to its spine, hidden under the flesh, which is why no one noticed it before. (See Yuzhuan 900)

    Bao arrived at the northern tower on the ritual mound to spend the night. At the second drumbeat, a person wearing a yellow linen single-layered garment and a white cap appeared, accompanied by someone holding a torch up the stairs. Bao, frightened, hid inside the wall. Soon after, three maids entered the tent, sent to greet a woman, who then entered the tent with the person in the white cap. Before dawn, the person in the white cap always left first. This happened over four or five nights. Later, at dawn, the person in the white cap finally left (as noted in the Taiping Yulan). Bao then entered the tent and asked the serving woman, "Who was the person who left earlier?" The woman replied, "It was Tonglang; he is the tree east of the road near the temple." At the second drumbeat, Tonglang returned, and Bao seized him, binding him to the tower pillar. The next day, he examined him and found that he was shaped like a person, about three feet long. Bao had him caged and sent to the Chancellor. While crossing the river, halfway through, a storm arose; Tonglang was thrown into the water, and the storm then ceased. (Lüshi Chunqiu 88, Taiping Yulan 956)

    A woman from the Dongguo family in Shanyin, Kuaiji, had previously had an affair with a man from the county. This man returned to the east of the county on the Lingci Bridge; the woman went aboard his boat to meet him, and they spent the night together. She prepared a meal of scorpions for them. After eating, the woman took two scorpions ashore. When the boat returned to the village, someone encountered her and said that the woman had died. They went to check on her, and she had not yet been buried; when they lifted the blanket to look at her, they found that each of her hands held a scorpion. (Taiping Yulan 943)
