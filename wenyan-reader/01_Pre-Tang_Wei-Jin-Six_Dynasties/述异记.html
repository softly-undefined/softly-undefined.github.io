    The Strange Record (佚名)

    On Mount Lu, there is a valley known as Kangwang Valley, also referred to as North Ridge in the Shi Leifu Zhi. There is a city here called Zhaocun. Whenever it is about to rain, one can hear the sounds of drums, horns, flutes, and pipes from the mountain, which gradually approach the city, followed by heavy rain and dark clouds. The local people consider this a regular sign of impending rain. It is also recorded in the Shi Leifu Zhi that this was the city of King Kang of Zhou, who was fond of unusual things and traveled to famous mountains, not far away. The city often finds ancient artifacts, large tripods, and weapons like bows, crossbows, and gold, indicating that this is not a place inhabited by ordinary people. The mountain is called "Kangwang" and the city "Zhaocun," suggesting that these names have historical significance. (Shi Leifu Zhi 85)

    On Mount Lu, there are three stone bridges, each tens of meters long but less than a foot wide, and when looked down upon, they appear to have no bottom. During the Xiankang period, Governor Yu of Jiangzhou welcomed Wu Meng, and Wu Meng and his disciples visited the mountain and passed over these bridges. They saw an old man sitting under a ginkgo tree, holding a jade cup filled with sweet dew, which he gave to Wu Meng, who then distributed it to his disciples. They also saw a grand terrace with wide halls, jade palaces and golden rooms, dazzling with precious stones and brilliant colors. There were many precious jade and gold vessels, whose names were unknown. They saw several people talking with Wu Meng, as if they were old acquaintances, and they spent the whole day with the jade ointment. (Zhu Lin 31, Shi Leifu Zhi 41, 633) [1]

    Yudu County in Nankang extends westward along the river, and the Tang Huiyao (唐会要) records it as "crossing south of the river." Three miles from the county is a place called Mengkou, where there is a cave that looks like a stone chamber, known as the Mengkou Cave. An old tradition, as mentioned in the Leeguji (类聚) volume 90, tells of a divine chicken with a color resembling fine gold. This chicken would emerge from the cave, spread its wings, fly far, and make a long cry. When its sound was heard, it would immediately fly back into the cave. Thus, the stone was named the Golden Chicken Stone. Once, a farmer plowing the side of the mountain saw the chicken come out to play. A tall person then appeared, holding a slingshot and aiming at the chicken. The chicken, seeing the tall person, flew back into the cave. The pebble hit the top of the cave, and the pebble was about six feet in diameter, hanging down and covering the cave, but there was still a small gap that was not large enough for a person to pass through. Another time, a person traveling upstream by boat had not yet reached the cliff several miles away when a person in a yellow robe appeared, carrying two baskets of cucumbers. The person asked to be transported, and the boat owner agreed. The person in the yellow robe then asked for food, and the boat owner gave him a plate of food and wine. After finishing eating, the boat arrived at the base of the cliff. The boat owner then asked for cucumbers, but the person in the yellow robe refused and spat on the plate. The person then climbed up the cliff and entered the stone. Initially, the boat owner was very angry, but when he saw the person enter the stone, he realized the person was supernatural.
