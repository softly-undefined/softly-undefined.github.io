    The Zhongzhao Gushi, a work of two volumes, was compiled by Wei Chi Wo of the Southern Tang Dynasty. The details of Wei Chi Wo's background are not fully known, but the book's preface states that it was written by Wei Chi Wo, who was a historian commissioned to write it during the reign of the Li family, who claimed to be descendants of Emperor Taizong of Tang. Thus, they referred to Chang'an as the "Central Court." This work records the historical anecdotes of the four Tang dynasties of Xuan, Yi, Zhao, and Ai. The first volume mostly documents the deeds of emperors and officials, as well as the systems of the court, while the second volume contains a miscellaneous collection of supernatural and strange events. Some of these accounts cannot be fully relied upon. For example, the story of Emperor Xuanzong being persecuted by Emperor Wuzong and seeking refuge as a monk in Jiangnan has been criticized by Sima Guang's Kao Yi as baseless and unfounded. Another example is the story of Lu Yan plotting against Liu Zhan, which relied on Zhang Gong Su, the governor of Youzhou, to submit a petition to clear the matter. However, Zhang Gong Su was not the governor of Youzhou at that time; it was Zhang Yun Shen. Therefore, the account is in error. Additionally, the story of Zheng Tian's ghost pregnancy is identical to the beginning and end of the story "Qi Tui Nu Chuan" written by a Tang person, but with different names. Despite these inaccuracies, the time of the Southern Tang Dynasty was not far from the Tang Dynasty, so the accounts from the Li family's literature are often credible. For instance, the story of Cui Yanzhao and Wang Ning's enmity, though Sima Guang's Kao Yi points out the error of replacing Yanzhao with Ning in the salt and iron administration, the entire story is taken from this work. Another example is Emperor Zhaozong's poem "Pusa Man," which is often recorded by literary critics and is mostly based on this work. Readers should distinguish and observe separately from the official history, discarding errors and retaining truths, which can still provide valuable evidence.

    There was a legendary figure known as the Dragon Raiser. In Chang'an, there was a family or person who raised dragons. When they passed through Huazhou, Li Ne was the governor of Huazhou. His father, Jian Shao, was previously on good terms with Bai Juyi. Li Ne was an upright person. Upon hearing about the dragons, he considered it a falsehood and ordered them to be brought to the government office for inspection. They were placed in a small bottle and then poured into a basin, where they turned out to be two small eels. Li Ne angrily asked how this could be verified. The person replied that verification was not difficult and suggested digging a hole one foot wide in the ground. Water was then poured into the hole. The eels were thrown into the water and began to swim around, their tails touching the corners of the hole, causing it to expand and the water to rise. The hole had widened to several feet. The person advised Li Ne that if the hole continued to widen, it would be difficult to control. The eels were then placed back into the bottle. Li Ne was surprised and gave generous gifts of money and silk before taking the eels back to the imperial palace.

    Li Cong, serving as the governor of Hunan, received a carp presented by a fisherman. The carp was several feet long. Li Cong ordered his family to cook it. In the carp's belly, they found a seal. The inscription read "Hengshan County Seal." Li Cong then instructed his guards to search for recent documents from Hengshan County. Upon examining the seal, it was clearly recognized. He then sent for the magistrate of Hengshan County to bring the seal. When the magistrate arrived, the seal was indeed newly cast. Li Cong questioned him in private. The magistrate confessed guilt and said, "The old seal was stolen by a bad person. I and the chief clerk were both worried about punishment, so we secretly ordered the craftsmen to make it. Now we only await the death sentence." Li Cong pitied him and kept the matter secret. He broke the new seal and ordered the magistrate to carry the old seal back to the county. Few knew about it.

    Duke Du Cong, a minister, enjoyed a rare combination of good fortune and longevity. He ate five meals a day, which was his usual routine. Each day's expenses amounted to ten thousand coins. At night, he had one meal. In his later years, a physician consulted him, saying, "Your Excellency should not eat at night, as it may cause blockage in your organs and lead to illness." Li Cong laughed and replied, "For over sixty years, I have done this. What harm could it do?"
