    The Zhongzhao Gushi, a work of two volumes, was compiled by Wei Chi Wo of the Southern Tang Dynasty. The details of Wei Chi Wo's background are not fully known, but the book's preface states that it was written by Wei Chi Wo, who was a historian commissioned to write it during the reign of the Li family, who claimed to be descendants of Emperor Taizong of Tang. Thus, they referred to Chang'an as the "Central Court." This work records the historical anecdotes of the four Tang dynasties of Xuan, Yi, Zhao, and Ai. The first volume mostly documents the deeds of emperors and officials, as well as the systems of the court, while the second volume contains a miscellaneous collection of supernatural and strange events. Some of these accounts cannot be fully relied upon. For example, the story of Emperor Xuanzong being persecuted by Emperor Wuzong and seeking refuge as a monk in Jiangnan has been criticized by Sima Guang's Kao Yi as baseless and unfounded. Another example is the story of Lu Yan plotting against Liu Zhan, which relied on Zhang Gong Su, the governor of Youzhou, to submit a petition to clear the matter. However, Zhang Gong Su was not the governor of Youzhou at that time; it was Zhang Yun Shen. Therefore, the account is in error. Additionally, the story of Zheng Tian's ghost pregnancy is identical to the beginning and end of the story "Qi Tui Nu Chuan" written by a Tang person, but with different names. Despite these inaccuracies, the time of the Southern Tang Dynasty was not far from the Tang Dynasty, so the accounts from the Li family's literature are often credible. For instance, the story of Cui Yanzhao and Wang Ning's enmity, though Sima Guang's Kao Yi points out the error of replacing Yanzhao with Ning in the salt and iron administration, the entire story is taken from this work. Another example is Emperor Zhaozong's poem "Pusa Man," which is often recorded by literary critics and is mostly based on this work. Readers should distinguish and observe separately from the official history, discarding errors and retaining truths, which can still provide valuable evidence. [1]
