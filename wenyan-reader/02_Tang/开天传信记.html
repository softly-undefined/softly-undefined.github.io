    Records of the Heavenly Era, Passed Down with Faith
    A Second Category of the Novel Genre: Strange Tales
    Abstract

    Your subjects, the court officials, respectfully submit that Records of the Heavenly Era, Passed Down with Faith is a one-scroll work composed by Zheng Qi of the Tang Dynasty. Zheng Qi, with the courtesy name Yunwu, was from Xingyang. He passed the imperial examination and was repeatedly promoted to the position of Right Attendant of the Scattered Gentlemen. He was fond of composing poems and ballads, indirectly criticizing Emperor Zhaozong, and it was believed that he had deep knowledge, which led to his promotion to the position of Minister of Rites and member of the imperial council. This is the person referred to as "Zheng Wu, the After-the-Pause," whose actions as chancellor are known. According to the Old History of the Tang Dynasty, Zheng Qi once held the positions of Censor, Palace Attendant, Grain and Household Censor, Gold and Criminal Censor, and Right Justice Censor. However, the original manuscript of this book lists his position as Junior Censor of the Ministry of Personnel, which is not mentioned in his biography. Perhaps there is a gap or omission in the historical record. The book contains thirty-two stories of the Kaiyuan and Tianbao eras, all of which are recorded in the preface as being written during his spare time from official duties, seeking out lost or forgotten knowledge with the aim of absolute credibility, hence the title Records of the Heavenly Era, Passed Down with Faith.

    One of the stories recounts Emperor Xuanzong's leisurely activities in the southern part of the capital, where Wang Ju invited him to his home to plot against the family of Empress Wei. According to the Old History of the Tang Dynasty, Wang Ju was selected to serve as a chief clerk and visited the crown prince to offer advice, but there is no mention of him having previously visited Wang Ju's home. Sima Guang, in his Comprehensive Mirror to Aid in Government, also does not follow this account, but the New History of the Tang Dynasty incorporates it. However, during the time when the family of Empress Wei held power, Wang Ju was fleeing with the party of Wang Tongjiao to Jiangdu, so how could he have chosen to live in the area of Wei and Du? Zheng Qi's account seems unlikely to be factual, and it is perhaps for this reason that Sima Guang did not include it. Other stories, such as Zheng Qi's encounter with the god of Mount Hua, his dream of visiting the Moon Palace, and the supernatural abilities of Luō Gōngyuǎn and Yè Fǎshàn, also involve elements of the supernatural and are not entirely consistent with refined and elegant writing. However, because these stories have been widely circulated for a long time, many books discussing Tang Dynasty events often reference them, so they are included here as a contribution to the genre of strange tales.

    This translation was respectfully submitted in the twelfth month of the forty-sixth year of the Qianlong era.

    The Record of the Truths of Kaiyuan and Tianbao
    By Zheng Qi of the Tang Dynasty

    Who am I? Repeatedly holding the position of Lang, I have thought diligently about the classics to self-cultivate and encourage myself. I secretly believe that the stories of our dynasty were most prosperous during the Kaiyuan and Tianbao periods. I hold close the classics and laws, viewing state affairs through a narrow tube, participating in what I hear, but there may be omissions. The prosperity of the peaceful times cannot be allowed to fall. I take advantage of my spare time to search for and transmit these truths, calling it the Record of the Truths of Kaiyuan and Tianbao. A small and insignificant vessel, the fault of the Zhou tripod not being in harmony, how far is it? Those who are fond of learning should observe their intentions, forgive their foolishness, and recognize their genuine intentions. When the future emperor was living in his princely residence, he often played in the southern part of Chang'an, between Wei and Du, chasing hares and enjoying himself so much that he forgot to return, accompanied by several companions. When they were very tired, they rested under a large tree in the outskirts. Suddenly, a scholar invited the future emperor to his home. The scholar's home was poor, with only a wife and a donkey. After sitting for a short while, the scholar killed the donkey, chopped garlic, and prepared a feast of meat and wine, which was abundant. The future emperor looked at him with surprise and found him noble and extraordinary. He asked the scholar's name and learned that it was Wang Ju. From then on, whenever the future emperor visited Wei and Du, he would always stop by Wang Ju's house. Wang Ju's advice was in line with his thoughts, and they became closer and more intimate. When the Wei clan gained power, the future emperor was very worried and secretly spoke to Wang Ju. Wang Ju said, "If there is chaos, then kill them. What else is there to doubt?" The future emperor then accepted Wang Ju's plan, quelled the disaster, and was repeatedly promoted to the position of Zhongshu Shilang, indeed included in the temple sacrifices.

The emperor said, "Locusts are a heavenly calamity, truly caused by our lack of virtue. You request to capture locusts, but does this not go against our principles and harm righteousness?"     In the early years of Tianbao, the emperor toured the Huaqing Palace. There was Liu Zhaoxia who presented a congratulatory poem for the emperor's visit to the hot springs. The style of the poem was bold and varied, mixed with humor, and much of it is not recorded here. Here is a brief excerpt:

    "If it is the second year of Tianbao, after the tenth month and before the twelfth, the imperial officials prepare the necessary supplies, and the emperor's carriage arrives at the hot springs. The gates of heaven open, revealing the chariots of the immortals. The imperial carriage emerges, followed by an array of armored soldiers in a grand procession.

    One team in blue, another in yellow, bears tread on chests, and leopards grab their backs. A red team, a team of embroidered robes, jade-adorned horses, and golden saddles. The clouds of virtue stretch straight, grasping the marrow of Pangu and the flesh of Nüwa. How could the thousand emperors of ancient times compare to me, the emperor of today? In his own account, he says, 'There are strange and unlucky setbacks on the road, leading to wild and reckless behavior. Though my bones are short, my skills and arts are long. In dreams, I return to wealth and honor, but upon waking, I remain in sorrow. Today is a once-in-a-thousand-years encounter; bow your head, do not be five or six angles.' The emperor found this poem strange and intended to reward Liu Zhaoxia. He ordered Liu Zhaoxia to remove the lines 'five or six angles' and submitted a response: 'I wrote this poem with divine assistance, believing that my writing was flawless and my pen did not pause. I do not wish to change it under heaven.' The emperor replied, 'You are indeed a poor and thin person,' and granted him the position of Spring Official and Guard Captain on the left."
