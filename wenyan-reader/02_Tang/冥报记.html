    　 冥报记卷上　
    　　夫含气有生。无不有识。有识而有行。随行善恶而受其报。如农夫之播植。随所植而收之。此盖物之常理。固无所可疑也。上智达其本源。知而无见。下愚闇其踪迹。迷而不返。皆绝言也。中品之人。未能自达。随缘动见。逐见生疑。疑见多端。各怀异执释典论其分别。凡有六十二见。邪倒于是乎生者也。临在中人之后。幸而寤其万一。比见众人不信因果者说见虽多。同谓善恶无报。无报之说。略有三种。一者自然。故无因果。唯当任欲待事而已。二者灭尽。言死而身灭。识无所住。身识都尽。谁受苦乐。以无受故。知无因果。三者无报。

    Records of Rebirth (唐 唐临)
    Compiled by Tang Lin, Minister of Personnel

    Volume 1 of Records of Rebirth
    All beings that have breath and life possess consciousness. With consciousness comes action, and one receives retribution according to the good or evil of their actions. This is like a farmer sowing seeds, for whatever is sown is reaped. This is the constant principle of all things, and there is nothing to doubt. The highest wisdom understands its fundamental source, knowing it without seeing it. The lowest ignorance is unaware of its traces, wandering blindly and never returning. These are matters beyond words. People of middle capacity cannot fully comprehend. They encounter phenomena by chance, and from these encounters, doubts arise. These doubts take many forms, each person holding their own differing views. The Buddhist scriptures discuss their distinctions, totaling sixty-two views, from which heretical beliefs arise. I, the author, am among those of middle capacity, and fortunately, I have awakened to a small part of it. I have seen many who do not believe in cause and effect, and though their views are numerous, they all assert that good and evil actions have no retribution. The doctrine of no retribution has three main forms:

    1. Naturalism, which holds that there is no cause and effect, and one should simply follow their desires and wait for events to unfold.
    2. Annihilationism, which claims that upon death, the body and consciousness cease to exist, leaving no one to experience suffering or joy. Since there is no experiencer, there is no cause and effect.
    3. The doctrine of no retribution, which denies any consequences for actions.

    Observing that some people today cultivate morality, if they are poor and lowly, they die early; or they commit evil deeds, if they are wealthy and noble, they live long. Because of this incident, one knows that there is no cause and effect. I secretly think that Confucian texts discuss the rewards and punishments for good and evil deeds, which are many. Those who are near in time receive their reward immediately; those who are in the middle receive their reward after many years; those who are far away receive their reward in the descendants. Those who receive their reward immediately, such as the ruler of Chu who swallowed a leech and all chronic diseases were cured, the ruler of Song who did not pray and the omen stars retreated, the person with a bad tooth who was punished immediately upon turning around, Zhao Gao who caused chaos and soon his family was destroyed, and so on. Those who receive their reward after many years, such as Wei Ke who married his concubine and finally saved his army, Sun Shu who buried a snake and eventually enjoyed many blessings, Emperor Yuan of Han who poisoned Prince Li and caused disaster, Qi who killed Peng Sheng and was punished by having a pig appear, and so on. Those who receive their reward in the descendants, such as Fu Fu who was respectful to three commands and spread Confucius's teachings, Deng Xun who saved a thousand people each year and left a legacy of peace and prosperity, Chen Ping who had secret plans and knew he would have no descendants, Luan Yan who was extravagant and was punished with disaster, and so on. As for Yu Shun, who ascended to the throne through filial piety, and King Wen of Zhou, who received his mandate through benevolence and wisdom.

    3

    Jie and Zhou perished their countries through cruelty. You and Li perished through debauchery and indulgence. The virtues and achievements of the Three Dynasties ensured their long-lasting rule. The tyrannical Qin emperor, though powerful, saw his dynasty fall within a generation. Such cases as these are numerous. Even though the scale may differ, they are all verifications of good and evil. But when it comes to the principles of kingship, it is related to the will of heaven. In ordinary discourse, it is not appropriate to say. What is recorded now merely takes these subtle verifications, in order to inspire those of the same kind and warn and instruct descendants, as evidence in the world of humans and spirits. The teachings of the Buddha are nothing but cause and effect. Cause is the action, and is the retribution. Nothing is not a cause, and nothing has a cause that does not have a retribution. However, the teachings of retribution also have three kinds: immediate retribution, where good and evil deeds done in this life are experienced in this life; rebirth retribution, where deeds done in this life are experienced in a future life, depending on the nature of the deeds; and future retribution, where good and evil deeds done in past lives are experienced in multiple lives.

    4

    Therefore, in the present actions, one cannot immediately receive retribution. Sometimes the next generation may suffer, or after five or ten lifetimes, the consequences may finally manifest. All these are called delayed retribution. In these three kinds of retribution—immediate, intermediate, and delayed—they encompass all phenomena, leaving nothing out. Thus, the various views are now greatly enlightened. However, today's common people are still confused by this concept. They focus on causes and forget the results, doubting what they hear but believing what they see. Therefore, when they hear about delayed retribution, they either accept it or reject it. But when they see evidence, they are amazed, sigh, and believe. In the past, there were notable figures such as the Jin dynasty scholar Xie Fu, the Song dynasty chancellor Fu Gao, the Southern Dynasties literary figure Bao Yan, and the Southern Qi scholar Lu Guo. Some were respected figures of their time, while others were famous scholars of the era. They all compiled collections of miracles, such as the Guanshiyin Yingyan Ji (Records of the Miracles of Avalokiteshvara), the Xuan Yan Ji (Records of Miracles) by King Jingling Xiao Ziliang of the Qi dynasty, and the Mingxiang Ji (Records of Supernatural Events) by Wang Yan. All these works are meant to demonstrate good and evil, to encourage and warn future generations, truly making those who hear deeply reflect. I have been inspired by their spirit and intent and also wish to encourage others. I have recorded what I have heard and compiled this record. I have also described what I have experienced and the reasons for what I have heard and seen. The words are not embellished, and the facts are presented clearly and accurately. May future readers pay attention to it.

    5

    In the capital, there was a highly respected monk named Xinxing. Originally, he was a monk at the Fa Zang Temple in Xiangzhou. At first, his mother had no children, and she was worried for a long time. A monk passed by and advised her to recite the name of Guanyin Bodhisattva. The mother said she would pray at night. Soon after, she became pregnant and gave birth to Xinxing. From a young age, he was intelligent and extensively studied Buddhist scriptures and treatises. His understanding exceeded that of others. He believed that the Buddha's teachings aimed at saving and guiding beings, sometimes according to the nature of the listener, he pointed out the path, and sometimes according to the times and circumstances, he decided on the appropriate dharma. Now, it has been a long time since the Buddha's time, and the natures and times have changed greatly. If lower beings practice higher methods, the method may not suit their nature, and they might go astray. Therefore, he compiled and collated scriptures and treatises to verify what should be learned by people, compiling thirty-six volumes titled 'Ren Ji Lu' (Human Collection Record).

    At the beginning of the Kaihuang era, the Left Chancellor and Duke of Qi heard of his great reputation, reported to Emperor Wen, and summoned him to the capital. He stayed at the True Silence Temple built by the official. Xinxing then compiled four volumes of the Three Stages Method based on the Vinaya and Sutras. The main idea is to encourage universal reverence, recognize the root of evil, contemplate Buddha-nature, prescribe medicine according to the illness, and teach the immediate one vehicle. From all over the country, those who are brave and diligent in practice all followed him. Xinxing often practiced asceticism, begging for food, and worshipped six times a day. He worked hard to stabilize his mind, focusing on emptiness and true wisdom. Whenever he sat in meditation and preached, he often saw four green-clad boys holding flowers standing by. Once, while sitting in meditation with his disciples in the hall, everyone suddenly smelled a strange fragrance, and light filled the hall. They were all amazed and asked Xinxing about it.

    Xin Xing commanded his disciple Monk Yong to inquire about something. Yong said, "I saw a transformed Buddha coming down from the sky, arriving before the Master, touching his head, and giving him a prophecy. It was as if the Buddha also touched my head and gave me a prophecy. The rest of the scene was the same as what I described." Later, Yong managed his group of disciples and retreated to Mount Taibai. One day, he called together the monks and said, "I will return to the capital with you." The monks, respecting Yong, all agreed. They then descended the mountain and stayed overnight in Wugong. Before dawn, they set out, and Yong told the group, "Strive, everyone. Tonight we must enter the city." They reached a place called Cao, and Yong said, "The city gates are closed." They then stayed at an inn. In the evening, Yong wept and said, "It is too late." When asked why, he did not respond. The next morning, they entered the city and arrived at Zhenji Temple. At dusk the previous night, Xin Xing had passed away. The monks at the temple were surprised to see Yong and asked why he had come. Yong replied, "I saw many people carrying incense, flowers, banners, and canopies coming from the west and entering Kaiyuan Gate, heading towards Zhenji Temple. I suspected the Master wanted to leave, so I came. Last night, I saw the Master leading his retinue westward and looking back to say goodbye to me. That's why I know it's too late." Initially, the monks in the capital who doubted Xin Xing's teachings discussed it and referred to the Fayuan Zangjing (Transmission of the Dharma Treasury), which states that if a person has heard the true Dharma in a past life, their ears will be perfectly aligned. They then examined Xin Xing's skull and found that his ears were indeed perfectly aligned. They all felt ashamed and regretful, finally believing and respecting him. Initially, Xin Xing's followers lived in five temples in the capital. Later, the number of temples increased, but they were still known as the Five Masters. (As told by Old Monk and Uncle Lin.)

    The Monk Hui Ru of the True Silence Temple in the Capital City

    In his youth, Hui Ru was diligent in his ascetic practices and studied under the teacher Xin Xing. After Xin Xing's death, Hui Ru continued to follow his teachings. During the reign of Emperor Yang of Sui, he engaged in meditation and cultivation of concentration. For seven days, he remained motionless, which amazed the crowd who thought he had entered a meditative state. Later, Hui Ru opened his eyes and cried, with tears flowing freely. The monks were surprised and asked him why. He replied that his feet were burning and in pain, and he would speak after examining the wound. The monks asked further, and Hui Ru said that he had been invited by King Yama. He had walked the path for seven days, and King Yama asked if he wished to see any deceased friends. Hui Ru answered that he wanted to see two people. King Yama immediately sent for one, but only a turtle appeared, licking Hui Ru's feet before leaving with tears in its eyes. Another person was said to have committed heavy sins and could not be called. Hui Ru was ordered to go and see them. A messenger led Hui Ru to the prison gate, which was very solid. The messenger called the guard, and someone answered. The messenger told Hui Ru to quickly avoid the path and not block the gate. As soon as Hui Ru moved aside, the gate opened, and a great fire flowed out, like a star forging iron, striking Hui Ru's feet. He brushed the blanket over it, raised his eyes to look at the gate, which was already closed. He could not see anyone. King Yama gave him thirty bolts of silk, but Hui Ru refused, saying it had already been sent to the back room. The monks rushed there, and when they looked, the silk was on the bed. Hui Ru's burned feet were as large as coins, and it took more than a hundred days to heal. He died at the beginning of the reign of Emperor Gaozu of Tang. The True Silence Temple is now known as the Transformation and Measure Temple. (This temple was established by Duke Qi, my maternal grandfather, and I often visited it. I often heard my uncle speak of this.)

    A Great Monk from the State of Jiang. The monk named Seng Che was young and skilled. He built a hall on the western slope of Lonely Mountain, surrounded by many trees. The location was quite suitable for mountain living. Once, while Seng Che was walking through the mountains, he saw a leper in a cave. The leper had ulcers and a foul odor and begged Seng Che for food. Moved by compassion, Seng Che called him out and took him back to his temple. He built a cave next to the temple for the leper, provided him with clothing and food, and taught him to recite the Lotus Sutra. This person could not read or write and was also dull-witted. Seng Che taught him word by word, which required a great deal of effort. However, he never tired. After reciting about half of the sutra, the person began to have dreams where someone taught him. From then on, he gradually became more intelligent. By the time he had recited five or six chapters, his wounds began to heal. By the time he finished reciting the entire sutra, his beard and eyebrows had grown back, and he had regained his normal weight and appearance. He was also able to heal others. Once, when Seng Che himself was suffering from swelling, he sent this person to recite incantations, which proved effective.

    10

    There was a virtuous nun from Hedong who often recited the Lotus Sutra. She sought out a skilled calligrapher and offered several times the usual payment. She specially prepared a clean room and asked him to write this scripture. She bathed once, burned incense to perfume her clothes, and also had a wall in the writing room broken through to the outside, adding a bamboo tube. She ordered the calligrapher to hold the bamboo tube and exhale outside whenever he wanted to breathe out. She completed one scroll of the scripture in eight years, offering the utmost respect and veneration.

    The monk Fahuan from Longmen often gathered a large audience to lecture on the Lotus Sutra. Because this nun's copy was meticulously accurate, he sent someone to request it. The nun firmly refused to give it, and Fahuan criticized and reproached her. The nun, unable to refuse, then personally delivered it. When Fahuan opened and read it, he saw only yellow paper with no writing. When he opened other scrolls, they were all the same. Fahuan and his followers felt ashamed and fearful, and immediately returned it to the nun.

    The nun wept and accepted it, washed the box with fragrant water, bathed, and placed it on her head. She walked around the Buddha, reciting sutras, for seven days and nights without stopping. Afterward, when she opened it, the text was as before.

The next morning, Zhi Gan arrived at the prefecture and reported to the prefect Li Defeng. Li Defeng sent someone to Longzhou to investigate. Upon arrival, it was discovered that the local official had already died. When asked about the matter, it was revealed that this occurred at the time when the official came to report. From then on, the matter ceased. Later, the prefectural office sent Zhi Gan to escort the prisoners to the capital. They reached the border of Fengzhou, but the prisoners all escaped. Zhi Gan was worried and fearful, and after chasing and pursuing for several days, he could not catch them. That night, he stayed at a monastery. Suddenly, he saw his former subordinate ghost official come to report, saying, "The prisoners are all captured. One has already died, and three are in a valley in the south mountains. They have all been captured and bound. Please do not worry." After speaking, the ghost official left. Zhi Gan immediately requested troops and entered the western valley of the south mountains. Indeed, he saw four prisoners. The prisoners knew that running away was futile, so they came to resist. Zhi Gan overpowered them, killing one prisoner and binding the other three. Just as he had been told, everything unfolded as expected. Zhi Gan is still alive today, serving as the Military Governor of Cizhou. The Imperial Steward Liu Hen told me about it. Heng served as the Prefect of Caozhou and asked Zhi Gan personally, saying, "Yes, the Censor Wei Tongjie also said he had heard it. All said it was like this."
