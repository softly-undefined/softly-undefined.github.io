    The Qinding Siku Quanshu, Volume Twelve of the Subsidiary Section
    Introduction
    Collection of Strange Records, a work of the second category of the Xiaoshuo class [Strange Tales of the Officials], is compiled by Xue Yongruo of the Tang Dynasty. According to the Artistic Literature Record of the Tang Dynasty, Xue Yongruo, also known as Zhongsheng, was a governor of Guangzhou during the Longqing period, but his place of origin is not recorded. This edition of the book is titled "Hedong" at the beginning, but during the Tang Dynasty, aristocratic families often used their clan origins as identifiers; for example, Liu must be from Pengcheng, and Li must be from Longxi. Therefore, it is uncertain where Xue Yongruo was actually born. This book contains sixteen stories. According to the Reading Records of Zhao Gongwu, the first story recorded is about Xu Zuoqing's transformation, and this edition also places this story first, which aligns with Zhao's records. It is likely still the original version. The stories commonly known include those of Di Renjie's green fur coat, Wang Wei's black robe, and others.

    集异记

    The Ji Yi Ji (Records of Strange Events) records ten individuals, including Liu Fangyuan and Ma Su, in volume 348 of the Tai Ping Guang Ji (Comprehensive Records of the Great Peace). It mentions a section about Li Quanzhi, which describes a flood in Jiyin during the year of Ren Xu in the reign of Emperor Fanchang. According to this account, the god of the grain was in the same boat as Li Quanzhi. This version of the text does not include this event, as it seems to be a compilation and not a complete work. The text records various supernatural events, with an elegant and well-structured narrative. The recorded poems are quite skillful and are considered superior to other novels. However, only the inscription on the mirror by Shi Kuang does not resemble the language of the Three Dynasties.

    Chen Zhen Sun, in his Book Catalogue and Commentary, stated that the work touched on contemporary taboos, hence its name was omitted. The preface also mentions that the work is not just for entertainment but also provides rough and clear advice, or perhaps to gain some protection by hearing unpleasant words. However, when one examines the content, it is not obvious that it touches on taboos. When verified by the citations in the Tai Ping Guang Ji, it is indeed this book and not a fabrication. It is unclear where the allegory lies.

    唐　薛用弱　撰
    　　徐佐卿
    　　明皇天宝十三载重阳日猎于沙苑云间有孤鶻徊翔焉上亲御弧矢一发而中其鶻则带箭徐坠将及地丈许歘然矫翰西南而逝万众极目良久乃灭益州城距郭十五里有明月观焉依山临水松桂深寂道流非修习精悫者莫得而居观之东廊第一院尤为幽绝每有自称青城道士徐佐卿者风局清古一岁率三四而至焉观之耆旧因虚其院之正堂以俟其来而佐卿至则栖焉或三五日或旬朔言归青城甚为道流之所倾仰一日忽自外至神爽不怡谓院中人曰吾行山中偶为飞矢所加寻已无恙矣然此箭非人间所有吾留之于壁上后年箭主到此即宜付之慎无坠失仍援毫记壁云留箭之日则十三载九月九日也及玄宗避狄幸蜀暇日命驾行游偶至斯观乐其佳景因遍幸道室既入此堂忽覩挂箭则命侍臣取而翫之盖御箭也深异之因询观之道士皆以实对即是佐卿所题乃前岁沙苑纵畋之日也佐卿盖中箭孤鶻耳究其题乃沙苑翻飞当日集于斯欤上大奇之因收其箭而宝焉自后蜀人亦无复有逢佐卿者矣

    During the reign of Emperor Xuanzong of the Tang Dynasty, in the thirteenth year of the Tianbao era, on the Double Ninth Festival, the emperor went hunting in the clouds above Shayan. There, a solitary falcon was circling. The emperor personally shot an arrow and hit the falcon, which then fell slowly with the arrow still in it, just before reaching the ground, it suddenly flew southwest and disappeared. The crowd watched in amazement for a long time until it vanished.

    Fifteen miles from the city of Yizhou, there is a Moonlight Temple by the mountains and waters, surrounded by deep and quiet pines and cedars. Only those who have diligently practiced can live here. The first courtyard on the east corridor is especially secluded and beautiful. Every year, a man claiming to be the Taoist Master Xu Zuoqing from Qingcheng Mountain would come, usually three or four times a year. The old inhabitants of the temple would leave the main hall of the first courtyard empty in anticipation of his arrival. When he arrived, he would stay there for a few days or a month, saying he was returning to Qingcheng. He was greatly respected by the Taoists.

    One day, he suddenly arrived from outside, looking troubled. He told the people in the courtyard, "While traveling in the mountains, I was accidentally hit by an arrow, but I am now fine. However, this arrow is not from the human world. I left it on the wall. When the owner of the arrow comes back in two years, you should give it to him. Be careful not to lose it. I also wrote on the wall, 'The day the arrow was left is the ninth day of the ninth month of the thirteenth year.' "

    When Emperor Xuanzong fled to Shu to avoid the nomads, on a leisure day, he ordered his carriage to go for a tour and happened to come to this temple. He enjoyed the beautiful scenery and visited all the Taoist rooms. Upon entering this hall, he suddenly saw the hanging arrow and ordered his attendants to take it and play with it. It turned out to be the emperor's arrow. He was deeply surprised and asked the Taoists, who all told him the truth. It was indeed what Xu Zuoqing had written, referring to the day he shot the falcon in Shayan. Xu Zuoqing must have been the one who shot the falcon. Investigating the inscription, it seemed that the falcon had flown from Shayan to this place on the same day. The emperor was greatly surprised and kept the arrow as a treasure. Afterward, no one in Shu ever saw Xu Zuoqing again.

    Wang Jixi
    During the reign of Emperor Xuanzong, when the emperor fled south, all officials rushed to his temporary residence. Wang Jixi, a skilled player of wei qi from the imperial academy, followed him. The roads in the Shu region were narrow and difficult, and travelers often had to stop at posthouses along the way, which were frequently occupied by powerful officials. Wang Jixi, restless and without a place to settle, followed the stream deep into the mountains and stayed overnight in the home of a solitary old woman. Only the wife and daughter-in-law were present, providing water and firewood. As soon as it grew dark, they closed their doors and went to rest, while Wang Jixi slept under the eaves. In the middle of the night, he could not sleep and suddenly heard the sound of a game of wei qi inside the house. The grandmother said to the daughter-in-law, "This is a fine night, and we have nothing to do. Let's play a game of wei qi and bet on it. Would you like to?" The daughter-in-law replied, "Yes." Wang Jixi was intrigued by this, especially since there were no lights in the room, and the grandmother and daughter-in-law were in separate rooms. He leaned his ear against the door and soon heard the daughter-in-law say, "Place a piece at five east and nine south." The grandmother replied, "Place a piece at five east and twelve south." The daughter-in-law then said, "Place a piece at eight west and ten south." The grandmother responded, "Place a piece at nine west and ten south." Each time a piece was placed, there was a long period of thought and deliberation.

    The Equalizing Pavilion

    During the reign of Emperor Wen of Sui, the monk Chéngkōng, at the age of twenty, vowed to cast an iron statue seventy feet tall in the west of the Fen River near Jin Yang. He gathered iron and coal, meticulously calculated the expenses, and over twenty years, the resources were finally prepared. He then announced far and wide, gathering the wise and the foolish, and chose a day to cast the statue. When the smoke and flames subsided and the furnace was opened, the statue was not completed. Chéngkōng deeply blamed himself, prostrated himself in repentance, reaffirmed his previous vow, and planned another casting. He worked diligently and arduously for another twenty years, and the expenses were again prepared. He then announced far and wide, gathering the wise and the foolish, and chose a day to cast the statue. When the furnace was opened, the statue was again not completed. Chéngkōng then called out to heaven, seeking mercy, and knelt before the Buddha, asking for forgiveness. He severely criticized himself and strove hard, encouraging himself, and after another twenty years, the effort was again gathered. He then announced far and wide, gathering the wise and the foolish, and chose a day to cast the statue. When the time came, Chéngkōng climbed to the top of the furnace, a hundred feet high, and spoke loudly to the spectators: "When I was young, I made a vow to cast the Great Buddha. This year, I am eighty, and two attempts have failed. This goes against my heart, and I will not have a body to fulfill my vow. How much more so the current good deeds that are wasted."

    Pei Gao, a Confucian scholar-official, lived in Luoyang. In midsummer, he returned from the west of Zheng up to the Duanwu Festival to pay respects to his relatives. His horse was slow and weak, and he just arrived at the Stone Bridge. Walking along with a sense of urgency, he moved very fast. Soon, there was a rider leading another horse, whose steps were extremely fast. Seeing Pei Gao, the rider showed kindness. Pei Gao then said, "Are you not entering the capital by evening?"     Xing Caojin

    Posthumously granted the title of Minister of Works, Xing Caojin was renowned from the Zhidé era onward as a valiant general of the He Luo region. He served as governor of Weizhou, but was subordinated to the warlord Tian Chengsi. Once, while quelling a rebellion, an arrow struck his eye. They then used an iron clamp and a strong man to extract it, but the pain was excruciating, and the arrowhead was so firmly lodged that it could not be moved. Xing Caojin, in agony and without recourse, had his wife and children engage in extensive Buddhist rituals in hopes of divine mercy. After several days, they bound him to the bed once more and attempted to remove the arrowhead, but it remained as firmly lodged as before. Xing Caojin groaned in pain, endured it in silence, and waited for death. One day, during a daytime nap, he dreamed of a foreign monk entering his courtyard. Xing Caojin told the monk of his suffering. After a while, the monk said, “If you pour rice water into the wound, it will heal by itself.”

    Upon waking, Xing Caojin immediately told this to a physician, who replied, “Rice water is simply the water used for washing rice. How could that be suitable for an open wound?” He then ordered a wide inquiry among the people, but no one could understand the meaning. The next day, a foreign monk suddenly arrived at the gate begging for food. Xing Caojin quickly summoned him inside. From the central hall, he saw that this was the very monk he had dreamed of the previous day. He invited him to sit beside him and told him of his plight. The monk said, “Why not use cold food paste?” Xing Caojin then realized that the paste was rice water, and since what he had seen matched his dream, he applied it as instructed. As soon as it was applied, it cooled instantly, and the pain was greatly reduced. By nightfall, the wound began to itch. They then bound him as before and used force to pull out the arrowhead. Just as the clamp reached his face, the arrowhead suddenly emerged. After applying medicine for less than ten days, he was fully recovered. Alas, how clearly evident is the grace and protection of the Western saints!

The county was plagued by mythical creatures known as shanxiao, which could transform in a hundred different ways and no one dared to provoke them.     During the time when the Prince of Ning was gathering guests for conversation, a horse dealer named Qu Shen Nu presented two horses. The Prince immediately inspected the horses in the main hall, testing their steps, appearance, and spirit, which were all splendid. The guests observed them and found them equally impressive. The Prince then asked Qu Shen Nu, "What are their prices?" The broker first pointed to one and said it cost one thousand strings of cash, then pointed to the other and said it cost five hundred strings of cash. The Prince happily told his attendants to pay according to what was said and have the horses delivered to the stable. The guests could not understand why the prices were so different. They all inquired about it. The Prince then looked at the horse that cost a thousand strings of cash and said, "This horse, whether running fast or slow, after a hundred turns, does not raise any dust from its hooves." He then looked at the horse that cost five hundred strings of cash and said, "This horse, after ten passes, raises quite a bit of dust from its hooves." Using this standard, one can discern the high and low prices. The guests then understood.
