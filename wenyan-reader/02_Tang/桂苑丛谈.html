    The Guizhuan Congtan by Feng Yi of the Tang Dynasty

    Zhang Chuo and His Daoist Practices

    In the early years of the Xiantong era, there was a scholar named Zhang Chuo who, after failing the imperial examinations, traveled extensively in the Jiangnan region. He possessed certain supernatural abilities and practiced Daoist cultivation, abstaining from food and focusing on cultivating qi. He loved drinking wine and playing chess, and he was also involved in alchemical practices, particularly the creation of elixirs. One day, the sky seemed to laugh, and Zhang Chuo wrote down his thoughts, saying:

    "How can one catch the sun, which keeps flying overhead? The red furnace burns elixirs in vain, and the beauty of the jade-like face cannot be preserved. This year the flowers bloom on the branches, but next year the leaves fall from the trees. It is better to drink wine and not worry about the passing of time."

    People were amazed by this. He did not like to be overly decorated and often visited taverns where he enjoyed drinking. If someone invited him to drink and he was pleased, he would cut out twenty to thirty butterflies, blow them with his breath, and make them fly in a row. He could keep them in his hand for several moments before returning them. Those who saw this asked for them, but he would always find an excuse to refuse. He once traveled to Yancheng and was often drunk. Some people wanted to test him, so they competed in strength and detained him in the county. When he woke up, he composed two poems to express his feelings. The magistrate, seeing his talent, immediately released him. One of the poems reads:

    "Family traditions often have the fragrance of orchids and lilies, and the lineage of a powerful family has been passed down through generations."

    The Grand Marshal Zhu Ya's Case of the Temple

    The Grand Marshal Zhu Ya was sent out to govern the right side of the Zhejiang region. A monk named Ganlu, who served as the temple's officer, complained that the temple's assets had been secretly used by his predecessor, and that a certain amount of the temple's gold had been taken. He cited several predecessors, all of whom had clearly documented the handover of assets in writing. The collective testimony pointed to the newly appointed officer as the one who had secretly used the gold. When he first took office, the handover was clear, but when the time came to hand over the gold, it was nowhere to be found. The case was thoroughly investigated, and the evidence clearly showed his guilt. However, the reason for the expenditure had not been fully investigated. Some argued that the monks, unconstrained by minor details, had spent the gold, and thus he had no grounds to appeal, willing to face death rather than admit guilt. During the deliberation, the official suspected that the investigation was not complete. He probed the monk with intent, and the monk reported the truth, saying, "Those living in the temple enjoy their role as officers. Those who have been in charge before, for many years, have passed on empty documents of weight and value, in reality, there was no gold. The crowd believes I am isolated, do not mix with others, and wants to take this opportunity to exclude me." He shed tears, unable to bear the injustice. The official felt compassion and said, "This is not difficult." In a short time, he said, "I have figured it out." He immediately ordered several palanquins and summoned the involved monks to face the case. They were all sent in with screens, so that they could not see each other. He ordered to fetch yellow clay, and each was ordered to mold the next sample of gold from before and after the handover, to serve as evidence. The monks, not knowing the shape, finally failed to mold it. The official, angry, ordered to accuse the predecessors, who all admitted their guilt one by one. Those who were excluded were finally cleared of the charge.

    Cui and Zhang Proclaim Themselves as Heroes

    After failing the imperial examinations, the scholar-officials Cui Ya and Zhang Hu often traveled through the regions of Jiangsu and Anhui, where they frequently indulged in drinking and sometimes mocked and teased their contemporaries. When inspired by their drinking, they would proclaim themselves as heroes. The two shared similar tastes and became very close friends. Cui, while drunk, composed a poem about a hero: "Three feet of snow on Taihang Mountain, three feet of iron in Cui Ya's sleeve. If one day I meet a person with a heart, I will leave home immediately." This poem spread widely among the people, and they began to call Cui and Zhang true heroes. As a result, many people prepared wine and food to entertain them, allowing them to praise each other. One day, Zhang submitted a poem to the official in charge of salt production, and his son was appointed to a minor position in the canal system, which was colloquially known as "Winter Melon." Zhang had two sons, one named Chun'er and the other Gui-zi. They wrote a poem together: "Chun'er circles the tree in the spring garden, Gui-zi searches for flowers under the moonlight." Someone joked, "Your sons are not suitable for such positions." Zhang replied, "Winter Melon should produce Hu-zi." The jesters laughed heartily.

His governance was widely praised; within a short time, he improved the land and waterworks, transformed customs and manners, and won the hearts of the people.     The Square Bamboo Walking Stick

    Grand General Zhu Ya, twice governor of the eastern part of Zhejiang, once said upon his departure from his previous post, "I traveled to Ganlu Temple and visited the old monk in the temple to bid farewell." The old monk was skilled in reception, and during their conversation, he often spoke of his proficiency in Buddhist teachings, rarely discussing other matters. Thus, Grand General felt pity and respect for him. After they finished drinking tea, he was about to leave. He said, "In the past, a guest left me a walking stick made of square bamboo, which I would like to give to you as a parting gift." He quickly ordered it to be fetched, and it arrived shortly. The stick, though made of bamboo, was square-shaped, with its joints and fine hairs protruding from all sides, naturally beautiful. It was also a treasure of Zhu Ya, as one could tell. A few years later, when he took charge of Zhu Fang again, after three days, he visited the temple again and asked where the walking stick from before was. The monk replied, "I have treasured it until now." Grand General asked to see it, and the monk had rounded off the stick and painted it with lacquer. Grand General sighed and said, "From now on, I will no longer look at this monk with the same respect."
