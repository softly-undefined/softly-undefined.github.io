    Summary

    We respectfully submit that the Records of the Great Tang is a single volume whose author's name is unknown. It records various events from the early Tang Dynasty to the reign of Emperor Yuanhe. Neither the bibliographies of the Tang nor the Song Dynasties list this work. It has a preface written by the author, who states that in the summer of the eighth year, he traveled south to the mountainous regions. On leisure days, while traveling by boat, he recorded what he heard. Upon examination, it is found that after the reign of Emperor Mu, only the reigns of Emperor Taizong, Emperor Dazong, and Emperor Xiantong had an eighth year. This book does not specify the era name, and it is also unclear when the eighth year refers to. The records of the deeds and words of Tang nobles and officials are quite detailed, and many of these records are used in historical works. Occasionally, it includes humorous anecdotes and trivial matters from court and common life, which often differ from other works. It mentions that during the reign of Emperor Chengyuan, Princesses Zhengguo and Haunguo were given posthumous titles, marking the beginning of such posthumous titles for princesses. However, it does not mention that Princess Pingyang, daughter of Emperor Gaozu, already had a posthumous title. Additionally, it recounts a story about Xiang Yingshi encountering an old man who said he resembled Prince Poyang. According to the Book of Strange Stories, the old man was actually a notorious thief, but here he is recorded as a strange person. This discrepancy arises because different versions were circulating at the time, and the author recorded what he heard, which inevitably led to contradictions. However, the parts that can be relied upon are about six or seven out of ten. This work was submitted for official review in June of the forty-fourth year of Emperor Qianlong's reign.
