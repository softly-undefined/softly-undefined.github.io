    The Imperially Approved Complete Library, Volume 12, Subcategory of the Novelist Category: Miscellaneous Stories - Preface

    We officials respectfully present the Miscellaneous Records of Emperor Ming in two volumes and the Supplementary Records in one volume, compiled by Zheng Chuhui of the Tang dynasty. Zheng Chuhui, courtesy name Yanmei, was from Yingyang and the grandson of the chancellor Zheng Yuqing. He passed the imperial examination in the eighth year of the Taihe era and rose to the position of Censor-in-Inspector of the Ministry of Justice and Military Governor of the Xuanwu Army. His biography is attached to the Old Book of Tang entry on Zheng Yuqing. This work was completed in the ninth year of the Dazhong era and records various miscellaneous events. According to historical records, when Zheng Chuhui was serving as a proofreader, he compiled three volumes of Miscellaneous Records of Emperor Ming, which circulated in the world. Zhao Gongwu's Records of Books mentions Miscellaneous Records of Emperor Ming in two volumes, but also states that there is a Supplementary Record in one volume, which includes twelve events recorded by the Censor-in-Inspector. Zhao Gongwu counts both the main text and the supplementary volume together, while Ye Mengde in his Summer Retreat Notes says that Zheng Chuhui's Miscellaneous Records of Emperor Ming records the dispute between Zhang Qujiang and Li Linfu over the real estate of Niu Xianke. At that time, it was autumn, and the emperor ordered Gao Lishi to give a white feather fan to Zhang Qujiang, who, feeling anxious, wrote a poem to present. It seemed to say that Emperor Ming was about to be dismissed for disobeying orders, hence the gift of the fan in autumn to convey his intention. The New Book of Tang included this event in the biography. [1]

    Imperially Ordered Four Treasuries of Literature
    Miscellaneous Records of the Ming Emperor, Volume One
    Compiled by Zheng Chuhui of the Tang Dynasty

    During the reign of Emperor Xuanzong of the Tang Dynasty, when Fang Guan was the magistrate of Lü County, Xing Zhen Hépú came from Mount Tai. Fang Guan treated him with utmost respect and courtesy. They walked together leisurely, and before they knew it, they had traveled dozens of miles to the village of Xiagu. There, they encountered an abandoned Buddhist temple with dense pine and bamboo trees. Hépú sat beneath a pine tree and struck the ground with his staff, instructing his attendant to dig several feet deep. They uncovered a jar containing letters written by Lou Shide and Yong Gong. Hépú smiled and asked Fang, "Do you recognize these?" Fang suddenly remembered his past life as a monk, and Hépú explained that Yong Gong was his former self. Hépú then told Fang, "When you die, it will be because you ate fish. After your death, you will be buried in a coffin made of paulownia wood. You will not die in your private residence, nor in a public hall, nor in a Taoist temple, nor in the home of relatives or friends. Later, you will be exiled to Lang Prefecture and will stay in the Zijigong Temple. After several days of illness, the magistrate of the prefecture suddenly prepared fish and invited Fang to his official residence. Fang happily agreed and went there. After finishing the meal, he returned home and died suddenly. The prefect ordered Fang's body to be placed in the temple, and the coffin was made of paulownia wood. [3]

    During the Tianbao period, the princesses imitated each other in their extravagant feasts. The Empress appointed Yuan Siyi as the Inspector of Feasts, and the delicacies from land and sea amounted to thousands of dishes, costing the property of ten ordinary households. Du Hua, the Scribe of the Secretariat, once encountered the princesses' feast after court, as it was set up on the main thoroughfare. He passed through with shouts and reined in his horse, but hundreds of children of the imperial garden rushed forward with sticks, and Du Hua barely escaped with his life.

    On the birthday of Empress Wu Huifei, the emperor and the princesses performed a dance at the base of the Wansui Pavilion. The emperor rode in a palanquin and secretly observed from the overpass as the imperial guards finished eating and threw leftover buns and oranges into a water drain. The emperor was greatly angered and ordered Gao Lishi to beat them to death with a stick. Just as the emperor was furious, no one dared to speak. Prince Ning calmly asked the emperor, "If you secretly observed the minor fault of the imperial guards and had them killed, it might cause the officials to lose their composure and also lose the bigger picture. Your Majesty values frugality and cherishes things, yet you treat human life as less valuable than leftover food. How can you do this?"

    The emperor suddenly realized his mistake and immediately ordered a pardon.

    Afterward, Du Fu drifted and lived in the area around Xiangtan, traveling to Laiyang County in Hengzhou. He was quite annoyed by the county magistrate. Du Fu sent a poem to the magistrate, who then arranged a feast of roasted beef and white wine for him. Du Fu drank too much and died in one night. In his collection, there is still a poem dedicated to Nie of Laiyang.
