    Strange Tales of the Kong Family
    [By Kong Yue of the Jin dynasty]

    King Wen of Chu was fond of hunting, and the finest dogs and eagles from across the land had all gathered there. Someone presented an eagle, saying, "This is no match for the king's eagles." Soon, a creature appeared in the clouds, hovering and gliding with a bright white plumage, but its form was indistinguishable. The eagle then raised its wings and ascended, as swift as a bolt of lightning. In a moment, its feathers fell like snow, and its blood spattered like rain. After a long while, a large bird fell to the ground and died. Measuring its wings, they spanned dozens of miles, and its beak had a yellow hue. None could identify it. A learned gentleman remarked, "This was a young great peng, just beginning to fly, and thus it was defeated by the eagle." King Wen then greatly rewarded the person who had presented the eagle.
    Chuxue Ji, Volume 30

    Lu Chong was a man from Fanyang. His home was thirty li west of his residence, and there was a tomb belonging to Cui Shao Fu, a lesser official, with a daughter's name (possibly a misprint or omission). One day before the Winter Solstice, Lu Chong went hunting west of his home and saw a roe deer. He lifted his bow and shot it, hitting it immediately. The deer fell down and then got up again. Lu Chong pursued it and did not realize how far he had gone. Suddenly, he saw a gate that resembled a government building, with a bell in the middle and a place below where announcements were made. Lu Chong went forward and asked, "What kind of office is this?" The response was, "It is the office of the lesser official." Lu Chong said, "My clothes are shabby, how can I see important people?" Immediately, someone brought new clothes to welcome him. Lu Chong put them on, and they fit perfectly. He then entered to introduce himself to the lesser official. They had several rounds of wine and food. Cui said, "Recently, I received a letter from your esteemed father, seeking marriage for his daughter. That is why I invited you here." He then showed the letter to Lu Chong. Lu Chong's father had died when he was young, but he had seen his father's handwriting. He sighed and could not speak. Cui then ordered the inner chambers to have the lady prepare herself and sent Lu Chong to the eastern corridor. When Lu Chong arrived, the girl had already gotten off the carriage and stood there. They bowed together at the head of the seat. After three days, Lu Chong returned to see Cui. Cui said, "You can return now! The girl has a pregnant appearance. If she gives birth to a boy, he will be returned; if she gives birth to a girl, she will be kept." He ordered the outer chambers to prepare the carriage to send the guest away. Cui saw him off to the door, and they held hands and shed tears. The feeling of separation was no different from that of living people. He also gave him a set of clothes and bedding. Lu Chong then got into the carriage and left as fast as lightning. Soon he arrived home. The family met and were both sad and happy. They asked questions and learned that Cui was a deceased person and that he had entered his tomb. They were greatly distressed.

    After four years, as recorded in the Mengqiu Zhi (蒙求), the date is given as three years; according to the Shi Jian (诗笺), it is three months and three days. Playing by the water, he suddenly saw two ox-drawn carts, rising and sinking alternately. In the Lei Lin (类林), "sinking" is used instead of "drowning," and this is the same. After getting on land, Chong went and opened the rear door of the cart, seeing Cui's daughter and a three-year-old boy riding together. Chong saw them with great joy and wanted to take her hand. The girl pointed to the rear cart and said, "The governor has seen me!" Then, he saw the Lesser Official and went to greet him. The girl handed the child back to Chong and, along with a golden ornament, gave a poem saying:

    "The lustrous quality of the lingzhi (灵芝), how splendid and graceful it is!
    Its brilliance was evident at the time, extraordinary and showing its miraculous nature.
    It contained its essence but did not yet show its beauty; in midsummer, it withered under frost.
    Its glory and brightness will long be hidden, never to be used in this world.
    Not understanding the workings of yin and yang, a wise person suddenly appeared.
    Our meeting was brief, and our separation was swift, all due to the spirits.
    What can I give as a gift to you, a golden ornament for your child?
    Love and affection will be cut off from here, causing pain to the heart and spleen!"

    Chong took the child and the poem, and suddenly, the two carts disappeared. He carried the child back, and the four people present thought it was a ghost or demon. All spat at it from a distance, but its form remained unchanged.

    Zong asked the child, "Who is your father?" The child immediately went into Zong's arms. The crowd was initially surprised and then examined the poem, deeply sighing at the profound connection between life and death. Zong went to the market to sell the loom, setting a high price and not wanting to sell quickly; hoping for someone who would recognize its value. Suddenly, an old maid asked Zong about the origin of the loom and reported back to her mistress. The mistress was the girl's aunt and sent someone to verify it, and indeed it was so. She said to Zong, "My aunt's daughter, the daughter of Cui Shao Fu, died unmarried. The family mourned her deeply and gave a golden loom, placing it in the coffin. Now seeing your loom, it looks very similar. Can you tell me the story of the loom?" Zong replied with the story, and they went to Zong's home to bring the child. The child had the appearance of the Cui family and resembled Zong's features. The aunt said, "My nephew was born in late March, and his father said, 'Spring warmth is gentle.' I hope he will grow strong." The child was named Wenxiu, and Wenxiu was the result of a secret marriage, and the signs were already evident. The child grew up to be a capable person, serving as governor of several commanderies, all achieving notable success. Later, Zong gave birth to Zhi, who became a Han imperial censor. Zhi's son, Yu, became a Wei Grand Works Minister. The family has continued to be prominent until today.

    5

    At the end of the Eastern Han Dynasty, there was a man who suffered from severe abdominal pain, as recorded in the Three-Character Classic and the Comprehensive Mirror to Aid in Government. Another account in the Taiping Guangji describes him as having a heart and abdominal disease that caused him constant pain day and night. On his deathbed, he commanded his son, saying, "After I am gone, you may dissect me." Despite his reluctance, his son followed his father's command and found a copper spear, as described in the Taiping Guangji, which could hold several dou. Later, Hua Tuo heard of his illness and understood it. He went to treat him, taking a medicine from his medicine box and administering it, which caused the spear to instantly transform into clear wine. This is recorded in the Book of Prose (Shu Chao), Taiping Guangji (743, 757), and Comprehensive Mirror to Aid in Government (218).

    Zhong Hui was the maternal cousin of Xun Jibei, and the two had a strained relationship. Xun owned a precious sword worth millions, as recorded in the Shishuo Xinyu Craftsmanship section, which he often kept with his mother, Zhong Furen. Zhong Hui, skilled in calligraphy, imitated Xun's handwriting to write a letter requesting the sword, which he then secretly took without returning it to Xun. Xun knew it was Zhong but had no way to recover it, so he thought of a way to retaliate. Later, Zhong's brothers built a mansion for millions, which was just completed and very exquisite, but they had not yet moved in. Xun, who was extremely skilled in painting, secretly went to paint the door and hall of Zhong's mansion, depicting the image of a Grand Tutor; the clothes and appearance were as if he were alive. When the two Zhongs entered, they were greatly moved, and the mansion then became empty and abandoned. At this point, Zhong was skilled in calligraphy, and the above is supplemented from the Shishuo Xinyu. At the time, everyone considered Xun's retaliation to be far more severe than his initial loss. Both their calligraphy and painting were extremely skillful, as recorded in the Shishuo Xinyu annotation, which cites Kong's Record of Strange Things.

    6

    During the reign of Emperor Ling of Jin, there was a tiger with a white forehead in Yixing, which was referred to in the Chu Xue Ji (Initial Studies Record) as a "white forehead tiger." There was also a green dragon on the long bridge over a stream, both of which were known to devour people. Along with these, there was a man named Zhou, who was also considered one of the three major dangers in the county. Zhou was from the area.

    Gan Bao's father had a favorite concubine, and Gan Bao's mother was extremely jealous. When Gan Bao's father was buried, she pushed the concubine into the tomb. After ten years, when Gan Bao's mother died, the tomb was opened, and the concubine was found lying on the coffin. Upon inspection, she was still warm and gradually regained breath. She was carried back home and woke up after a day. She said, "Gan Bao's father often brought me food and slept with me; our affection was like that of life." The family would tell her about good or bad news, and she would confirm that everything was true. After recovering for several years, she eventually died. Gan Bao then wrote Search for the Supernatural, in which he said that this was due to some kind of inspiration. This story is noted in the Shi Shuo Xin Yu (New Account of the Tales of the World) and the Chu Xue Ji.

    During the reign of Emperor Ming of Jin, a horse presenter dreamed that the river god asked for his horse. When the horse arrived, the emperor had the same dream and immediately threw the horse into the river to offer it to the god. Originally, Grand Tutor Chu also liked this horse. The emperor said, "It has already been given to the river god." After Grand Tutor Chu died, soldiers saw him riding this horse. This story is recorded in the Guang Ji (Extensive Records), volume 276.

    7

    A man from Kuaiji named Sheng Yi once woke up early one morning when there were no people on the road. He saw a person on a willow tree inside his gate, about two feet tall, dressed in red clothing, a hat, and a crown, leaning down to lick the dew on the leaves. After a long while, the figure suddenly saw Sheng Yi, its expression filled with surprise, and quickly disappeared. (Classical Text Compilation 89, Imperial Review 957)

    A local official from Kuaiji named Xie Zong went on leave to Wu and was alone in a boat. Suddenly, a woman with a charming and seductive appearance entered the boat. She asked Xie Zong, "Do you have any fine silk? I want to buy it." Xie Zong engaged in conversation with her, and she gradually became more accommodating. They stayed in the boat overnight, enjoying a feast. When dawn came, she asked Xie Zong to take her along, and he agreed. From then on, the boat's occupants heard only voices and smelled a fragrant scent at night. After a year, they would often stay in the same boat, but when they secretly watched, there was no one there. They realized it was a malevolent spirit and decided to capture it together. After a long time, they found an object as large as a pillow; shortly after, they found two more objects, both small like fists. When they lit a fire to examine them, they turned out to be three turtles. Xie Zong mourned for several days before realizing the truth. He said, "This woman gave birth to two boys in a year, the older one named Dao Min and the younger one named Dao Xing," and after they turned into turtles, he sent them back to the river. (Imperial Review 931)

    In the south, there are people whose heads can fly. Their local custom involves worshiping something called "Chong Luo," and they are known as "Luo Min." (Miscellaneous Records of the South 4, citing Yu Shi's Strange Tales; Note: Yu Shi is likely a misprint for Kong Shi.)
