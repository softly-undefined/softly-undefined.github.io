    High Slope Odd Collection
    Two Volumes (Collected by the Governor of Jiangxi)
    By Yang Yi of the Ming Dynasty.

    Yang Yi, who has previously recorded Chimney Mouth Secrets, wrote this compilation of strange tales. There is a preface where he mentions that "High Slope" is the name of a place in the capital. According to Zhang Yu's Fangxiang Qian Tong Ji from the Ming Dynasty, there is a High Slope Lane in the eastern part of the city, which was likely his residence. Qian Xiyan, in his Biao Yuan, states that Yang Yi, the Minister of Rites, had never believed in strange and supernatural stories. However, after hearing that Wang Weixian had personally seen a man riding a crane, he became deeply convinced and wrote High Slope Odd Collection, which became widely known. Nevertheless, the stories in the book are often fabricated. For example, Huang Ze, a renowned scholar from the late Yuan and early Ming dynasties, was a teacher to Zhao Fang, who was known for his expertise in Confucian classics. Yet, Yang Yi claimed that Liu Ji entered a stone wall, received a book from heaven, and studied with Huang Ze, which can be considered a fabrication. He also described the Weaver Girl crossing the river, where the Star of Literature secretly watched her intimacy. The Weaver Girl accidentally pulled the Star of Literature's robe, and the Heavenly Emperor was displeased, striking the Cowherd on the cheek, causing him to bleed and injure his eyebrows. This story openly dared to insult heaven. Such absurdity and fabrication in novels are rarely seen.

    A Record of Intimate Conversations

    One volume (contributed by the Viceroy of the Two Jiangs)

    The old edition attributes the work to Yang Yi of the Ming dynasty. Yi's courtesy name is Mengyu, and he was from Changshu. He passed the imperial examinations in the year Bingshu of the Jiajing era (1546) and held the position of Deputy Censor of Shandong. This book is a miscellaneous record of Ming dynasty events, containing only about twenty entries, many of which are unreliable or unverified. For example, it claims that the Jianwen Emperor escaped through a tunnel, that the Renzong Emperor was poisoned, and that the Xuangong Emperor went incognito—all of which are baseless rumors from the streets. The anecdotes about the Xiaozong and Wuzong Emperors are especially vulgar and crude. According to the Changshu Gazetteer, Yi wrote Nanguan Collection and Gaopo Strange Compilation, but not this book. It is suspected to be a forgery.

    Gao Po Yi Zuan (高坡异纂) by Yang Yi (杨仪) of the Ming dynasty

    Preface

    When I was young and reading, I secretly detested all accounts of immortals and strange phenomena found in the scrolls, discarding them upon seeing them. Even when I read them, I often could not finish the text. During the reigns of Emperors Zhengde and Jiajing, I witnessed two strange events in the county, and I began to marvel at the ancient accounts, which are not necessarily false. The wonders of the universe’s creation and transformation involve the interplay of existence and non-existence, the cyclical nature of beginnings and endings. Dreams and desires, fleeting and ephemeral, are all manifestations of ultimate truth. Yet there are things that are temporary and cannot endure, or that change but do not remain constant; people simply cannot think deeply and observe carefully, so how can we call all of this falsehood? When I resided in the capital, my friends through literature were nearly everywhere, all of them great sages and gentlemen of the world. The strange and supernatural stories they told were either based on the true accounts passed down by the elders or were their own eyewitness reports, all solidly grounded in evidence. At times, when I had a day off, the sons of the Gao family from Xiangfu, who were prosperous, and Xie Fengyi would come to visit me, often recounting what they had heard to alleviate my troubled heart. Thus, I compiled both new and old accounts, removing the vulgar and indecent, eliminating the trivial and flattering talk that lacks substance, and selected about ten out of every fifteen or sixteen stories, compiling them into three volumes titled Gao Po Yi Zuan. I did this merely to demonstrate the unpredictability of creation and to prove that the accounts of the ancients are not false. Gao Po is the name of a village in the capital, and Yi Zuan refers to the trivial and flattering talk that is insufficient for establishing a meaningful discourse. This was written on the sixth day of the eighth lunar month in the year Renchen of Jiajing.

    8

    Zhang Sanfeng, a native of Yizhou in Liaodong, was the fifth son of Zhang Zhong'an. He possessed divine arts, and his predictions about current events were all verified, leading many people to revere and believe in him. At the end of the Yuan Dynasty, he lived in the Jintai Temple in Baoji. Suddenly, he left behind a poem and passed away. A local man named Yang Yueshan bought a coffin to bury him. When they were about to bury him, they heard sounds coming from inside the coffin. They opened the coffin to see, and he came back to life. He left behind a small drum and a gourd, which he left with his family. He then went to Shaanxi and Sichuan, and climbed Mount Wudang. He was often seen in the areas of Xiangyang and Dangyang. In the 24th year of the Hongwu era, the emperor issued an edict to search for him, but failed to find him. During the Yongle era, the emperor sent Li Ke, the Director of the Bureau of Ceremonies, Ren Yiyu, a Taoist priest, and Yang Yongji, the commander of Minzhou, to visit all the famous mountains in the country to search for him. He also ordered the Taoist priest Sun Bichun to build a palace on Mount Wudang to wait for him, but he never met him. In the third year of the Tian Shun era, he was posthumously bestowed the title of Tongwei Xianhua Zhenren (a Taoist title). At the end of the Tian Shun era, he sometimes appeared and sometimes disappeared. Those who asked him about the great way, he specifically advised them with benevolence and righteousness. All his predictions came true, and when asked, he responded appropriately.
