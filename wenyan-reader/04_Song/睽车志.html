    Record of the Cui Horse [Song Dynasty] by Guo Tan

    Chapter One

    During the reign of Emperor Huizong, there was a man from Chang'an who herded cattle in the wild. He frequently lost his cattle, and upon searching for them, he found them lying in one place, where the grass was soft and lush, different from ordinary grass within a square zhang. Afterward, whenever he searched in that place, he would always find the cattle. One day, during a heavy snowfall, he noticed that the place where the cow was lying was uniquely undisturbed by snow. Finding this strange, he dug it up and found a stone box about two zhang deep, inscribed with the words: "Yellow jade ritual object for earth worship from the Kaiyuan era." Opening it, he found a ritual object shaped like today's, but made of white, beautiful jade, and inside, about an inch in size, it contained new millet. (Told by Grand Tutor Zheng Gong Yi Nian in three stories)

    Near the city of Chang'an, on the official road, there was a large ancient tomb. Because it was located along the path frequently traveled by people, it remained undamaged for a long time. During the early years of the Jianyan era, when the Jurchen invaded, someone opened the tomb and found many ancient bronze bells and tripods. Verifying the inscriptions, they were all from the Three Dynasties. The tomb was a tunnel-like cave, with soil as hard as stone. All around were carvings of people and guards. The men wore headbands, and the women wore segmented headbands and clothing, all with wide sleeves, quite similar to today's style, but slightly different. Thus, it was known that the clothing of thousands of years ago had already been like this.

Upon completing his term and returning home, he had long admired the teachings of immortals and longevity. One day, a hermit visited him, dressed in tattered clothes and covered in sores and scabs. The hermit said to Meng, "Because you love the Dao, I have come to visit you. Recently in Qingzhou, I printed and distributed the Duren Jing (The Scripture of Deliverance). I once received a scroll of it, do you remember?" Meng looked at the document and confirmed that it was indeed the one he had previously distributed. The hermit then said, "I can transform mercury into white gold, and I wish to teach you." Meng replied, "I do not want it." The hermit said, "If you truly do not want it, let me just show you." He took out several hundred pieces of silver from his waist, instructed Meng's attendant to buy mercury, and then placed the mercury in a pot surrounded by burning charcoal. He added a powder from his belt into the pot, and after a short while, he took out the transformed substance, which was indeed white gold. On another day, the hermit returned and said, "I am here to bid you farewell. I just got some fine tea and wish to share it with you." He took out a piece of Jianzhou tea wrapped in tattered cloth, infested with lice and fleas. Meng hesitated and excused himself, saying he lacked tea utensils. The hermit took out paper, wrapped and crushed the tea, and boiled water in a silver pot on the stove. He poured the tea into two cups, bowed, and invited Meng to drink. Meng excused himself, saying the tea was too hot. After a while, the hermit said the tea had cooled and should be left to warm again before drinking. The hermit became angry and said, "Do you really dislike me?" He covered the tea, stood up without bowing, and left. Meng saw him off at the door and returned to see that the place where the tea was covered had turned into gold. The cup and pot, where the tea had been, had also turned into gold inside and out. Meng then realized that the hermit was an extraordinary person and hurriedly searched for him, but he had already disappeared.

    The Elder Master Chao of the Baoren Monastery in Yizhen said: During the Shaoxing era, I once traveled with three fellow monks to Hunan, passing through valleys and mountains, becoming lost and disoriented. As evening fell, we arrived at an ancient abandoned temple, where we decided to take shelter for the night. The walls and buildings were in ruins, and there was no sign of life. One room had its door closed, as if someone were inside. Inside, there was only a simple earthen bed and a ground-level stove with a faint fire covered by ash, and beside it was a clay pot. Upon closer inspection, we saw that it contained yams being cooked. The monks, who were hungry, found the food to be very delicious. Later, we noticed that the window was covered with ancient silk paper from the Chuncheng era, which was a Buddhist ordination certificate. In the room, there were several large jars containing yams, chestnuts, or Chinese yams, but no salt or vinegar. Suddenly, a man appeared carrying a hoe and yams and chestnuts, his hair hanging loose and his body covered in yellow hair, wearing tattered Buddhist robes. He entered and sat on the earthen bed without speaking to us or responding to our words. As night deepened, we all leaned against the walls and sat to sleep. By morning, the man had vanished without a trace. Only four clay pots remained by the stove, one of which was empty, suggesting that the man had eaten from it and left. The other three pots were filled with yams and millet, cooked until soft and hot, as if they were meant to serve as a meal for guests. We ate from them and left, then walked through rocky valleys and dense undergrowth for over twenty miles before finding our way back.

Qionglong Mountain is located to the west of the city, and Weiting is 45 li northeast of the city. Before the imperial examination, it was widely believed that Huang You would top the nation. Indeed, when the names were announced, he did. Huang You, whose courtesy name was Ziyu, was from Pingjiang, and his selection from the national school was a fulfillment of the prophecy of the top scholar's return to the south.
