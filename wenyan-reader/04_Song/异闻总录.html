    During the Da Guan era, in the capital city, a doctor named Geng Yu bought a beautiful and cunning servant girl, and she had been with him for a year. She once stood at the door, and a child passed by, recognizing her as his mother, fondly attached and reluctant to leave, and the servant girl also comforted and pitied him. The child returned home and told his father, "My mother is in a certain house." At the time, his mother had died, and the mourning period had ended, but the father did not believe him. He went to the burial place to check, and it seemed to have been disturbed by thieves, and the body was not there. He then returned home with the child and visited the neighbors of the Geng family, secretly inquiring about the servant girl's name and surname, and found out that she was truly his wife. He then pretended to be a peddler, loitering on the road, waiting for her to come out and see her. His wife called him forward, and they recounted their separation, followed by tears. She told others, "This is my husband, and the smaller one is my son!" Geng Yu heard this and became angry, scolding him, "When I bought you last year, you did not have a husband, and there are contracts and brokers who can verify this, so how dare you!" The husband took the case to the Kaifeng Prefecture, tracing the origin, but the servant girl was confused and could not recall, only saying that she had walked to a bridge, got lost, and was led away by a matchmaker, forced by hunger and starvation, so she sold herself. The matchmaker also said that she really met her at Guangbei Bridge, where she sought to return home for food, and thus sold herself to pay off debts. The magistrate did not have time to investigate the details, ordering the husband to pay the remaining price to Geng Yu and take his wife, but Geng Yu was not satisfied. The husband then took the case to the Imperial Censorate, but the proceedings were not completed, and the woman was lost again, so the lawsuit ended. Within a year, Geng Yu died, and his family also declined.

He asked her why she was there, and she replied, "I am a woman from the vegetable garden. My husband has not yet died, but I am pitifully without a place to return to." The teacher asked Dong what he had, and Dong said he had left a loose-fitting garment behind. The teacher took it and saw that it was dirty and without seams. He ordered it to be thrown into the fire and sent the students to trace the trail. An old gardener said, "Previously, a little boy was herding sheep, and a female sheep fell into the well at the western gate. It could not be retrieved. Now, the white-clad woman has appeared, could she be a ghost?" The teacher called a Taoist priest to recite spells and threw black beans into the well. The ghost no longer came, but Dong also died.

Once, family members went for a walk in the forest and saw a willow tree that was hollow inside and could hold water, serving as a mirror. The daughter-in-law playfully peered into it and immediately fell ill. She returned home and lay in a daze, no longer aware of anything. Then, there was a voice speaking from the air, interacting with people. It could hear human singing and respond in harmony, turning gracefully like a rising melody, with lingering resonance. If there was a slight error in the melody, it would laugh and point out the mistake. It could discuss literature and poetry, generally making sense, yet it was so close yet invisible in form. If servants or concubines did something wrong, it would speak openly to the master, even if something was done hundreds of miles away, it would immediately reveal it, nothing was hidden or concealed. The family accumulated anger and frustration, resorting to violence, prayer, and various other methods, but ultimately achieving nothing. After several years, the Rao family burned incense and prayed, saying, "We have been favored by your divine presence for a long time. Humans and spirits have different paths, and we fear to be disrespectful, which would shame the spirit. We wish to build a new temple in the mountains, with incense, altars, and offerings, and hope that the spirit will move to the new temple, so that both parties can be content. Is this not a good plan?" The spirit agreed, and from then on, there was no further contact. The Rao family was pleased with their plan and built a very grand temple, choosing a day to invite the spirit to the temple. Five days later, it arrived, and its playful remarks were as before. The elder of the Rao family scolded it, saying, "You are now being worshipped in the temple, why do you still torment us?" It laughed and said, "Am I a fool?     A minor official from Wuchang, Li, dreamed of being summoned to the underworld. The master asked him, "When you were the son of the Zhang family in your previous life, how could you push your wife into the water?" Li suddenly remembered the incident in his dream and replied, "My wife fell into the water and died accidentally; I did not push her." The master sought evidence from the local mountain and river deities, which matched what Li said, and he was then released. Some days later, he met a woman at an inn who claimed to be his wife from a previous life. She clung to him, unwilling to let go, and they were intimate as if they were alive. All relatives and friends knew about it, and after several years, she finally left. Li did not marry again for the rest of his life. Although he had no other suffering, he often suffered from lower back pain. He made two wooden clubs, hollowed out the inside, and struck himself dozens of times each day, which alleviated the pain slightly. This was likely caused by the lingering influence of the ghostly atmosphere.
