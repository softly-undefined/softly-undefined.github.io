    The Strange Record (佚名)

    On Mount Lu, there is a valley known as Kangwang Valley, also referred to as North Ridge in the Shi Leifu Zhi. There is a city here called Zhaocun. Whenever it is about to rain, one can hear the sounds of drums, horns, flutes, and pipes from the mountain, which gradually approach the city, followed by heavy rain and dark clouds. The local people consider this a regular sign of impending rain. It is also recorded in the Shi Leifu Zhi that this was the city of King Kang of Zhou, who was fond of unusual things and traveled to famous mountains, not far away. The city often finds ancient artifacts, large tripods, and weapons like bows, crossbows, and gold, indicating that this is not a place inhabited by ordinary people. The mountain is called "Kangwang" and the city "Zhaocun," suggesting that these names have historical significance. (Shi Leifu Zhi 85)

    On Mount Lu, there are three stone bridges, each tens of meters long but less than a foot wide, and when looked down upon, they appear to have no bottom. During the Xiankang period, Governor Yu of Jiangzhou welcomed Wu Meng, and Wu Meng and his disciples visited the mountain and passed over these bridges. They saw an old man sitting under a ginkgo tree, holding a jade cup filled with sweet dew, which he gave to Wu Meng, who then distributed it to his disciples. They also saw a grand terrace with wide halls, jade palaces and golden rooms, dazzling with precious stones and brilliant colors. There were many precious jade and gold vessels, whose names were unknown. They saw several people talking with Wu Meng, as if they were old acquaintances, and they spent the whole day with the jade ointment. (Zhu Lin 31, Shi Leifu Zhi 41, 633)

    Once upon a time, there was a person who went to Mount Lu to gather pine trees. They heard a voice say, "This cannot be taken." The person followed the sound and climbed up, where they saw a strange flower. Its form was extremely lovely, and its fragrance was extraordinary. Knowing it was divine, they picked it up and consumed it, thereby gaining a lifespan of three hundred years. (Zhu Lin, Chapter Thirty-Six)

    In the southern wilderness of Nankang, there was Dongwang Mountain. Three villagers climbed to the top of the mountain and found a clear and deep lake, as well as a fruit grove that spanned about four li in circumference. All kinds of fruits were planted in neat rows, with no mixed trees, as if arranged by human effort. The sweet fruits were just ripe, and the three villagers ate together until they were full. They each carried two fruits to show to others, and then began their return. As they searched for the path they had come, they wandered around for half a day, becoming lost. Suddenly, they heard a voice from the air say, "Quickly release the two fruits, and then you may go." The one carrying the fruits was terrified and threw them on the ground. Instantly, they saw the path back, and together they returned home. (Yulian 906, 490; Chuxueji 28; Leiju 86; Shileifu Zhi 27)

    There is a deity in Nankang named "Shandu." It has a human-like form, about two feet long, black in color, with red eyes, and yellow hair covering its body. It builds its nest in the deep mountains among trees. The nest shape is like a hard bird egg, according to the broad records, it is shaped like an egg and is about three feet high. Inside is very smooth and brightly colored in five hues. Two pieces are layered together, connected in the middle. The locals say, "The upper part is the male chamber, and the lower part is the female chamber." Around the sides, there are openings like circles. Its body is light and hollow, somewhat resembling a wooden tube. In the center, it is lined with bird feathers. This deity can change and hide itself. Rarely, according to the broad records, can one suddenly see its form. It is likely of the same kind as wood, guest, mountain, ○, etc.

    Fifteen miles northwest of Gannan County, there is an ancient pond named Yuguang Pond. On it stands a large paulownia tree, about twenty times the girth of a man's arm. The tree is old and hollow inside, and there is a Shandu nest in it. In the first year of the Yuanjia era of the Song Dynasty, two brothers named Yuan Daotong and Yuan Daoxu, who lived in the county, cut down this tree. They took the nest home. The Shandu appeared to the two brothers and said, "I live in the wilderness, what concern is it of yours? There are many large trees that can be used, how can they be counted? The tree has my nest, so you cut it down. Now I will burn your house to avenge your wickedness." At the second watch of the night, fire broke out simultaneously on the inside and outside of the house. The entire house was completely destroyed. Recorded in the Yuelan (Imperial View) volume 884 and the Guangji (Broad Records) volume 324.

    Yudu County in Nankang extends westward along the river, and the Tang Huiyao (唐会要) records it as "crossing south of the river." Three miles from the county is a place called Mengkou, where there is a cave that looks like a stone chamber, known as the Mengkou Cave. An old tradition, as mentioned in the Leeguji (类聚) volume 90, tells of a divine chicken with a color resembling fine gold. This chicken would emerge from the cave, spread its wings, fly far, and make a long cry. When its sound was heard, it would immediately fly back into the cave. Thus, the stone was named the Golden Chicken Stone. Once, a farmer plowing the side of the mountain saw the chicken come out to play. A tall person then appeared, holding a slingshot and aiming at the chicken. The chicken, seeing the tall person, flew back into the cave. The pebble hit the top of the cave, and the pebble was about six feet in diameter, hanging down and covering the cave, but there was still a small gap that was not large enough for a person to pass through. Another time, a person traveling upstream by boat had not yet reached the cliff several miles away when a person in a yellow robe appeared, carrying two baskets of cucumbers. The person asked to be transported, and the boat owner agreed. The person in the yellow robe then asked for food, and the boat owner gave him a plate of food and wine. After finishing eating, the boat arrived at the base of the cliff. The boat owner then asked for cucumbers, but the person in the yellow robe refused and spat on the plate. The person then climbed up the cliff and entered the stone. Initially, the boat owner was very angry, but when he saw the person enter the stone, he realized the person was supernatural. He then took the plate to look at it and found that the spittle on the plate had turned into gold. This story is also mentioned in the Ren Fang Shuyi Ji (任昉述异记), but it is much simpler and less detailed than this account.

According to the Guangji (Record of Unusual Things), a man named Luo Gensheng lived near this encampment, where he cultivated land and grew melons. He also built a shrine nearby. Luo Gensheng bowed in gratitude and swore, saying: "I suspect that someone from the encampment may be using this fertile land to grow crops and might be pretending that the deity is driving me away. If this is indeed a divine command, I wish to have it written in red ink as a favor." The next morning, when he went to check, the board was still there, with all the black ink replaced by red. Luo Gensheng thanked the deity and left. (From Guangji, Volume 294)

    Lu Ji, when young, was quite fond of hunting. During his time in Wu, a guest presented him with a swift dog named Huang'er. After Lu Ji became an official in Luoyang, he often took the dog with him. This dog was clever and intelligent, able to understand human speech. It had once traveled three hundred li on its own and returned home the next day. When Lu Ji was away in the capital for a long time and had no news from home, he jokingly asked the dog, "There is no letter from home, can you carry a letter to fetch news?" The dog happily wagged its tail and made a sound to respond. Lu Ji then wrote a letter, placed it in a bamboo tube, and tied it to the dog's neck. The dog set off along the post road, running swiftly towards Wu. When hungry, it would enter the grass to eat meat and satisfy itself. Whenever it encountered large bodies of water, it would lower its ears and wag its tail towards the people crossing, who would feel pity and allow it to board their boats. As soon as it neared the shore, the dog would jump onto the boat and continue on its way as fast as the wind. It arrived directly at Lu Ji's home, where it barked to indicate the presence of the letter. Lu Ji's family opened the bamboo tube, took out the letter, and read it. The dog then barked again, as if making a request. The family wrote a reply, placed it in the bamboo tube, and tied it to the dog's neck again. Once the dog had the reply, it ran back to Luoyang. While a human journey would take five months, the dog's round trip took only half a month. After the dog died, it was buried, and its remains were sent back to be buried at the south of Lu Ji's camp, two hundred steps from his home. The people at the camp called the grave "Huang'er's Tomb."
