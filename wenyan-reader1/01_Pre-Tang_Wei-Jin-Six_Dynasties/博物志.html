    　　　　Ministers respectfully examined the Record of Diverse Knowledge in ten volumes, which is attributed to Zhang Hua of the Jin dynasty. According to Wang Jia's Record of Lost and Found, Zhang Hua enjoyed studying the mysterious and esoteric sections of the imperial library, collecting the lost and forgotten from all over the world, from the beginning of written records, examining supernatural phenomena, and incorporating what is said in the streets and alleys. He compiled the Record of Diverse Knowledge in four hundred volumes and presented it to Emperor Wu. The emperor questioned Zhang Hua about his comprehensive knowledge spanning generations, acknowledging his extensive learning but noting that the stories and sayings collected also contained many exaggerations. He suggested that the work could be further edited to remove the frivolous and doubtful parts and divided into ten volumes. Thus, the book was created during the reign of Emperor Wu. However, in the fourth volume, under the section on the nature of things, it mentions that during the Tai Shi period, there was a fire at the armory, indicating that the book must have been written after these events.     Before the Wei dynasty, Emperor Yu the Great governed the four directions and established the land. The "Book of the Yellow River Map" states that the land extends north-south for three hundred million three thousand five hundred and fifty li. Among the prominent geographical features, Mount Kunlun stands out, spanning ten thousand li in width and one hundred thousand li in height. It is the birthplace of divine beings and the gathering place of sages and immortals. From this mountain, five-colored clouds and waters flow, and its southern springs flow into China, known as the "River" (referring to the Yellow River). The mountain is centrally located, with eighty cities surrounding it, and China occupies one part of the southeastern corner of the world.

    The domain of China lies on the left by the sea and on the right by the desert. In terms of square extent, it measures fifteen thousand li, extending east to Penglai and west to Longyou, and spanning the northern and southern regions. During the reigns of Yao and Shun, it measured ten thousand li, and during the Three Dynasties, it measured seven thousand li, varying with the moral quality of the rulers.

    Emperor Yao divided the land into nine provinces, while Emperor Shun divided it into twelve.

    The Qin region is bordered by the Lantian stronghold to the front and the Huyuan barrier to the rear. To the left are the Xiaohan and Hangu passes, and to the right are the Long and Shu regions, with the desert to the west. It is a land of perilous terrain.

    The land of Shu and Han is similar to that of Qin, extending south to Qiongzhuo and north to Baoshan, with the difficult terrain of the Jianmen Pass to the west. It is a land of extreme danger and height, suitable for defense.

    The Zhou region lies in the central area, with the Xiaogou pass to the west, and facing the Jingshan to the east, the Shaoshi to the south, and the Taiyue to the north. It is a land divided by the Sanhe (three rivers) and where thunder and wind originate, a land of four dangers.

    The Wei region lies with the Yellow River to the front and the Zhang River to the back, facing Wangwu and looking toward Liangshan. It possesses the treasures of Lantian and the deep pools of Fucheng.

    The Zhao region lies with the nine provinces to the east and the Hengshan to the west, with the waterfall of Wobu and the perilous terrain of Feihuxing. It extends to the plains of Yingle and Zhuolu.

    The character for "Luoyang" (洛阳) was originally composed of water (水) and fire (火) components.
